Introduction: Learning Life’s Big Lessons

  • The author, Edward Packard, looks back at age ninety.
  • He realized he “went off the rails” often, and luck helped him survive.
  • Many mistakes could have been avoided if he’d grasped a few principles sooner.
  • This presentation shares those 9 things, hoping you learn them earlier!

Start by making it relatable: everyone makes mistakes, but learning from others can save you trouble. Packard offers a lifetime of wisdom distilled into actionable advice.

1) Be Self-Constituted: Build Your Inner Compass

  • What it means: Being “consistent, unified, and whole” – having integrity.
  • It’s about setting your own “virtuous moral framework” to guide your actions.
  • Think of good values like kindness, honesty, and fairness.
  • Without it, you’re “ruled by a heap of impulses”—a bit of a mess.

How your moral framework works:

Note

Think of a raft on a river:
You’re poling, trying to steer the best course. Your “self-constitution” is your strong paddle and clear direction.

Real-world connection: Imagine facing peer pressure to cheat. If you’re self-constituted with a moral framework of honesty, you’ll naturally decline because it aligns with your core principles, not just fear of getting caught. Kant’s “universal law” suggests acting only on principles you’d want everyone to follow.

2) Keep Awake and Aware: Don’t Sleepwalk Through Life

  • Sleepwalking: Doing things without thinking about their purpose or consequences.
  • It affects judgment, even in smart people or leaders making big decisions (like leaders before WWI).
  • Avoiding “mental lethargy” means confronting uncomfortable facts instead of ignoring them.

Being Awake & Aware

  • Fully present in the moment.
  • Understand why you’re doing something.
  • Consider effects on self and others.
  • Makes you “a buddha” (Thich Nhat Hanh: understanding, compassionate, loving).

Sleepwalking

  • Autopilot mode.
  • Ignoring unpleasant facts.
  • Can lead to catastrophic outcomes.
  • Failure to weigh risks.

Warning

Habitual sleepwalking can lead to acting (or not acting) in ways that cause obvious future problems if you were truly awake.

Contemporary example: Mindlessly scrolling social media for hours without a purpose is a form of sleepwalking. Being awake means setting an intention, like “I will spend 15 minutes checking news, then do something productive.” Think about how easily we can just react instead of choose.

3) Consider What Others May Be Thinking and Feeling

  • Empathy: Sensing how others feel.
  • Theory of Mind: Guessing how they are thinking.
  • For a long time, the author only thought about his own best interests.
  • He learned that considering others helps avoid awkward moments and builds good relationships.

3) Consider What Others May Be Thinking and Feeling

Interaction Flow

Tip

Before speaking or acting, pause for a few seconds. Ask yourself: 1. How would I feel if someone said/did this to me? 2. What might be going on in their mind right now? This small pause can prevent big regrets.

Classic example: The author talks about making a “witty” remark about someone’s boat that he now realizes was crude and offensive. He thought it would impress, but he didn’t consider how it would land for the other person.

4) Make Happiness Your Default State of Mind

  • Can happiness be simple? The Dalai Lama suggests yes!
  • It’s less about complex meditation and more about how you interact with the world.
  • Key ingredients: Love for others, respect for their rights/dignity, compassion, and self-restraint.

\[ \text{Happiness} = (\text{Love Given} + \text{Compassion}) \times \text{Responsibility} \]

  • Even more important: The warmth and affection we give is more crucial than what we receive.
  • To love is more important than to be loved.

Note

This perspective suggests happiness isn’t something you get, but something you practice by giving of yourself positively.

Practical application: Instead of waiting for good things to happen to make you happy, actively seek opportunities to be kind, helpful, or understanding. Helping a classmate, volunteering, or simply listening to a friend can activate this “giving” happiness.

5) Seek an Eternal Perspective: Look Beyond Yourself

  • What it is: Expanding your view beyond your ego to include others, nature, and the entire cosmos.
  • Philosopher Spinoza found joy and calmness (equanimity) in understanding the natural order.
  • This perspective is like “compassion without attachment” – you’re active, but not controlled by fear or desire for specific outcomes.

Benefits of an Eternal Perspective

  • Reduces anger, envy, pride.
  • Emotional disengagement from specific outcomes can bring inner peace.
  • You can stay “alive, in action” without being swept away by desires or fears.
  • See the beauty in the journey, not just the destination (like the snow leopard trek).

Potential Challenges (and replies from text)

  • “Will life be colorless?” “Won’t it drain excitement?”
    • Reply: Not necessarily. The expedition itself (the looking at nature, the walking) was wonderful, even without seeing the snow leopard. The monk’s “Isn’t that wonderful!” when they found nothing showed release from attachment.
  • “Is it at odds with self-interest?”
    • Reply: Spinoza would say it’s a necessary condition for a truly fulfilling life, bringing equanimity and joy.

Modern thinking: Think about climate change or conservation. An “eternal perspective” acknowledges our tiny place in a vast, ongoing system and encourages actions that benefit future generations or the planet, not just immediate personal gain.

6) Guard Against Self-Deception: Be Honest With Yourself

  • Self-deception: When your beliefs and decisions are skewed by biased emotions, wishful thinking, or subconscious justifications.
  • Confirmation bias: A common example where you favor information that supports what you already believe, ignoring anything that contradicts it.
  • Even brilliant people fall prey to it, using their intellect to justify flawed conclusions.

Warning

“Certitude is not the test of certainty.” – Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr. Feeling sure about something doesn’t mean you’re right.

  • Francis Bacon: The mind “pulls everything else into agreement with and support” for its favored views.
  • Daniel Kahneman: Strong beliefs are often sustained by a “community of likeminded believers.”

7) How to Confront Mortality: Facing Life’s End

  • Stoics (ancient Greeks/Romans): Contemplate death often to not be shocked when it comes.
  • Spinoza’s approach (author’s preference): Find peace and self-control by gaining an “eternal perspective” through knowledge.
  • Rejects supernatural claims; focuses on the natural world.

Famous Views on Mortality

  • George Eliot: “I try to delight in the sunshine that will be when I shall never see it any more.” Focus on what endures beyond your individual life.
  • Bertrand Russell: Overcome fear of death by making your “interests gradually wider and more impersonal,” merging with “universal life.”

Fearlessness & Acceptance

  • Katharine Hepburn: “I look forward to oblivion.” A brave acceptance of the end.
  • Michel de Montaigne: “I want death to find me planting my cabbages, neither worrying about it, nor about the unfinished gardening.” Live fully, without preoccupation.

Important

Confronting mortality isn’t about being gloomy; it’s about shifting your focus from the “small bundle of facts that make our own personality” to something larger, which can bring calmness and richer participation in life.

Personal reflection: How can thinking about your own limited time make you value what you do each day more? This isn’t about dread, but about thoughtful living and prioritizing.

8) What an Outsized Role is Played by Luck

  • Luck isn’t just winning the lottery: It includes your genetic make-up, family, where you grew up, and random opportunities.
  • Actor Wallace Shawn noted that being born to “well-heeled, sophisticated, highly intelligent” parents is a form of luck.
  • Even basic needs like peace and food are forms of luck for many.
  • Key takeaway: If you’ve achieved a lot, luck played a significant part in the opportunities you had.

  • Implications of understanding luck:

    • If lucky: Cultivate humility and generosity.
    • If unlucky: Cultivate compassion for yourself and “irrepressible resolve.”

Discussion point: This isn’t to say hard work isn’t important, but it rebalances the perspective. A student who excels in school likely had the luck of a stable home environment, good teachers, and innate ability. Recognizing this can foster less judgment of others and more desire to create opportunities.

9) To Consider What You Have at the Moment

  • Be dynamic and take initiative, but also mindful.
  • Sometimes, it’s vital to pause and appreciate what’s right in front of you.
  • We often don’t truly value things until they are lost.

\[\text{Value Lost} > \text{Value Possessed}\]

This isn’t a strict formula, but a common human tendency.

  • Shakespeare (Much Ado About Nothing):

    That what we have we prize not to the worth Whiles we enjoy it, but being lacked and lost, Why, then we rack the value, then we find The virtue that possession would not show us While it was ours.

Tip

Take a moment each day to consciously acknowledge and appreciate something you have right now – a friendship, a quiet moment, a skill you possess.

Contemporary example: Think about how easily we take our smartphones for granted. It’s a powerful tool, but if it’s lost, its “virtue” (its utility and convenience) becomes incredibly apparent. Appreciating things proactively can deepen life’s richness.

Quick Recap: Nine Lessons for a Better Life

1. Build a strong moral character.

2. Stay awake and aware.

3. Think about others’ feelings.

4. Choose happiness as your default.

5. See the bigger picture (eternal perspective).

6. Watch out for self-deception.

7. Understand and face mortality.

8. Recognize the role of luck.

9. Appreciate what you have now.

Applying These Lessons to Your Life

  • These aren’t just ideas; they’re practices.
  • Pick one lesson that resonated most with you today.
  • Try to apply it in a small way this week.
  • Even small changes, consistently done, can make a big difference over time.

Encourage students to choose one point and try to implement it directly. Ask them to reflect on a specific situation where this lesson could be applied in their daily lives (e.g., before sending a text, giving a compliment, making a decision about their future).
