6.1 DSP Systems as Difference Equations

Key Idea

A (linear, timeŌĆæinvariant, causal) DSP system (digital filter) with input \(x(n)\) and output \(y(n)\) is described by an \(N\)thŌĆæorder linear difference equation:

\[ \begin{aligned} y(n) &= b_{0}x(n) + b_{1}x(n-1) + \cdots + b_{M}x(n-M) \\ &\quad - a_{1}y(n-1) - \cdots - a_{N}y(n-N) \end{aligned} \]

Or compactly:

\[ y(n)=\sum_{i=0}^{M} b_i x(n-i) - \sum_{j=1}^{N} a_j y(n-j) \]

  • \(\{b_i\}\): feedŌĆæforward (input) coefficients.
  • \(\{a_j\}\): feedback (output) coefficients.
  • \(M\), \(N\): input/output orders.

Note

Digital filtering = recursively computing \(y(n)\) from the difference equation, given input \(x(n)\) and initial conditions.

Highlight that this is the discreteŌĆætime analog of an LTI differential equation in analog circuits.

Explain: - ŌĆ£LinearŌĆØ comes from sums of scaled \(x(\cdot)\) and \(y(\cdot)\). - ŌĆ£TimeŌĆæinvariantŌĆØ since the coefficients donŌĆÖt depend on \(n\). - ŌĆ£CausalŌĆØ because only present and past samples appear.

Digital filtering is exactly what a microcontroller/DSP chip does for each input sample.

Example 6.1 ŌĆō Manual Digital Filtering (Nonzero ICs)

System:

\[ y(n) = 0.5\,y(n-2) + x(n-1) \]

Identify coefficients:

  • \(N=2\), \(M=1\)
  • \(a_1=0,\ a_2=-0.5\)
  • \(b_0=0,\ b_1=1\)

Case (a) Nonzero initial conditions:

  • \(y(-2)=1,\ y(-1)=0,\ x(-1)=-1\)
  • Input \(x(n) = (0.5)^n u(n)\)

Compute for \(n=0,1,2,3\):

\[ \begin{aligned} x(0) &= (0.5)^0 u(0) = 1 \\ y(0) &= 0.5\,y(-2) + x(-1) = 0.5\cdot1 + (-1) = -0.5 \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x(1) &= 0.5 \\ y(1) &= 0.5\,y(-1) + x(0) = 0.5\cdot0 + 1 = 1.0 \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x(2) &= 0.25 \\ y(2) &= 0.5\,y(0) + x(1) = 0.5\cdot(-0.5) + 0.5 = 0.25 \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x(3) &= 0.125 \\ y(3) &= 0.5\,y(1) + x(2) = 0.5\cdot1 + 0.25 = 0.75 \end{aligned} \]

Work through one or two steps on the board, then let students compute the others in pairs.

Key point: initial conditions directly affect early outputs. After some time, the system ŌĆ£forgetsŌĆØ them if it is stable.

Example 6.1 ŌĆō Effect of Zero Initial Conditions

Case (b) Zero initial conditions:

  • \(y(-2)=0,\ y(-1)=0,\ x(-1)=0\)
  • Same input: \(x(n) = (0.5)^n u(n)\)

For \(n=0,1,2,3\):

\[ \begin{aligned} x(0) &= 1 \\ y(0) &= 0.5\,y(-2) + x(-1) = 0\cdot1 + 0 = 0 \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x(1) &= 0.5 \\ y(1) &= 0.5\,y(-1) + x(0) = 0\cdot0 + 1 = 1 \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x(2) &= 0.25 \\ y(2) &= 0.5\,y(0) + x(1) = 0.5\cdot0 + 0.5 = 0.5 \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} x(3) &= 0.125 \\ y(3) &= 0.5\,y(1) + x(2) = 0.5\cdot1 + 0.25 = 0.75 \end{aligned} \]

Notice:

  • Outputs differ at \(n=0,1,2\).
  • By \(n=3\), both cases give \(y(3)=0.75\).

Tip

For a stable filter, the effect of initial conditions decays over time.

Connect this to practical ECE applications:

  • When turning on a digital audio filter, the first few samples might be ŌĆ£transientŌĆØ due to initial conditions.
  • In simulations, you often ignore an initial transient segment.

Example 6.2 ŌĆō MATLAB Simulation of a DSP System

System:

\[ y(n) = 2x(n) - 4x(n-1) - 0.5y(n-1) - y(n-2) \]

Initial conditions:

  • \(y(-2)=1,\ y(-1)=0,\ x(-1)=-1\)
  • Input \(x(n) = (0.8)^n u(n)\)

Goal: compute \(y(n)\) for \(n=0,\dots,19\) using MATLAB.

We preŌĆæallocate vectors for \(y(n)\) and embed initial conditions as extra entries:

Ask students: based on the plot, does this system seem stable? Why?

Using MATLAB filter() and filtic()

General DSP form:

  • Coefficient vectors:

    \[ A = [1\ a_1\ a_2\ \dots\ a_N] \] \[ B = [b_0\ b_1\ b_2\ \dots\ b_M] \]

  • Difference equation:

    \[ y(n)=\sum_{i=0}^{M}b_i x(n-i)-\sum_{j=1}^{N} a_j y(n-j) \]

MATLAB syntax with initial conditions:

Zi = filtic(B, A, Yi, Xi);   % compute internal states (direct-form II)
y  = filter(B, A, x, Zi);    % filter operation
  • \(X_i = [x(-1)\ x(-2)\ \dots]\)
  • \(Y_i = [y(-1)\ y(-2)\ \dots]\)
  • \(Z_i = [w(-1)\ w(-2)\ \dots]\): internal delay states

Zero initial conditions:

y = filter(B, A, x);         % Zi defaults to zeros

Note

filtic() maps your physical initial conditions \((x(-k), y(-k))\) into the internal state vector required by the filter() implementation (directŌĆæform II).

Clarify that the difference equation coefficients \(a_i, b_i\) are what you design, and filter() is just one standard way MATLAB ŌĆ£realizesŌĆØ the filter.

Mention that directŌĆæform II realization details will appear later in the chapter, but here we just use the MATLAB black box.

6.2 From Difference Equation to Transfer Function \(H(z)\)

Given the general LTI difference equation with zero initial conditions:

\[ \begin{aligned} y(n) &= b_0 x(n) + b_1 x(n-1) + \cdots + b_M x(n-M) \\ &\quad - a_1 y(n-1) - \cdots - a_N y(n-N) \end{aligned} \]

Take the \(z\)-transform of both sides, using

  • \(Z\{x(n-k)\} = X(z) z^{-k}\)
  • \(Z\{y(n-k)\} = Y(z) z^{-k}\)

We obtain:

\[ \begin{aligned} Y(z) &= b_0 X(z) + b_1 X(z) z^{-1} + \cdots + b_M X(z) z^{-M} \\ &\quad - a_1 Y(z) z^{-1} - \cdots - a_N Y(z) z^{-N} \end{aligned} \]

Rearrange:

\[ H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + b_M z^{-M}} {1 + a_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + a_N z^{-N}} = \frac{B(z)}{A(z)} \]

with

\[ B(z) = b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + b_M z^{-M} \]

\[ A(z) = 1 + a_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + a_N z^{-N} \]

Important

Transfer function definition

\[ H(z) = \frac{\text{$z$-transform of output}}{\text{$z$-transform of input}} = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} \]

This is the central bridge:

  • Difference equation Ōåö’ĖÄ transfer function.
  • Time domain Ōåö’ĖÄ \(z\)-domain.

Once we have \(H(z)\), we can: - Analyze stability via poles. - Analyze frequency response via \(H(e^{j\Omega})\).

Example 6.3 ŌĆō Difference Equation ŌåÆ \(H(z)\)

Given:

\[ y(n) = x(n) - x(n-2) - 1.3\,y(n-1) - 0.36\,y(n-2) \]

  1. Take \(z\)-transform (zero ICs):

\[ Y(z) = X(z) - X(z)z^{-2} - 1.3 Y(z)z^{-1} - 0.36 Y(z)z^{-2} \]

  1. Group \(Y(z)\) and \(X(z)\):

\[ Y(z)\big(1 + 1.3z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2}\big) = (1 - z^{-2})X(z) \]

  1. Transfer function:

\[ H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{1 - z^{-2}}{1 + 1.3z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2}} \]

So:

  • \(A(z) = 1 + 1.3z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2}\)
  • \(B(z) = 1 - z^{-2}\)

Point out pattern:

  • Terms involving \(y(n-k)\) go to denominator \(A(z)\).
  • Terms involving \(x(n-k)\) go to numerator \(B(z)\).

Ask: Is this an IIR or FIR system? ŌåÆ Because of feedback terms \(y(n-1), y(n-2)\), itŌĆÖs an IIR.

Example 6.4 ŌĆō FIR System

Given:

\[ y(n) = x(n) - 0.5x(n-1) + 0.36x(n-2) \]

Take \(z\)-transform:

\[ Y(z) = X(z) - 0.5 X(z)z^{-1} + 0.36 X(z)z^{-2} \]

So:

\[ H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = 1 - 0.5z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2} \]

  • \(A(z) = 1\)
  • \(B(z) = 1 - 0.5z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2}\)

Tip

No feedback terms (\(a_j = 0\)) ŌćÆ the impulse response has finite length ŌćÆ FIR filter.

Contrast with Example 6.3.

Mention that FIR filters are always BIBO stable (poles at \(z=0\) only), important in many ECE applications (e.g., precise phase control, linear phase designs).

Example 6.5 ŌĆō From \(H(z)\) Back to Difference Equation

(a)

Given:

\[ H(z) = \frac{z^{2} - 1}{z^{2} + 1.3z + 0.36} \]

  1. Divide numerator/denominator by \(z^2\):

\[ H(z) = \frac{(z^{2}-1)/z^{2}}{(z^{2} + 1.3z + 0.36)/z^{2}} = \frac{1 - z^{-2}}{1 + 1.3z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2}} \]

  1. Write \(Y(z)/X(z)\):

\[ \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{1 - z^{-2}}{1 + 1.3z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2}} \]

  1. CrossŌĆæmultiply:

\[ Y(z)(1 + 1.3z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2}) = X(z)(1 - z^{-2}) \]

  1. Inverse \(z\)-transform (using shift property):

\[ y(n) + 1.3 y(n-1) + 0.36 y(n-2) = x(n) - x(n-2) \]

Rearranged:

\[ y(n) = x(n) - x(n-2) - 1.3 y(n-1) - 0.36 y(n-2) \]

Exactly the system from Example 6.3.

(b)

Given:

\[ H(z) = \frac{z^{2} - 0.5z + 0.36}{z^{2}} \]

Divide by \(z^{2}\):

\[ H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = 1 - 0.5z^{-1} + 0.36z^{-2} \]

Thus:

\[ Y(z) = X(z) - 0.5 z^{-1}X(z) + 0.36 z^{-2}X(z) \]

Inverse \(z\)-transform:

\[ y(n) = x(n) - 0.5 x(n-1) + 0.36 x(n-2) \]

Same as Example 6.4.

This pair of examples reinforces the twoŌĆæway mapping between time and \(z\)-domain descriptions.

Practical note: in filter design tools (MATLAB, Python), you often specify \(H(z)\) (coeff vectors A,B) and then the implementation executes the equivalent difference equation sample by sample.

Factorized PoleŌĆōZero Form of \(H(z)\)

Any rational \(H(z)\) can be factored as:

\[ H(z) = \frac{b_0 (z - z_1)(z - z_2)\cdots(z - z_M)} {(z - p_1)(z - p_2)\cdots(z - p_N)} \]

  • \(\{z_i\}\): zeros (roots of numerator).
  • \(\{p_i\}\): poles (roots of denominator).

Example 6.6

Given:

\[ H(z) = \frac{1 - z^{-2}}{1 + 1.3z^{-1} + 0.36 z^{-2}} \]

Multiply numerator & denominator by \(z^{2}\):

\[ H(z) = \frac{z^{2} - 1}{z^{2} + 1.3z + 0.36} \]

Solve roots:

  • \(z^{2} - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow z = 1,\ -1\) (zeros).
  • \(z^{2} + 1.3z + 0.36 = 0 \Rightarrow z = -0.4,\ -0.9\) (poles).

PoleŌĆōzero form:

\[ H(z) = \frac{(z - 1)(z + 1)}{(z + 0.4)(z + 0.9)} \]

Explain that plotting these poles and zeros in the \(z\)-plane gives powerful geometric intuition about stability and frequency response.

Zeros ŌĆ£push downŌĆØ magnitude near their angles; poles ŌĆ£pull upŌĆØ magnitude.

6.2.1 Impulse, Step, and System Responses

Impulse response \(h(n)\)

  • Response to input \(x(n) = \delta(n)\).

  • With \(X(z) = Z\{\delta(n)\} = 1\):

    \[ h(n) = Z^{-1}\{H(z) X(z)\} = Z^{-1}\{H(z)\} \]

Step response (to \(x(n)=u(n)\), zero ICs):

  • \(X(z) = Z\{u(n)\} = \dfrac{z}{z-1}\).

    \[ y(n) = Z^{-1}\Big\{ H(z) \frac{z}{z-1} \Big\} \]

General system response to arbitrary \(x(n)\):

  • In \(z\)-domain:

    \[ Y(z) = H(z) X(z) \]

  • In time domain:

    \[ y(n) = Z^{-1}\{Y(z)\} \]

Note

Equivalently, in time domain, for LTI systems:

\[ y(n) = (h * x)(n) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} h(k)\,x(n-k) \]

(convolution).

Relate this to circuit theory:

  • Impulse response Ōåö’ĖÄ unit impulse response of an LTI system.
  • Step response Ōåö’ĖÄ standard test used for characterizing systems (e.g., step response of amplifiers or control systems).

Here we exploit the convenience of \(z\)-transform algebra instead of doing convolution by hand.

Example 6.7 ŌĆō Responses of a FirstŌĆæOrder System

System transfer function:

\[ H(z) = \frac{z+1}{z-0.5} \]

We will find:

  1. Impulse response \(h(n)\).
  2. Step response to \(x(n)=u(n)\).
  3. Response to \(x(n) = (0.25)^n u(n)\).

6.7 (a) Impulse Response

We rewrite:

\[ \frac{H(z)}{z} = \frac{z+1}{z(z-0.5)} = \frac{A}{z} + \frac{B}{z-0.5} \]

Find residues:

  • At \(z=0\):

    \[ A = \left. \frac{z+1}{z-0.5} \right|_{z=0} = \frac{0+1}{0-0.5} = -2 \]

  • At \(z=0.5\):

    \[ B = \left. \frac{z+1}{z} \right|_{z=0.5} = \frac{1.5}{0.5} = 3 \]

Thus:

\[ \frac{H(z)}{z} = -\frac{2}{z} + \frac{3}{z-0.5} \]

Multiply both sides by \(z\):

\[ H(z) = -2 + \frac{3z}{z-0.5} \]

Inverse \(z\)-transform:

  • \(-2 \leftrightarrow -2\,\delta(n)\).
  • \(\dfrac{z}{z-0.5} \leftrightarrow (0.5)^n u(n)\).

So:

\[ h(n) = -2\,\delta(n) + 3 (0.5)^n u(n) \]

6.7 (b) Step Response

Step input: \(x(n) = u(n)\), \(X(z) = \dfrac{z}{z-1}\).

Thus:

\[ Y(z) = H(z) X(z) = \frac{z+1}{z-0.5}\cdot\frac{z}{z-1} \]

Write:

\[ \frac{Y(z)}{z} = \frac{z+1}{(z-0.5)(z-1)} = \frac{A}{z-0.5} + \frac{B}{z-1} \]

Residues:

  • At \(z=0.5\):

    \[ A = \left. \frac{z+1}{z-1} \right|_{z=0.5} = \frac{1.5}{-0.5} = -3 \]

  • At \(z=1\):

    \[ B = \left. \frac{z+1}{z-0.5} \right|_{z=1} = \frac{2}{0.5} = 4 \]

So:

\[ Y(z) = \frac{-3z}{z-0.5} + \frac{4z}{z-1} \]

Inverse \(z\)-transform:

  • \(\dfrac{z}{z-0.5} \leftrightarrow (0.5)^n u(n)\).
  • \(\dfrac{z}{z-1} \leftrightarrow u(n)\).

Hence step response:

\[ y(n) = -3 (0.5)^n u(n) + 4 u(n) \]

6.7 (c) Response to \(x(n) = (0.25)^n u(n)\)

Input:

\[ X(z) = Z\{(0.25)^n u(n)\} = \frac{z}{z-0.25} \]

Then:

\[ Y(z) = H(z) X(z) = \frac{z+1}{z-0.5}\cdot\frac{z}{z-0.25} = \frac{z(z+1)}{(z-0.5)(z-0.25)} \]

Partial fraction:

\[ \frac{Y(z)}{z} = \frac{z+1}{(z-0.5)(z-0.25)} = \frac{A}{z-0.5} + \frac{B}{z-0.25} \]

Solving yields:

\[ Y(z) = \frac{6z}{z-0.5} - \frac{5z}{z-0.25} \]

Inverse \(z\)-transform:

\[ y(n) = 6(0.5)^n u(n) - 5(0.25)^n u(n) \]

  • Top: impulse response \(h(n)\).
  • Middle: step response.
  • Bottom: response to \((0.25)^n u(n)\).

Use this example to reinforce:

  • Partial fractions in the \(z\)-domain lead directly to standard sequences.
  • Poles at \(0.5\) and \(0.25\) cause exponential decays in time.

Ask: Is the system stable? (Yes, |0.5|<1).

6.3 The \(z\)-Plane PoleŌĆōZero Plot and Stability

z-plane basics

  • Horizontal axis: \(\Re\{z\}\).
  • Vertical axis: \(\Im\{z\}\).
  • The unit circle \(|z|=1\) divides the plane.
  • Poles: marked with ŌĆ£├ŚŌĆØ.
  • Zeros: marked with ŌĆ£ŌŚŗŌĆØ.

We plot poles and zeros of \(H(z)\) to analyze:

  • Stability (pole locations).
  • Frequency response shape.

Emphasize that many DSP design tools visualise poleŌĆōzero plots because they give immediate insight:

  • Poles near the unit circle ŌåÆ sharp resonances.
  • Zeros on the unit circle ŌåÆ sharp notches.

Especially important in control, audio equalization, and communication filters.

Example 6.8 ŌĆō PoleŌĆōZero Plot

Given:

\[ H(z) = \frac{z^{-1}-0.5z^{-2}}{1 + 1.2z^{-1} + 0.45z^{-2}} \]

Convert to advanced form (positive powers) by multiplying numerator and denominator by \(z^{2}\):

\[ H(z) = \frac{(z^{-1}-0.5z^{-2})z^{2}}{(1+1.2z^{-1}+0.45z^{-2})z^{2}} = \frac{z - 0.5}{z^{2} + 1.2z + 0.45} \]

Find zeros and poles:

  • Zero: solve \(z - 0.5 = 0\) ŌćÆ \(z_1 = 0.5\).

  • Poles: solve \(z^{2} + 1.2z + 0.45 = 0\):

    \[ p_1 = -0.6 + j0.3,\quad p_2 = -0.6 - j0.3 \]

Magnitude of poles:

\[ |p_{1,2}| = \sqrt{(-0.6)^2 + (0.3)^2} \approx 0.671 < 1 \]

So poles are inside the unit circle.

PoleŌĆōzero form:

\[ H(z) = \frac{z-0.5}{(z + 0.6 - j0.3)(z + 0.6 + j0.3)} \]

Ask students to comment:

  • Are the poles stable? (Yes, inside unit circle).
  • How do the complex conjugate poles suggest oscillatory decaying behavior in the impulse response?

Connect to secondŌĆæorder resonant behavior in analog circuits (underdamped response).

Mapping Between \(s\)-Plane and \(z\)-Plane

ContinuousŌĆætime sampled signal (sampling period \(T\)):

\[ x_s(t) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x(nT)\,\delta(t-nT) \]

Laplace transform: using \(L\{\delta(t-nT)\}=e^{-nTs}\):

\[ X_s(s) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x(nT)e^{-nTs} \]

OneŌĆæsided \(z\)-transform of sequence \(x(n)\):

\[ X(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x(n) z^{-n} \]

Comparing terms, we see the mapping:

\[ z = e^{sT} \]

Let \(s = -\alpha \pm j\omega\):

\[ z = e^{-\alpha T \pm j\omega T} = e^{-\alpha T} \angle \pm\omega T \]

Conclusions:

  • \(\alpha > 0\) ŌćÆ left half \(s\)-plane (stable continuous system) maps to \(|z| = e^{-\alpha T} < 1\) = inside the unit circle.
  • \(\alpha = 0\) ŌćÆ \(s = j\omega\) axis maps to \(|z|=1\) = unit circle.
  • \(\alpha < 0\) ŌćÆ unstable continuous poles map outside unit circle.

Stress the powerful analogy:

  • Stable analog LTI systems have poles in LHHP.
  • Stable digital LTI systems have poles inside the unit circle.

This is heavily used in transforming analog filter designs into digital ones (bilinear transform, impulse invariance, etc.).

Stability Rules for DSP Systems

A digital system is BIBO stable if every bounded input produces a bounded output.

For a rational \(H(z)\), stability is determined by pole locations:

  1. Stable:
    • All poles of \(H(z)\) are inside the unit circle.
  2. Unstable:
    • At least one pole is outside the unit circle.
  3. Marginally stable:
    • Outermost pole(s) lie on the unit circle and are firstŌĆæorder.
  4. Unstable (special case):
    • Outermost pole(s) on the unit circle are multipleŌĆæorder (e.g., double pole).
  5. Zeros do not affect stability (only poles matter).

Illustrations in Fig. 6.9:

Tie back to impulse response:

  • Stable: \(h(n)\) decays to zero.
  • Unstable: \(h(n)\) grows without bound.
  • Marginally stable: \(h(n)\) oscillates but does not decay (constant amplitude sinusoid).

6.4 Digital Filter Frequency Response

From analog to digital frequency response:

  • Analog LTI system: \(H(s)\).
  • SteadyŌĆæstate frequency response: \(H(j\omega) = H(s)\big|_{s=j\omega}\).

For digital systems:

  • Use mapping \(z = e^{sT}\).
  • On \(j\omega\) axis ŌåÆ \(z = e^{j\omega T}\).

Thus digital frequency response:

\[ H(e^{j\omega T}) = H(z)\big|_{z=e^{j\omega T}} = \big|H(e^{j\omega T})\big| \angle H(e^{j\omega T}) \]

Define normalized digital radian frequency:

\[ \Omega = \omega T \]

Then we write:

\[ H(e^{j\Omega}) = H(z)\big|_{z=e^{j\Omega}} = \big|H(e^{j\Omega})\big| \angle H(e^{j\Omega}) \]

Note

In DSP, we typically work with \(\Omega \in [0,\pi]\) radians, corresponding to physical frequencies \(f \in [0, f_s/2]\).

Emphasize that \(H(e^{j\Omega})\) is obtained by evaluating the transfer function on the unit circle in the \(z\)-plane.

This is the key link between poleŌĆōzero plots and frequency response.

Transient vs SteadyŌĆæState Response to a Sinusoid

Consider a digital filter with transfer function \(H(z)\) and sinusoidal input:

  • Input amplitude: \(K\).
  • Frequency: \(\Omega\).

We can show (by partial fractions and inverse \(z\)-transform, as in the text) that the output has form:

\[ y(n) = y_{tr}(n) + y_{ss}(n) \]

where

  • \(y_{tr}(n)\): transient response (decays with \(n\) for a stable system).
  • \(y_{ss}(n)\): steadyŌĆæstate sinusoid at same frequency.

General steadyŌĆæstate result:

For input \(x(n) = K\sin(\Omega n)\,u(n)\),

\[ y_{ss}(n) = K\big|H(e^{j\Omega})\big| \sin\big(n\Omega + \angle H(e^{j\Omega})\big)u(n) \]

So:

  • Magnitude response: \(|H(e^{j\Omega})|\) = ratio of output amplitude to input amplitude.
  • Phase response: \(\angle H(e^{j\Omega})\) = phase shift between output and input.

This is the discreteŌĆætime analog of sinusoidal steady state in AC circuit analysis.

Once the transient dies out, the filter simply scales and phaseŌĆæshifts the sinusoid at each frequency.

Use this to motivate why we care so much about \(|H(e^{j\Omega})|\) and \(\angle H(e^{j\Omega})\).

Properties of \(H(e^{j\Omega})\)

1. Periodicity

From EulerŌĆÖs identity:

\[ e^{j(\Omega + k2\pi)} = e^{j\Omega} \]

for integer \(k\). Thus:

  • \(H(e^{j\Omega}) = H(e^{j(\Omega + k2\pi)})\).
  • \(|H(e^{j\Omega})| = |H(e^{j(\Omega + k2\pi)})|\).
  • \(\angle H(e^{j\Omega}) = \angle H(e^{j(\Omega + k2\pi)})\).

2. Symmetry (for realŌĆæcoefficient filters)

  • Magnitude: \(|H(e^{-j\Omega})| = |H(e^{j\Omega})|\).
  • Phase: \(\angle H(e^{-j\Omega}) = -\angle H(e^{j\Omega})\).

Because of sampling:

  • Maximum useful frequency is the folding frequency \(f_s/2\).

  • Corresponding normalized radian frequency:

    \[ \Omega = \omega T = 2\pi \frac{f_s}{2} T = \pi \]

Thus we only need \(\Omega \in [0,\pi]\).

Conversion to Hz:

\[ f = \frac{\Omega}{2\pi} f_s \]

Magnitude in decibels:

\[ |H(e^{j\Omega})|_{\text{dB}} = 20\log_{10}\big(|H(e^{j\Omega})|\big) \]

Explain that in most practical plots (MATLAB freqz), you see \(0\) to \(\pi\) on the horizontal axis.

Relate decibels to power/range constraints in communication, audio, and RF design.

FIR vs IIR Systems

Starting from the general difference equation:

\[ y(n) = \sum_{i=0}^{M} b_i x(n-i) - \sum_{j=1}^{N} a_j y(n-j) \]

  • If all \(a_j = 0\), we get:

    \[ y(n) = \sum_{i=0}^{M} b_i x(n-i) \]

    • The impulse response \(h(n)\) is just \(\{b_0, b_1, \dots, b_M\}\), and zero outside that range.
    • FiniteŌĆælength \(h(n)\) ŌćÆ Finite Impulse Response (FIR).
    • \(H(z) = B(z)\) with denominator \(A(z) = 1\).

  • If any \(a_j \neq 0\):

    • Impulse response \(h(n)\) generally has infinite duration.
    • \(H(z) = \dfrac{B(z)}{A(z)}\), \(A(z) \neq 1\).
    • This is an Infinite Impulse Response (IIR) filter.

Tip

Summary

  • FIR: no feedback, always BIBO stable, often used for linearŌĆæphase designs.
  • IIR: uses feedback, can achieve sharp responses with fewer coefficients, but stability must be checked (poles inside unit circle).

Connect to realŌĆæworld ECE choices:

  • Audio EQ on phones often uses IIR (biquad sections) for efficiency.
  • HighŌĆæprecision measurement filters or multirate filters often use FIR to guarantee linear phase and stability.

Summary / Key Points

  1. Difference equations are the timeŌĆædomain description of DSP systems (digital filters), involving past and present inputs and outputs.
  2. Digital filtering = recursively computing \(y(n)\) from the difference equation and initial conditions.
  3. The transfer function \(H(z) = \dfrac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \dfrac{B(z)}{A(z)}\) fully characterizes an LTI discreteŌĆætime system in the \(z\)-domain.
  4. You can convert between difference equations and \(H(z)\) (via \(z\)-transform and its inverse).
  5. Impulse response \(h(n)\) is \(Z^{-1}\{H(z)\}\).
    • Step and general responses follow from \(Y(z) = H(z)X(z)\) and inverse \(z\)-transform.
  1. Poles and zeros plotted in the \(z\)-plane determine stability and shape the frequency response.
    • Stable: all poles inside unit circle.
    • Marginally stable: outer poles on unit circle, first order.
    • Unstable: any pole outside unit circle or multiple poles on unit circle.
  2. The digital frequency response is \(H(e^{j\Omega}) = H(z)\big|_{z=e^{j\Omega}}\):
    • \(|H(e^{j\Omega})|\) gives gain vs frequency.
    • \(\angle H(e^{j\Omega})\) gives phase shift vs frequency.
  3. FIR filters (no feedback) have finite impulse responses and are always stable; IIR filters (with feedback) have infinite impulse responses and require poleŌĆælocation stability checks.

ReŌĆæemphasize practical implications:

  • When designing a filter, weŌĆÖll specify desired amplitude/phase responses, then choose FIR/IIR structures and compute coefficients.
  • Everything in this lecture is the mathematical backbone of that process.

Formulas Summary

General LTI DSP system:

\[ y(n) = \sum_{i=0}^{M} b_i x(n-i) - \sum_{j=1}^{N} a_j y(n-j) \]

Transfer function:

\[ H(z) = \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + b_M z^{-M}} {1 + a_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + a_N z^{-N}} = \frac{B(z)}{A(z)} \]

PoleŌĆōzero factorization:

\[ H(z) = \frac{b_0 (z - z_1)\cdots(z - z_M)} {(z - p_1)\cdots(z - p_N)} \]

Impulse response:

\[ h(n) = Z^{-1}\{H(z)\} \]

General response:

\[ Y(z) = H(z)X(z),\quad y(n) = Z^{-1}\{Y(z)\} \]

Mapping between domains:

\[ z = e^{sT},\quad s = -\alpha \pm j\omega \]

Stable continuous pole (\(\alpha>0\)) ŌćÆ \(|z| = e^{-\alpha T} < 1\)

Digital frequency response:

\[ H(e^{j\Omega}) = H(z)\Big|_{z=e^{j\Omega}},\quad \Omega = \omega T \]

Magnitude (dB) and phase:

\[ |H(e^{j\Omega})|_{\text{dB}} = 20\log_{10}\big(|H(e^{j\Omega})|\big) \]

\[ \angle H(e^{j\Omega}) = \arg\big(H(e^{j\Omega})\big) \]

\[ y_{ss}(n) = K|H(e^{j\Omega})|\sin\big(n\Omega + \angle H(e^{j\Omega})\big)u(n) \]

FIR vs IIR:

  • FIR: \(a_j = 0\) ŌćÆ \(H(z) = B(z)\), finiteŌĆælength \(h(n)\).
  • IIR: at least one \(a_j \neq 0\) ŌćÆ \(H(z) = \dfrac{B(z)}{A(z)}\), infiniteŌĆælength \(h(n)\).
