Units and Standards

Instructional Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Define accuracy, hysteresis, resolution, and sensitivity.
  • List SI units for length, time, mass, and electric current.
  • Convert between SI, English, and standard analog signal ranges.
  • Recognize common P&ID symbols and line types used in industry.

Standardization is the bedrock of engineering. Without a common set of units and symbols, we couldn’t communicate complex plant designs. Today we define the “rules of the game” for measurement and documentation.

The International System (SI)

As ECE students, we prioritize SI units, but you must remain fluent in English units for industrial legacy systems.

Quantity Unit Symbol
Length Meter m
Mass Kilogram kg
Time Second s
Electric current Ampere A
Temperature Kelvin K

Derived Units

  • Force: Newton (N) \(= 1 \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m/s^2}\)
  • Energy: Joule (J) \(= 1 \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m^2/s^2}\)

Note that the Kelvin is the base unit for temperature, but Celsius is common in the field. Also, we often deal with very small currents (mA) or very large pressures, making metric prefixes (milli, micro, kilo, mega) essential.

Standard Analog Signals

Information is transmitted across plants using two primary standards:

1. Electric Current

Range: 4 to 20 mA

  • Why? Immune to line resistance (noise).
  • Live Zero: 4mA indicates 0% signal. 0mA indicates a broken wire (fault).

2. Pneumatic Pressure

Range: 3 to 15 psi

  • Why? Safe in explosive environments (no sparks).
  • SI Equivalent: 20 to 100 kPa.

The “Live Zero” (4mA) is a brilliant engineering feature. If a wire snaps, the current drops to 0mA, which the controller recognizes immediately as a “Fault” rather than a “Zero measurement.”

The formula \(I = mT + I_0\) is a standard linear transformation. For ECEs, this is just \(y = mx + b\). You’ll do this calculation thousands of times in your career to calibrate sensors.

Performance Definitions

  • Transfer Function: The relationship between input \(x(t)\) and output \(y(t)\).
  • Accuracy: The maximum error (usually \(\pm\) % of Full Scale or Span).
  • Sensitivity: The ratio of output change to input change (e.g., \(5\mathrm{mV} / {}^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\)).
  • Resolution: The smallest measurable change in the input variable.

System Accuracy

When multiple blocks are in series, uncertainties add up!

Worst Case: \(\frac{\Delta V}{V} = \pm \frac{\Delta K}{K} \pm \frac{\Delta G}{G}\)

Realistic (RMS): \(\sqrt{(\frac{\Delta K}{K})^2 + (\frac{\Delta G}{G})^2}\)

Accuracy can be sneaky. % of Full Scale is very different from % of Reading. If you have a 100V meter with 1% FS accuracy, your error is ±1V whether you are measuring 100V or 2V. At 2V, that’s a 50% relative error!

Hysteresis: The Memory Effect

Hysteresis is a predictable error where the output depends on the history of the input (whether it is increasing or decreasing).

Hysteresis is common in mechanical systems (like gear backlash) or magnetic materials. If you always approach your setpoint from the same direction, you can negate this error.

P&ID: The Engineer’s Blueprint

P&ID (Piping and Instrumentation Diagrams) use symbols defined by ANSI/ISA S5.1.

Line Types

  • Heavy Solid: Main product flow.
  • Dashed (—): Electric signal (4-20mA).
  • Hatched (///): Pneumatic signal (3-15 psi).
  • Bubbled (-o-): Digital data link.

Imagine walking into a massive refinery. You can’t see through the steel pipes. The P&ID is your map; it tells you exactly where the sensors are and how the data flows.

P&ID Instrument Bubbles

Instruments are drawn as “bubbles” with letter codes:

First Letter (Variable) Succeeding (Function)
T (Temperature) C (Controller)
P (Pressure) T (Transmitter)
L (Level) V (Valve)
F (Flow) I (Indicator)

Example: TIC 101 = Temperature Indicating Controller, Loop 101.

If the bubble has a line through it, it’s located in the central control room. If it’s empty, it’s out “in the field” (on the factory floor).

Case Study: Analyzing a Loop

Consider a reaction vessel with a temperature loop:

  1. TT 301 measures and transmits.
  2. TC 301 decides the action.
  3. PY 301 converts the electronic signal to air pressure.
  4. TV 301 (Final Control Element) physically changes the process.

Notice the “PY” designation. It stands for Pressure (P) and a relay/conversion function (Y). It’s the bridge between the electronic world of the controller and the pneumatic world of the valve actuator.

Summary and Key Takeaways

  • Units: SI is standard, but English units (PSI, lb) persist in industry.
  • Signals: 4-20mA is the electronic standard; 3-15 psi is the pneumatic standard.
  • Error: Accuracy is often defined by Span or Full Scale.
  • Hysteresis: approach direction matters.
  • P&ID: Use the ANSI/ISA shorthand to map your systems.

Problem Set 1.3

Problem 1: Unit Conversion (Pressure)

The Challenge:

An industrial boiler’s safety valve is rated for a maximum pressure of 250 psi. To calibrate the electronic pressure transmitter (which uses SI units), you must convert this value to Pascals (Pa) and MegaPascals (MPa).

Given:

  • \(1 \text{ psi} = 6,894.76 \text{ Pa}\)

Recall

\(1 \text{ MPa} = 10^6 \text{ Pa}\).

Converting PSI to Pa/MPa is a daily task in global engineering firms where US-made hardware meets European-standard software.

Solution 1: Pressure Conversion

Step 1: Convert to Pa

\[P = 250 \text{ psi} \times 6,894.76 \frac{\text{Pa}}{\text{psi}} = 1,723,690 \text{ Pa}\]

Step 2: Convert to MPa

\[P = \frac{1,723,690 \text{ Pa}}{10^6} = 1.72 \text{ MPa}\]

Result: The safety limit is 1.72 MPa.

Encourage students to use scientific notation for very large numbers to avoid transcription errors.

Problem 2: Signal Mapping (4-20 mA)

The Challenge:

A level sensor measures a tank height from 0.0 to 5.0 meters. This measurement is converted into a 4 to 20 mA current signal.

  1. Find the linear equation \(I = f(h)\) relating current (\(I\)) to height (\(h\)).
  2. Calculate the current produced when the tank height is 3.2 meters.

Mapping a physical range to an electrical range is the core of “Signal Conditioning.” This is a linear \(y = mx + b\) problem.

Interactive Solution 2: Mapping Tool

Use the calculator below to verify the mapping logic.

The formula used: \(I = \frac{16}{h_{max}} \times h + 4\)

Manual Check:

\(I = (3.2 \times 3.2) + 4 = 10.24 + 4 = \mathbf{14.24 \text{ mA}}\)

Show students how the “Live Zero” (the +4) ensures that even at 0 meters, we have current flowing.

Problem 3: Understanding Accuracy

The Challenge:

A pressure gauge has a range of 50 to 150 psi. A reading of 85 psi is observed. Calculate the uncertainty (error) in psi if the accuracy is specified as:

  1. \(\pm 1\%\) of Full Scale (FS).
  2. \(\pm 1\%\) of Span.
  3. \(\pm 1\%\) of Reading.

This problem highlights why “Accuracy” is a tricky term. The same 1% means different things depending on the reference.

Solution 3: Accuracy Comparison

  • a) Full Scale (FS): Highest value is 150 psi. \(\text{Error} = \pm 0.01 \times 150 = \mathbf{\pm 1.5 \text{ psi}}\)

  • b) Span: (Max - Min) = \(150 - 50 = 100 \text{ psi}\). \(\text{Error} = \pm 0.01 \times 100 = \mathbf{\pm 1.0 \text{ psi}}\)

  • c) Reading: Current measurement is 85 psi. \(\text{Error} = \pm 0.01 \times 85 = \mathbf{\pm 0.85 \text{ psi}}\)

Important

Note that Span accuracy is tighter than Full Scale accuracy in this specific case.

Ask students: “Which specification would a salesperson prefer to use to make their device look better?” Usually, they use % of Reading if they can, as it sounds more precise.

Problem 4: System Accuracy (RMS)

The Challenge:

A measurement loop consists of two blocks in series:

  1. Transducer: Accuracy \(\pm 2.0\%\)
  2. Signal Conditioner: Accuracy \(\pm 0.8\%\)

Calculate the Worst-Case system accuracy and the RMS (statistically likely) system accuracy.

Errors compound. If the sensor is off and the conditioner is also off in the same direction, we get the worst case. If they are random, they might partially cancel out (RMS).

Interactive Solution 4: Error Accumulator

Adjust the uncertainties to see the impact on total system error.

viewof u1 = Inputs.range([0, 5], {value: 2.0, step: 0.1, label: "Block 1 Accuracy (%)"});
viewof u2 = Inputs.range([0, 5], {value: 0.8, step: 0.1, label: "Block 2 Accuracy (%)"});

Notice how the RMS value is always lower than the Worst-Case. In industrial design, we often use RMS because it is statistically rare for every single component to be at its maximum error limit simultaneously in the same direction.

Problem 5: Resolution and Linearity

The Challenge:

A digital temperature sensor uses an 8-bit ADC to cover a range of \(0^{\circ}\) to \(100^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\).

  1. What is the Resolution of this sensor in degrees Celsius?
  2. If the sensor has a Linearity of 0.5% FS, what is the maximum deviation in degrees?

Resolution is about the “stairs” (discrete steps). Linearity is about how far the “stairs” deviate from a straight line.

Solution 5: Resolution and Linearity

1. Resolution:

An 8-bit ADC has \(2^8 = 256\) states.

\[\text{Resolution} = \frac{\text{Range}}{\text{States}} = \frac{100^{\circ}\mathrm{C}}{256} \approx \mathbf{0.39^{\circ}\mathrm{C} \text{ per bit}}\]

2. Linearity:

\(0.5\%\) of Full Scale (\(100^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\)): \[\text{Deviation} = 0.005 \times 100 = \mathbf{\pm 0.5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}}\]

Note

In this system, the linearity error (\(0.5^{\circ}\)) is actually larger than the resolution step (\(0.39^{\circ}\))!

This is a common realization: just because you have high resolution (lots of bits) doesn’t mean you have high accuracy (linearity).

Summary of Key Formulae

Concept Formula
Signal Mapping \(y = \left(\frac{y_{max} - y_{min}}{x_{max} - x_{min}}\right)(x - x_{min}) + y_{min}\)
Span \(\text{Span} = \text{Range}_{max} - \text{Range}_{min}\)
Worst-Case Accuracy \(A_{total} = A_1 + A_2 + ... + A_n\)
RMS Accuracy \(A_{rms} = \sqrt{A_1^2 + A_2^2 + ... + A_n^2}\)
Digital Resolution \(\text{Res} = \frac{\text{Full Scale Range}}{2^n}\)

Encourage students to keep these formulas in their reference sheets for the upcoming lab and exams.
