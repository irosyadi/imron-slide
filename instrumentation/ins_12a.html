Analog vs. Digital Processing

Instructional Objectives

  • Define the roles of ADC and DAC in a control loop.
  • Explain Supervisory Control vs. Direct Digital Control (DDC).
  • Describe the concept of Smart Sensors and Process-Control Networks (Fieldbus).
  • Identify the function of Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs).

We are moving from the general block diagram to the specific “brains” of the system. In modern ECE, the shift from analog circuits to digital microprocessors has changed how we think about precision and networking.

Data Representation: Two Worlds

Analog Data

  • Continuous: Smooth variation.
  • Resolution: Theoretically infinite.
  • Example: A voltage that follows temperature exactly.
  • Issue: Prone to noise and nonlinearities.

Digital Data

  • Discrete: Represented by bits (0s and 1s).
  • Resolution: Limited by the number of bits.
  • Example: A 4-bit number representing 0–15V.
  • Advantage: Robust, programmable, and easy to network.

Analog is how the world works; Digital is how computers think. The transition between these two involves a trade-off: we gain programmability but lose the perfectly “smooth” nature of the signal.

The Conversion Bridge

To bridge the gap between the physical process (Analog) and the computer (Digital), we use converters.

  • ADC (Analog-to-Digital): Converts sensor voltage to binary numbers.
  • DAC (Digital-to-Analog): Converts computer commands back to a control voltage.

Hexadecimal Representation

Binary is hard for humans. We use Hexadecimal (Hex) as a compact shorthand:

1010 (Binary) = Ah (Hex) = 10 (Decimal).

Table 1 in your text shows the 4-bit mapping. Remember: \(2^n\) gives the number of states. An 8-bit ADC has 256 states, a 12-bit has 4096. Higher bits = Higher precision.

ON/OFF Control (Discrete)

The simplest digital control: The final element is either ON (1) or OFF (0).

  • Behavior: The variable oscillates around the setpoint.
  • Deadband: A range where no action occurs to prevent “chattering” (constant rapid switching).
  • Hysteresis: Different behavior depending on whether the value is rising or falling.

Tip

Common examples: Your home AC unit, water heater, or a simple thermostat.

ON/OFF is technically a 1-bit digital system. It’s inexpensive but results in constant cycling of the controlled variable.

Analog Control Systems

In a true analog loop, every signal is continuous.

  • No software involved.
  • Controlled via Operational Amplifiers (Op-Amps) and components.
  • Faster than many digital systems, but difficult to “reprogram” without changing hardware.

Before the 1970s, almost all industrial control was analog. If you wanted to change the control logic, you had to get a soldering iron out.

Supervisory Control

When computers were unreliable, they were used for Supervision, not direct action.

  • The Analog Loop handles the second-by-second control.
  • The Computer monitors the loop and updates the Setpoint.
  • Benefit: If the computer crashes, the analog loop keeps running at the last known safe setpoint.

Think of the computer as the “Manager” and the analog loop as the “Worker.” The manager gives orders (setpoints), but the worker does the actual heavy lifting.

Direct Digital Control (DDC)

In DDC, the computer is the controller.

  • Software performs error detection and control logic.
  • Flexibility: Change the control algorithm by simply updating code.
  • Miniaturization: Leads to “Smart Sensors” where all these blocks fit in one tiny housing.

This is the modern standard. Everything from a car engine to a nuclear reactor now uses some form of DDC.

Networked Control & Fieldbus

Modern plants connect hundreds of DDC loops via a Local Area Network (LAN) or Fieldbus.

  • Standards: Foundation Fieldbus (US) and Profibus (Europe).
  • Advantage: “Plug and Play” interoperability between different manufacturers.

Fieldbus allows for distributed control. Instead of thousands of wires running back to a central room, we have one digital cable that “talks” to every device.

Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs)

PLCs are specialized computers for industrial environments.

  • Origin: Replaced “Relay Logic” (complex wiring).
  • Capability: Primarily discrete (ON/OFF) but modern PLCs handle complex DDC.
  • Inputs: Thermal limit switches, push buttons, proximity sensors.
  • Outputs: Motor starters, solenoid valves, indicator lights.

If you go into industrial automation, the PLC will be your primary tool. They are designed to be rugged, surviving heat, dust, and electrical noise that would kill a standard office PC.

Summary and Review

  1. Analog is continuous; Digital is discrete/binary.
  2. ADC and DAC are essential interface components for DDC.
  3. Supervisory Control sets the goals; DDC takes the actions.
  4. Fieldbus is the “Ethernet” of the factory floor, allowing devices to talk.
  5. PLCs are the rugged workhorses of discrete-state industrial control.

Problem Set 1.2

Problem 1: ADC Resolution

A temperature sensor outputs a voltage from 0 to 15 V. You are using a 4-bit ADC to represent this data (as shown in Table 1 of the text).

  1. What is the voltage resolution (the smallest change in voltage that can be detected)?
  2. If the sensor outputs 6.4 V, what is the 4-bit binary representation?
  3. If the sensor outputs 6.8 V, what is the 4-bit binary representation?

Ask the students to recall the relationship between the number of bits and the number of discrete states. A 4-bit system has \(2^4 = 16\) states.

Solution 1: Resolution & Quantization

1. Voltage Resolution:

The range is 0 to 15 V. With 4 bits, there are \(2^4 = 16\) states (0 to 15). \[\text{Resolution} = \frac{V_{max}}{2^n - 1} = \frac{15\text{ V}}{15} = 1\text{ V/step}\]

2. & 3. Representation for 6.4 V and 6.8 V:

Since the system can only change at 1.0 V increments:

  • 6.4 V is truncated/rounded to the nearest lower integer (in this specific encoding): 6V \(\rightarrow\) 0110₂ (6h).
  • 6.8 V is still represented as 6V \(\rightarrow\) 0110₂ (6h) because it hasn’t reached the 7.0 V threshold.

Quantization Error

The difference between the actual 6.8 V and the represented 6.0 V is the quantization error.

Problem 2: Control Architecture Identification

Match the following scenarios to the correct control type:

A. Supervisory Control
B. Direct Digital Control (DDC)
C. ON/OFF Control

  1. A computer reads a temperature and sends a signal directly to a DAC to adjust a heater’s variable voltage.
  2. An analog loop regulates a valve, but a central computer sends a new setpoint every hour to optimize efficiency.
  3. A relay snaps shut when a tank is empty and opens when it is full.

Encourage students to look for where the “decision” is made and whether the control is continuous or discrete.

Solution 2: Identifying Systems

  1. B. Direct Digital Control (DDC): The computer performs the controller functions (error detection and logic) and talks directly to the final element via a DAC.
  2. A. Supervisory Control: The “heavy lifting” is done by analog loops. The computer only “supervises” by adjusting setpoints.
  3. C. ON/OFF Control: This is a discrete-state system where the controller output is binary (Open/Closed).

Students often confuse the placement of ADC and DAC. Remember: ADC follows the sensor; DAC precedes the actuator.

Solution 3: DDC Block Diagram

  • ADC: Converts the physical world into bits for the computer.
  • Computer: Calculates the error and determines the required action in software.
  • DAC: Converts the software’s decision back into a physical voltage/current.

Final Challenge: Design Logic

You are designing a Fieldbus network for a factory. You have a “Smart Sensor” and a “Programmable Logic Controller (PLC)”.

Question: Why is a Fieldbus better than running individual analog wires (4-20mA) from every sensor to the control room?

Think about cost, noise, and the “Plug and Play” concept mentioned in the text.

Solution: The Network Advantage

  1. Reduced Wiring: A single serial cable (the bus) replaces hundreds of individual wire pairs.
  2. Interoperability: Standards like Profibus or Fieldbus allow equipment from different vendors to work together (“Plug and Play”).
  3. Data Integrity: Digital signals on a bus are less susceptible to the voltage drops and noise that affect analog 4-20mA lines over long distances.
  4. Diagnostics: Smart sensors can send calibration data and error codes over the network, which a simple analog signal cannot do.

This wraps up our practice. These concepts of digital conversion and networking form the backbone of modern Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT).
