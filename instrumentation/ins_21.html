Instructional Objectives

The purpose of this presentation is to introduce analog signal-conditioning methods used in process-control systems.

  • Define the principles of analog signal conditioning.
  • Analyze the impact of loading on measurement accuracy.
  • Evaluate passive circuits (dividers, bridges, and filters).
  • Design simple RC filters for noise reduction.

Welcome to the lecture on Analog Signal Conditioning.
In process control, sensors rarely provide a signal that can be plugged directly into a controller or computer.
Today, we will focus on how we transform those raw sensor signals using passive components like resistors and capacitors to make them usable.

What is Signal Conditioning?

Signal conditioning refers to operations performed on signals to convert them into a form suitable for interfacing with other elements in a process-control loop.

  • Conditioned Output: Remains an analog representation of the variable.
  • Interface Requirement: Crucial before Analog-to-Digital Conversion (ADC).
  • Transfer Function: The mathematical relationship between input and output.
    • Example: A voltage amplifier has a transfer function \(V_{out} = G \cdot V_{in}\).

Even in a digital world, the “front end” is almost always analog.
We use the term ‘Transfer Function’ to describe how the circuit alters the signal.
If we have a sensor outputting 0.2V and we need 5V, our conditioning circuit must have a specific gain and bias.

Principles of Analog Signal Conditioning

Common Operations

1. Level & Bias Changes

Adjusting the magnitude (gain) and zero-value (offset).

  • Amplification: Gain \(> 1\)
  • Attenuation: Gain \(< 1\)

2. Linearization

Converting nonlinear sensor outputs (like CdS cells) into linear representations via circuits or software.

3. Conversions

  • Resistance to Voltage: Bridges.
  • Voltage to Current: 4-20 mA standards for transmission.

4. Filtering & Matching

  • Filtering: Removing 60 Hz line noise.
  • Impedance Matching: Preventing signal loss.

These four pillars define the engineer’s toolkit.
Level and bias adjustments are like the ‘zero’ and ‘span’ on an instrument.
Conversions are vital because while many sensors change resistance, most controllers expect voltage or current.

The Concept of Loading

Loading occurs when connecting a measurement circuit to a sensor causes the sensor’s output voltage to drop.

Thévenin’s Theorem Model:

A sensor can be modeled as a voltage source (\(V_x\)) in series with an internal resistance (\(R_x\)).

\[V_y = V_x \left(1 - \frac{R_x}{R_L + R_x}\right)\]

To minimize loading errors, we require \(R_L \gg R_x\).

This is a critical concept for ECE students.
If you use a multimeter with low input impedance to measure a high-impedance source, your reading will be wrong.
Equation 1 shows that if the load resistance RL is not significantly larger than the source resistance Rx, the measured voltage Vy will be lower than the actual source voltage Vx.

Passive Circuits

Voltage Divider Circuits

The simplest way to convert resistance change to voltage change.

\[V_D = \frac{R_2 V_s}{R_1 + R_2}\]

Challenges:

  1. Nonlinear: \(V_D\) vs \(R_2\) is not a straight line.
  2. Output Impedance: \(Z_{out} = R_1 \parallel R_2\).
  3. Self-Heating: Current through \(R_2\) may change its temperature.

The voltage divider is common but has flaws.
The relationship between R2 and VD is hyperbolic, not linear.
Also, the current required to generate a voltage drop might heat up the sensor, leading to measurement errors.

Wheatstone Bridge

Used for high-precision measurement of small resistance changes.

Null Condition (\(V_a = V_b\)): \[R_3 R_2 = R_1 R_4\]

The Wheatstone bridge is the gold standard for resistive sensors like strain gauges. Its greatest advantage is that it provides a differential output that is zero when the bridge is balanced. This allows us to amplify only the change in signal.

Bridge Offset Voltage

When the bridge is not balanced, the offset voltage \(\Delta V\) (assuming infinite detector impedance) is:

\[\Delta V = V \left[ \frac{R_3}{R_1 + R_3} - \frac{R_4}{R_2 + R_4} \right]\]

  • For small changes (\(\Delta R\)), the output is approximately linear.
  • For large changes, the output becomes significantly nonlinear.

Note the formula. If all resistors are equal, Delta V is zero. As R4 changes slightly, Delta V changes.
Engineers must decide if the sensor’s range stays within the ‘approximately linear’ region or if software linearization is needed.

Lead Compensation

In industrial settings, sensors are often far from the bridge circuit. Long wires add resistance that changes with temperature.

Solution: Three-wire connection

  • Wire 1: In series with \(R_3\).
  • Wire 2: In series with \(R_4\) (sensor).
  • Wire 3: Power lead (carries no bridge current).

Since Wire 1 and Wire 2 are identical and experience the same environment, their resistance changes cancel out in the null equation.

This is a classic engineering solution to a “real-world” problem.
By placing one lead in each arm of the bridge, the error becomes a “common-mode” signal that the bridge naturally rejects.

RC Filters

Low-Pass and High-Pass Basics

Used to eliminate unwanted noise (like 60 Hz hum or high-frequency transients).

Critical Frequency (\(f_c\)):

The frequency where output power drops by half (\(-3\text{ dB}\)) or voltage drops to \(0.707 V_{in}\).

\[f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC}\]

  • Low-Pass: Passes \(f < f_c\), blocks \(f > f_c\).
  • High-Pass: Passes \(f > f_c\), blocks \(f < f_c\).

Filters are essential for cleaning up signals.
A low-pass filter is like a sponge that soaks up fast jitters while letting the slow, steady signal through.
We choose R and C values to set the “cutoff” point where this soaking starts.

If we need a sharper drop, we would cascade multiple filters.

Advanced Passive Filtering

Cascaded Filters

  • Used to increase the “sharpness” of the cutoff.
  • Loading Warning: The second stage loads the first stage.
  • Resulting gain is the product of individual gains (if isolated).

Band-Pass Filters

  • Combination of a High-Pass and a Low-Pass in series.
  • Defined by a lower cutoff \(f_L\) and higher cutoff \(f_H\).

Notch Filters (Twin-T)

  • Specifically designed to reject a very narrow range of frequencies (e.g., exactly 60 Hz).

A Twin-T notch filter is particularly useful in medical electronics like ECGs, where the 60Hz power line noise is often much larger than the heart signal itself.
However, passive notch filters are sensitive to component tolerances, often requiring precision resistors.

Summary & Key Takeaways

  1. Signal Conditioning is the bridge between the raw sensor and the data acquisition system.
  2. Loading is the enemy of accuracy; always ensure \(R_{in} \gg R_{source}\).
  3. Bridges convert resistance to voltage with high precision and provide a differential “zero” reference.
  4. RC Filters are simple but effective tools for noise management, defined by \(f_c = 1/(2\pi RC)\).

To conclude: always look at the impedances before you connect two circuits.
In our next session, we will look at how Operational Amplifiers (Op-Amps) can make these conditioning tasks much easier by providing high input impedance and active filtering.

Problem 1: Loading Effects

Scenario:

A temperature sensor has an output resistance of \(5.0 \, \text{k}\Omega\) and a transfer function of \(20 \, \text{mV/}^\circ\text{C}\).

It is connected to an amplifier with an input resistance of \(10 \, \text{k}\Omega\) and a gain of 10.

Task:

Calculate the actual amplifier output voltage when the temperature is \(50^\circ\text{C}\).

Students often calculate the “ideal” value first (1.0V). The key is to realize the sensor acts as a Thévenin source, and the amplifier’s input resistance acts as a load. This demonstrates that \(R_{in}\) must be much larger than \(R_{source}\) to maintain accuracy.

Solution 1: Loading Effects

Step 1: Ideal Sensor Output

\(V_T = (20 \, \text{mV/}^\circ\text{C}) \times 50^\circ\text{C} = 1.0 \, \text{V}\)

Step 2: Loaded Input (\(V_{in}\))

Using the loading formula:

\(V_{in} = V_T \left( \frac{R_L}{R_L + R_x} \right)\)

\(V_{in} = 1.0 \left( \frac{10 \, \text{k}\Omega}{10 \, \text{k}\Omega + 5 \, \text{k}\Omega} \right) = 0.67 \, \text{V}\)

Step 3: Amplifier Output

\(V_{out} = \text{Gain} \times V_{in}\)

\(V_{out} = 10 \times 0.67 \, \text{V} = 6.7 \, \text{V}\)

Conclusion:

Without considering loading, we would have expected \(10 \, \text{V}\).

The error is a staggering 33%.

Problem 2: Voltage Divider Design

Scenario:

A supply voltage \(V_s = 5.0 \, \text{V}\) is connected to a divider where \(R_1 = 10.0 \, \text{k}\Omega\).

\(R_2\) is a sensor that varies from \(4.0 \, \text{k}\Omega\) to \(12.0 \, \text{k}\Omega\).

Task:

  1. Find the range of the output voltage \(V_D\).
  2. Determine the maximum power dissipated by the sensor.

Dividers are the simplest conversion circuits. Note how the output voltage doesn’t change linearly with R2. Power dissipation is critical to avoid “self-heating” errors in thermal sensors.

Solution 2: Voltage Divider

1. Output Voltage Range:

  • At \(R_2 = 4 \, \text{k}\Omega\): \(V_{D} = \frac{5 \times 4}{10 + 4} = 1.43 \, \text{V}\)
  • At \(R_2 = 12 \, \text{k}\Omega\): \(V_{D} = \frac{5 \times 12}{10 + 12} = 2.73 \, \text{V}\)

2. Power Dissipation (\(P = V^2 / R\)):

  • \(P_{min} = \frac{(1.43)^2}{4000} \approx 0.51 \, \text{mW}\)
  • \(P_{max} = \frac{(2.73)^2}{12000} \approx 0.62 \, \text{mW}\)

Tip

In ECE design, always check if \(0.62 \, \text{mW}\) exceeds the sensor’s power rating to prevent damage or bias.

Problem 3: Bridge Resolution

Scenario:

A Wheatstone bridge has \(R_1 = R_2 = R_3 = R_4 = 120 \, \Omega\) and \(V = 10.0 \, \text{V}\).

A Digital Voltmeter (DVM) with \(100 \, \mu\text{V}\) resolution is used as the detector.

Task:

Find the smallest measurable change in resistance (\(\Delta R_4\)) that the DVM can detect.

Resolution defines the sensitivity of our measurement system. Even if a sensor changes resistance, if the bridge output change is smaller than the DVM resolution, we can’t see it.

Solution 3: Bridge Resolution

The offset voltage is:

\(\Delta V = \frac{V R_3}{R_1 + R_3} - \frac{V (R_4 - \Delta R)}{R_2 + (R_4 - \Delta R)}\)

Given \(\Delta V = 100 \, \mu\text{V}\), \(V = 10 \, \text{V}\), and \(R = 120 \, \Omega\):

\(10^{-4} = \frac{10(120)}{240} - \frac{10(120 - \Delta R)}{120 + (120 - \Delta R)}\)

\(10^{-4} = 5 - \frac{1200 - 10\Delta R}{240 - \Delta R}\)

Solving for \(\Delta R\):

\(\Delta R \approx 0.0048 \, \Omega\)

Result: The bridge can resolve a resistance change as small as \(4.8 \, \text{m}\Omega\).

Problem 4: AC Bridge Balance

Scenario:

An AC bridge is at null.

\(Z_1 = 1 \, \text{k}\Omega\) (Resistor). \(Z_2 = 2 \, \text{k}\Omega\) (Resistor). \(Z_3 = 1 \, \text{k}\Omega\) resistor in series with a \(1 \, \mu\text{F}\) capacitor.

Task:

Find the equivalent resistance (\(R_x\)) and capacitance (\(C_x\)) for the unknown arm \(Z_x\).

In AC bridges, we must balance both the real (magnitude) and imaginary (phase) parts. The frequency (\(\omega\)) usually cancels out in simple RC bridges, making the balance independent of frequency.

Solution 4: AC Bridge Balance

Null Condition: \(Z_1 Z_x = Z_2 Z_3\)

\(R_1 (R_x - \frac{j}{\omega C_x}) = R_2 (R_3 - \frac{j}{\omega C})\)

Equating Real Parts:

\(R_1 R_x = R_2 R_3 \implies R_x = \frac{R_2 R_3}{R_1} = \frac{(2\text{k})(1\text{k})}{1\text{k}} = 2 \, \text{k}\Omega\)

Equating Imaginary Parts:

\(\frac{R_1}{\omega C_x} = \frac{R_2}{\omega C} \implies C_x = C \frac{R_1}{R_2} = (1 \, \mu\text{F}) \frac{1\text{k}}{2\text{k}} = 0.5 \, \mu\text{F}\)

Problem 5: Low-Pass Filter Design

Requirement:

A signal contains data at \(f < 1 \, \text{kHz}\) but has noise at \(1 \, \text{MHz}\). Design a filter that attenuates the \(1 \, \text{MHz}\) noise to \(1\%\) of its original amplitude (\(V_{out}/V_{in} = 0.01\)).

Task:

  1. Calculate the required critical frequency \(f_c\).
  2. Select \(R\) and \(C\) values.

Engineers often start with a desired attenuation at a specific noise frequency. Then they work backward to find the cutoff frequency.

Solution 5: Low-Pass Filter

Step 1: Find \(f_c\)

\(\frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (f/f_c)^2}}\) \(0.01 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (10^6/f_c)^2}} \implies (10^6/f_c)^2 \approx 10,000\)

\(f_c = 10 \, \text{kHz}\)

Step 2: Component Selection

Pick \(C = 0.01 \, \mu\text{F}\):

\(R = \frac{1}{2 \pi f_c C} = \frac{1}{2 \pi (10^4)(10^{-8})} = 1591 \, \Omega\)

Standard Choice: Use \(R = 1.5 \, \text{k}\Omega\) or a \(1.6 \, \text{k}\Omega\) resistor.

Problem 6: Twin-T Notch Filter

Scenario:

An EKG monitor is picking up \(60 \, \text{Hz}\) interference from power lines.

Design a Twin-T notch filter to reject this specific frequency.

Task:

  1. Find \(f_c\) for the Twin-T circuit.
  2. If \(C = 0.1 \, \mu\text{F}\), find \(R, R_1,\) and \(C_1\).

The Twin-T is a special passive filter. Note that the notch frequency \(f_n\) is NOT equal to \(f_c\). It is \(0.785 \times f_c\). This is a common point of confusion for students.

Solution 6: Twin-T Notch Filter

Step 1: Calculate \(f_c\)

\(f_n = 0.785 f_c \implies 60 = 0.785 f_c\)

\(f_c = 76.43 \, \text{Hz}\)

Step 2: Calculate Components

  • \(R = \frac{1}{2 \pi f_c C} = \frac{1}{2 \pi (76.43)(10^{-7})} = 20,823 \, \Omega\)

  • \(R_1 = \frac{\pi R}{10} = \frac{\pi (20823)}{10} \approx 6,541 \, \Omega\)

  • \(C_1 = \frac{10 C}{\pi} = \frac{10 (0.1 \, \mu\text{F})}{\pi} \approx 0.318 \, \mu\text{F}\)
