Understanding Sensor Behavior over Time

Static vs. Dynamic

  • Static Transfer Function: Defines output when input is constant.
  • Dynamic Transfer Function (Time Response): Defines how output changes when input varies in time.

Importance

Sensors provide the “knowledge” of the controlled variable. Lag or oscillation in measurement can lead to control errors.

In this section, we distinguish between static and dynamic behavior. While the static function tells us the steady-state value, the dynamic response tells us how we get there. This is critical for ECE students because control loops depend on timely data. If a sensor lags, the controller is acting on “old” information.

First-Order Response

The most common sensor response, characterized by an exponential approach to the final value.

General Equation: \[b(t) = b_i + (b_f - b_i)[1 - e^{-t/\tau}]\]

  • \(b_i\): Initial sensor output.
  • \(b_f\): Final sensor output.
  • \(\tau\): Time Constant (Characteristic of the sensor).

Note

At \(t = \tau\), the output has reached 63.2% of its total change.

At \(t = 5\tau\), the output has reached 99.3% (considered settled).

The first-order response is derived from a first-order differential equation, typically representing systems with a single energy storage element (like thermal mass in a thermometer or a capacitor in an RC filter). Note that the slope is steepest at t=0, which is a theoretical approximation of real-world sensors.

Second-Order Response

Characterized by oscillation (ringing) before settling at the final value.

General Nature: \[R(t) \propto R_0 e^{-at} \sin(2\pi f_n t)\]

  • \(a\): Damping constant (determines how fast oscillations decay).
  • \(f_n\): Natural frequency (speed of oscillation).
  • Tracking Rule: The sensor tracks well if the input changes slower than the period of \(f_n\).

Common Examples

  • Accelerometers
  • Pressure diaphragms
  • Mechanical galvanometers

Second-order systems involve two energy storage elements (like an LC circuit or a mass-spring system). The damping constant ‘a’ is critical; if it’s too low, the system rings for a long time, leading to measurement errors during the transient period.

Significance in Measurement

Accuracy \(\neq\) Significant Figures.

  • Significant Figures: The digits actually read or known from a measurement.
  • Rule of Thumb: Your result can have no more significance than the least significant number used in the calculation.

Example: The Digital Dilemma

A 12-digit calculator might show \(18.392857^\circ\text{C}\), but if your sensor only has 3 significant figures, the answer is \(18.4^\circ\text{C}\).

Important

Design values (e.g., “a \(1.1\text{k}\Omega\) resistor”) are often treated as exact in theory, but measurements of those components must respect physical limitations.

Students often fall into the trap of providing 10 decimal places because their calculator provides them. It’s vital to emphasize that these extra digits are physically meaningless if the original measurement was only accurate to two or three places.

Statistics: Mean and Standard Deviation

Confidence in measurements is improved via statistical analysis of random errors.

Arithmetic Mean (\(\bar{x}\)): \[\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}\]

Standard Deviation (\(\sigma\)): \[\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\]

\(\sigma\) measures the spread of data. A smaller \(\sigma\) means the sensor is more precise (readings are tightly clustered).

The arithmetic mean provides the “best estimate” of the true value, while the standard deviation provides the “uncertainty” or “precision” of that estimate. In process control, we use these to determine if a process is stable or if a sensor is failing.

Case Study: Control System Success

Problem: Evaluate a potato chip packaging system (Goal: 200g).

Before Control

  • Mean \(\bar{W}_b = 202\text{g}\)
  • \(\sigma_b = 11\text{g}\)
  • \(99\%\) range: \(202 \pm 33\text{g}\)

After Control

  • Mean \(\bar{W}_a = 199\text{g}\)
  • \(\sigma_a = 5\text{g}\)
  • \(99\%\) range: \(199 \pm 15\text{g}\)

Conclusion: The system moved the average closer to the target and cut the spread by 50%.

This practical example shows that ECE isn’t just about the electronics; it’s about the data. Even though the mean only moved by 3 grams, the reduction in standard deviation significantly improves the consistency of the product and reduces waste.

Summary and Key Takeaways

  1. Time Response: Sensors are not instantaneous. First-order systems lag; second-order systems oscillate.
  2. Time Constant (\(\tau\)): Defines the “speed” of first-order sensors; 5\(\tau\) is the standard settling time.
  3. Significance: Never report more digits than your measurement hardware can justify.
  4. Statistics: Use \(\bar{x}\) for the average and \(\sigma\) to quantify the reliability/spread of your process.

Wrap up by reminding students that these mathematical tools allow us to predict how a control loop will behave in the real world, where things don’t change instantly and measurements aren’t always perfect.

Problem 1: First-Order Temperature Sensor

Scenario:

A sensor measures temperature linearly:

  • Transfer Function: \(33\text{ mV}/^\circ\text{C}\)
  • Time Constant (\(\tau\)): \(1.5\text{ s}\)

The Event:

At \(t=0\), the temperature suddenly changes from \(20^\circ\text{C}\) to \(41^\circ\text{C}\).

Task:

  1. Find the sensor output (voltage) at \(t = 0.75\text{ s}\).
  2. Determine the indicated temperature and the error at that moment.

Strategy

  1. Convert temperatures to initial and final voltages (\(b_i, b_f\)).
  2. Apply the first-order equation: \(b(t) = b_i + (b_f - b_i)[1 - e^{-t/\tau}]\)

This problem from Example 15 in the text demonstrates the “lag” error. Even though the environment is at 41 degrees, the sensor is still transitioning. ECE students must understand that the voltage read at t=0.75s is NOT the true process value.

Problem 2: Measurement Uncertainty

Scenario:

A digital multimeter (DMM) measures current through a \(12.5\text{-k}\Omega\) resistor as \(2.21\text{ mA}\).

  • Scale: \(10\text{-mA}\) FS (Full Scale)
  • Instrument Accuracy: \(\pm 0.2\%\) FS

Task:

Calculate the voltage across the resistor and determine the total uncertainty.

Recall

Accuracy is applied to the Full Scale of the range used, not the reading itself.

\(Uncertainty = 0.002 \times 10\text{ mA} = 0.02\text{ mA}\)

This highlights the difference between reading precision and instrument accuracy. The DMM shows three digits (2.21), but the accuracy of the instrument provides a window of uncertainty around those digits.

Solution: Problem 2

1. Nominal Voltage Calculation:

\(V = I \times R\)

\(V = 2.21\text{ mA} \times 12.5\text{ k}\Omega = 27.625\text{ V}\)

2. Applying Sig Figs:

Since \(2.21\) has 3 sig figs, we round to:

\(V = 27.6\text{ V}\)

3. Uncertainty Calculation:

\(\Delta I = \pm 0.02\text{ mA}\)

Range: \(2.19\text{ mA}\) to \(2.23\text{ mA}\)

\(V_{min} = 2.19 \times 12.5 = 27.375\text{ V}\)

\(V_{max} = 2.23 \times 12.5 = 27.875\text{ V}\)

Result:

\(27.6 \pm 0.3\text{ V}\)

Note how we express the uncertainty (\(\pm 0.3\)) to match the decimal place of our significant figure (\(27.6\)). Reporting \(27.625 \pm 0.25\) would be incorrect engineering practice because it implies more significance than the current measurement supports.

Problem 3: Standard Deviation Analysis

Scenario:

Temperature was measured at 8 locations in a server room to check for “hot spots.”

Data (\(^\circ\text{C}\)): 21.2, 25.0, 18.5, 22.1, 19.7, 27.1, 19.0, 20.0

Task:

Calculate the Arithmetic Mean (\(\bar{x}\)) and the Standard Deviation (\(\sigma\)).

Formula Reminder

\[\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n} \quad , \quad \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\]

In ECE, we often average multiple sensor readings (like in a server room or a chemical reactor) to get a more reliable “global” variable for the control system.

Problem 4: Evaluating Control Performance

Scenario:

A potato chip bagger (Target = 200g).

Before Control: \(\bar{W} = 202\text{g}, \sigma = 11\text{g}\)

After Control: \(\bar{W} = 199\text{g}, \sigma = 5\text{g}\)

Question:

Using the \(99.7\%\) (\(3\sigma\)) rule, what are the weight ranges before and after?

Solution: Problem 4

Solution:

  • Before: \(202 \pm (3 \times 11) = 202 \pm 33\text{g}\) Range: 169g to 235g
  • After: \(199 \pm (3 \times 5) = 199 \pm 15\text{g}\) Range: 184g to 214g

Verdict: The system is much more consistent, reducing “underweight” complaints and “overweight” waste.

This concluding problem connects the math to business value. By reducing \(\sigma\) from 11 to 5, the engineer has significantly tightened the production tolerance, which is the primary goal of most process control systems.
