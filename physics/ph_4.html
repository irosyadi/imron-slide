4.1 Displacement and Velocity Vectors in Multiple Dimensions

Learning Objectives

This is a crucial topic as most real-world motion occurs in 2 or 3 dimensions, not just a straight line.

We’ll build upon our understanding of 1D motion, but now we must incorporate vector algebra.

Introduction to Multidimensional Motion

To describe motion in two and three dimensions, we must first establish a coordinate system and a convention for the axes. We generally use the coordinates x, y, and z to locate a particle at point P(x, y, z) in three dimensions.

If the particle is moving, the variables x, y, and z are functions of time (t):

x = x(t), y = y(t), z = z(t).

In one dimension, we only needed a single axis (e.g., the x-axis) and a sign (positive/negative) to describe position.

Now, for 2D or 3D motion, we need multiple axes and a clear convention.

The standard is a right-handed coordinate system, which we’ll assume unless stated otherwise.

This means if you point your right thumb along the positive z-axis, your fingers curl from the positive x-axis to the positive y-axis.

The coordinates x, y, and z are not fixed values; they change as the object moves, hence they are functions of time.

Position Vector in 3D

The position vector from the origin of the coordinate system to point P is r→(t).

In unit vector notation, r→(t) is:

\[ \mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\hat{i} + y(t)\hat{j} + z(t)\hat{k} \]

Tip

The unit vectors \(\hat{i}\), \(\hat{j}\), and \(\hat{k}\) represent directions along the positive x, y, and z axes, respectively.

Caption: Figure 4.2 A three-dimensional coordinate system with a particle at position P(x(t), y(t), z(t)).

The position vector r→(t) is a fundamental tool. It points from the origin of our chosen coordinate system to the location of the particle at a specific time t.

Its components, x(t), y(t), and z(t), tell us how far the particle is along each axis.

The use of unit vectors \(\hat{i}\), \(\hat{j}\), \(\hat{k}\) is standard and simplifies vector notation.

This figure shows a typical right-handed coordinate system, with x pointing out, y to the right, and z upwards.

Displacement Vector

The displacement vector Δr is the change in position of a particle. If a particle moves from position P₁ at time t₁ to position P₂ at time t₂, its displacement is:

\[ \Delta \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}(t_2) - \mathbf{r}(t_1) \]

This is the same concept as in 1D, but now we are subtracting vectors.

Caption: Figure 4.3 The displacement Δr→ = r→(t₂) - r→(t₁) is the vector from P₁ to P₂.

Displacement is always a straight line from the initial point to the final point, regardless of the path taken.

Remember vector subtraction: \(\Delta \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_2 - \mathbf{r}_1\). If \(\mathbf{r}_1 = x_1\hat{i} + y_1\hat{j} + z_1\hat{k}\) and \(\mathbf{r}_2 = x_2\hat{i} + y_2\hat{j} + z_2\hat{k}\), then \(\Delta \mathbf{r} = (x_2-x_1)\hat{i} + (y_2-y_1)\hat{j} + (z_2-z_1)\hat{k}\).

This highlights how the vector displacement is composed of the differences in the x, y, and z coordinates.

Problem: Satellite Displacement

A satellite is in a circular polar orbit around Earth. At time \(t_1\), it is directly over the North Pole. At a later time \(t_2\), it is at −45° latitude.

What is the magnitude and direction of the displacement vector of the satellite between \(t_1\) and \(t_2\)?

Assume:

  • Earth’s radius \(R_E = 6370\) km.
  • Satellite altitude = 400 km.
  • Origin is at the center of Earth.
  • y-axis points North, x-axis points East.

This problem applies the concept of displacement to a realistic engineering scenario: orbital mechanics.

We need to define positions using vectors and then find the difference.

The key is to correctly represent the satellite’s position in Cartesian coordinates using trigonometry.

Satellite Displacement - Strategy & Solution (Part 1)

Strategy:

  1. Visualize the orbit and positions in a 2D coordinate system (since orbit is circular and we can align it with relevant axes).
  2. Define the position vectors r→(t₁) and r→(t₂) using the given radius and angles.
  3. Calculate the displacement vector Δr = r→(t₂) - r→(t₁).
  4. Determine the magnitude and direction of Δr.

Solution:

The orbital radius is \(R = R_E + \text{altitude} = 6370 \text{ km} + 400 \text{ km} = 6770\) km.

At \(t_1\) (over North Pole):

The satellite is on the positive y-axis. \[ \mathbf{r}(t_1) = 0\hat{i} + 6770\hat{j} \text{ km} \]

At \(t_2\) (at -45° latitude):

Latitude of -45° means an angle of \(90^\circ - (-45^\circ) = 135^\circ\) from the positive x-axis, or specifically \(-45^\circ\) in a standard polar-to-Cartesian conversion with the x-axis as the reference.

\[ \mathbf{r}(t_2) = R \cos(-45^\circ)\hat{i} + R \sin(-45^\circ)\hat{j} \] \[ \mathbf{r}(t_2) = 6770 \text{ km} \left(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\hat{i} + 6770 \text{ km} \left(-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)\hat{j} \] \[ \mathbf{r}(t_2) \approx 4787\hat{i} - 4787\hat{j} \text{ km} \]

Step 1: We are considering the satellite’s orbit in a plane. Since it’s a polar orbit, we can choose our coordinate system such that the North Pole corresponds to a point on the y-axis. The -45° latitude position can be described using polar coordinates relative to the x-axis.

Step 2: The radius of the orbit is constant. For \(t_1\), the satellite is directly above the North Pole, meaning its position vector points straight up along the y-axis. For \(t_2\), we use trigonometry. A latitude of -45° corresponds to an angle of 45° South of the Equator. In our x-y plane (x=East, y=North), this position can be found using \(R\cos(\theta)\) for x and \(R\sin(\theta)\) for y, where \(\theta\) is measured from the positive x-axis. A -45° latitude means the angle from the positive x-axis is -45°.

Satellite Displacement - Solution (Part 2) & Significance

Solution (cont.):

Now, calculate the displacement vector:

\[ \Delta \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}(t_2) - \mathbf{r}(t_1) \] \[ \Delta \mathbf{r} = (4787\hat{i} - 4787\hat{j}) - (0\hat{i} + 6770\hat{j}) \text{ km} \] \[ \Delta \mathbf{r} = 4787\hat{i} - (4787 + 6770)\hat{j} \text{ km} \] \[ \Delta \mathbf{r} = 4787\hat{i} - 11557\hat{j} \text{ km} \]

Magnitude:

\[ |\Delta \mathbf{r}| = \sqrt{(4787)^2 + (-11557)^2} \text{ km} \] \[ |\Delta \mathbf{r}| \approx \sqrt{22.9 \times 10^6 + 133.6 \times 10^6} \text{ km} \] \[ |\Delta \mathbf{r}| \approx \sqrt{156.5 \times 10^6} \text{ km} \approx 12509 \text{ km} \]

Direction:

The angle \(\theta\) with the positive x-axis is: \[ \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{-11557}{4787}\right) \approx \tan^{-1}(-2.414) \] \[ \theta \approx -67.5^\circ \]

Caption: Figure 4.5 Displacement vector with components, angle, and magnitude.

Significance: The displacement vector is the shortest straight-line distance between the two points in space. The satellite traveled approximately 12,509 km from its position over the North Pole to the −45° latitude position. The angle indicates its general direction relative to the East axis.

Part 2 focuses on the calculation and interpretation.

We subtract the components: x-component of displacement is \(4787 - 0\), and y-component is \(-4787 - 6770\).

For magnitude, we use the Pythagorean theorem in 2D.

For direction, we use the arctangent function, making sure to consider the quadrant (in this case, the negative y-component and positive x-component place it in the fourth quadrant, consistent with -67.5°).

The figure visually represents these components and the resultant displacement vector.

It’s important to note that the actual path along the orbit is longer than this displacement vector.

Problem: Brownian Motion

Brownian motion describes the random movement of particles suspended in a fluid. This motion is three-dimensional. The displacements of a particle undergoing Brownian motion over four consecutive time intervals are given in micrometers (\(\mu\)m):

\(\Delta \mathbf{r}_1 = 2.0\hat{i} + 1.0\hat{j} + 3.0\hat{k}\)

\(\Delta \mathbf{r}_2 = -1.0\hat{i} + 0\hat{j} + 3.0\hat{k}\)

\(\Delta \mathbf{r}_3 = 4.0\hat{i} - 2.0\hat{j} + 1.0\hat{k}\)

\(\Delta \mathbf{r}_4 = -3.0\hat{i} + 1.0\hat{j} + 2.0\hat{k}\)

What is the total displacement of the particle from its starting point?

This example demonstrates adding multiple displacement vectors.

Brownian motion is relevant in many engineering fields, like material science, chemical engineering (diffusion), and even fluid dynamics.

The random nature means we can’t predict the exact path, but we can find the net change in position.

Brownian Motion - Solution

To find the total displacement, we simply add the individual displacement vectors:

\[ \Delta \mathbf{r}_{\text{Total}} = \Delta \mathbf{r}_1 + \Delta \mathbf{r}_2 + \Delta \mathbf{r}_3 + \Delta \mathbf{r}_4 \]

Add the \(\hat{i}\), \(\hat{j}\), and \(\hat{k}\) components separately:

\[ \Delta \mathbf{r}_{\text{Total}} = (2.0 - 1.0 + 4.0 - 3.0)\hat{i} + (1.0 + 0 - 2.0 + 1.0)\hat{j} + (3.0 + 3.0 + 1.0 + 2.0)\hat{k} \text{ } \mu\text{m} \]

\[ \Delta \mathbf{r}_{\text{Total}} = (2.0)\hat{i} + (0)\hat{j} + (9.0)\hat{k} \text{ } \mu\text{m} \]

Magnitude and Direction: \[ |\Delta \mathbf{r}_{\text{Total}}| = \sqrt{(2.0)^2 + (0)^2 + (9.0)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 0 + 81} = \sqrt{85} \approx 9.2 \mu\text{m} \]

The displacement is \(2.0\hat{i} + 9.0\hat{k} \, \mu\text{m}\). This vector lies in the xz-plane.

The angle with the x-axis is \(\theta = \tan^{-1}(9.0/2.0) \approx 77^\circ\).

Caption: Figure 4.6 Trajectory of a particle undergoing random displacements of Brownian motion. The total displacement is shown in red.

The crucial step here is understanding that vector addition is done component-wise.

We sum all the x-components, all the y-components, and all the z-components.

The resulting total displacement vector points from the particle’s initial position to its final position after these four movements.

Notice that the sum of the magnitudes of the individual displacements would be much larger than the magnitude of the total displacement, illustrating the effect of random directions.

This is a key concept in statistical mechanics and transport phenomena.

Velocity Vector - Definition

The instantaneous velocity vector v→(t) is the rate of change of the position vector with respect to time. It is the derivative of the position vector:

\[ \mathbf{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{r}(t+\Delta t) - \mathbf{r}(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} \]

The velocity vector is always tangent to the path of the particle.

Caption: Figure 4.7 A particle moves along a path. As Δt approaches zero, the velocity vector becomes tangent to the path.

This is the direct extension of the 1D definition of instantaneous velocity.

The limit process (\(\Delta t \to 0\)) turns the average velocity over an interval into the instantaneous velocity at a point.

Geometrically, as the two points defining the displacement vector get closer and closer, the displacement vector itself becomes more and more aligned with the tangent to the curve at that point.

This is a fundamental concept for analyzing dynamics, especially in fields like control systems or robotics.

Velocity Vector - Components

We can also express the instantaneous velocity in terms of its components along the coordinate axes:

Since \(\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\hat{i} + y(t)\hat{j} + z(t)\hat{k}\), we can write:

\[ \mathbf{v}(t) = v_x(t)\hat{i} + v_y(t)\hat{j} + v_z(t)\hat{k} \]

where the components are:

\[ v_x(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt}, \quad v_y(t) = \frac{dy(t)}{dt}, \quad v_z(t) = \frac{dz(t)}{dt} \]

The average velocity \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}}\) over a time interval \(t_1\) to \(t_2\) is:

\[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}(t_2) - \mathbf{r}(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} \]

This component-wise definition is extremely useful for calculations. To find the velocity vector, we just need to differentiate each coordinate function (x(t), y(t), z(t)) with respect to time.

The same applies to acceleration later on.

The average velocity is simply the total displacement divided by the time taken. It’s a vector connecting the start and end points of the motion over that interval.

It’s important to distinguish between instantaneous velocity (at a moment) and average velocity (over an interval).

Problem: Calculating the Velocity Vector

The position function of a particle is given by:

\[ \mathbf{r}(t) = (2.0t^2)\hat{i} + (2.0 + 3.0t)\hat{j} + (5.0t)\hat{k} \quad (\text{in meters}) \]

  1. What are the instantaneous velocity and speed at \(t = 2.0\) s?
  2. What is the average velocity between \(t = 1.0\) s and \(t = 3.0\) s?

This is a direct application of differentiation to find velocity.

Part (a) tests finding the instantaneous velocity vector and then its magnitude (speed).

Part (b) tests finding the average velocity vector.

These calculations are common in analyzing the motion of objects in engineering systems, such as vehicles, projectiles, or robotic arms.

Velocity Calculation - Solution (a)

(a) Instantaneous velocity and speed at \(t = 2.0\) s:

First, find the velocity vector \(\mathbf{v}(t)\) by differentiating \(\mathbf{r}(t)\):

\[ \mathbf{v}(t) = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(2.0t^2)\hat{i} + \frac{d}{dt}(2.0 + 3.0t)\hat{j} + \frac{d}{dt}(5.0t)\hat{k} \] \[ \mathbf{v}(t) = (4.0t)\hat{i} + (3.0)\hat{j} + (5.0)\hat{k} \quad (\text{m/s}) \]

Now, evaluate at \(t = 2.0\) s:

\[ \mathbf{v}(2.0 \text{ s}) = (4.0 \times 2.0)\hat{i} + 3.0\hat{j} + 5.0\hat{k} \quad (\text{m/s}) \] \[ \mathbf{v}(2.0 \text{ s}) = 8.0\hat{i} + 3.0\hat{j} + 5.0\hat{k} \quad (\text{m/s}) \]

Speed is the magnitude of the velocity vector:

\[ |\mathbf{v}(2.0 \text{ s})| = \sqrt{(8.0)^2 + (3.0)^2 + (5.0)^2} \quad (\text{m/s}) \] \[ |\mathbf{v}(2.0 \text{ s})| = \sqrt{64 + 9 + 25} = \sqrt{98} \approx 9.9 \text{ m/s} \]

For part (a), we differentiate each term of the position vector component-wise:

  • \(\frac{d}{dt}(2.0t^2) = 4.0t\)
  • \(\frac{d}{dt}(2.0 + 3.0t) = 3.0\) (constant terms and linear terms)
  • \(\frac{d}{dt}(5.0t) = 5.0\)

Then, substitute \(t=2.0\) s into the resulting velocity vector.

Speed is the magnitude, calculated using the 3D Pythagorean theorem.

Velocity Calculation - Solution (b)

(b) Average velocity between \(t = 1.0\) s and \(t = 3.0\) s:

First, find the position vectors at \(t_1 = 1.0\) s and \(t_2 = 3.0\) s:

\[ \mathbf{r}(1.0 \text{ s}) = (2.0(1.0)^2)\hat{i} + (2.0 + 3.0(1.0))\hat{j} + (5.0(1.0))\hat{k} \] \[ \mathbf{r}(1.0 \text{ s}) = 2.0\hat{i} + 5.0\hat{j} + 5.0\hat{k} \quad (\text{m}) \]

\[ \mathbf{r}(3.0 \text{ s}) = (2.0(3.0)^2)\hat{i} + (2.0 + 3.0(3.0))\hat{j} + (5.0(3.0))\hat{k} \] \[ \mathbf{r}(3.0 \text{ s}) = (2.0 \times 9.0)\hat{i} + (2.0 + 9.0)\hat{j} + 15.0\hat{k} \] \[ \mathbf{r}(3.0 \text{ s}) = 18.0\hat{i} + 11.0\hat{j} + 15.0\hat{k} \quad (\text{m}) \]

Now, calculate the average velocity:

\[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}(3.0 \text{ s}) - \mathbf{r}(1.0 \text{ s})}{3.0 \text{ s} - 1.0 \text{ s}} \] \[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{(18.0\hat{i} + 11.0\hat{j} + 15.0\hat{k}) - (2.0\hat{i} + 5.0\hat{j} + 5.0\hat{k})}{2.0 \text{ s}} \] \[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{(18.0-2.0)\hat{i} + (11.0-5.0)\hat{j} + (15.0-5.0)\hat{k}}{2.0 \text{ s}} \] \[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{16.0\hat{i} + 6.0\hat{j} + 10.0\hat{k}}{2.0 \text{ s}} \] \[ \mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}} = 8.0\hat{i} + 3.0\hat{j} + 5.0\hat{k} \quad (\text{m/s}) \]

Significance: Interestingly, the average velocity (8.0\(\hat{i}\) + 3.0\(\hat{j}\) + 5.0\(\hat{k}\) m/s) is the same as the instantaneous velocity at \(t = 2.0\) s. This occurs because the velocity vector \(\mathbf{v}(t) = 4.0t\hat{i} + 3.0\hat{j} + 5.0\hat{k}\) is a linear function of time. The instantaneous velocity at the midpoint of the interval (\(t=2.0\)s) equals the average velocity over that interval for linear velocity functions.

For part (b), we first need the position vectors at the start and end times. Substitute \(t=1.0\) s and \(t=3.0\) s into the position equation.

Then, we compute the displacement vector by subtracting the initial position from the final position.

Finally, divide this displacement vector by the time interval (\(3.0 - 1.0 = 2.0\) s).

The significance note points out a special case: when the velocity function is linear, the average velocity over an interval is equal to the instantaneous velocity at the midpoint of that interval. This is a property similar to how the average value of a linear function over an interval is the value at the midpoint.

In general, instantaneous and average velocities are different.

Independence of Perpendicular Motions

A crucial insight for analyzing multidimensional motion is that motion along perpendicular axes are independent of each other.

This means:

  • Motion along the x-axis is only affected by forces acting along the x-axis.
  • Motion along the y-axis is only affected by forces acting along the y-axis.
  • Motion along the z-axis is only affected by forces acting along the z-axis.

This principle allows us to break down complex 2D or 3D motion into simpler 1D problems.

This is perhaps the most powerful concept when extending from 1D to multi-D kinematics.

It simplifies analysis immensely. For example, when analyzing projectile motion, we treat the horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components separately. The horizontal motion is governed by initial horizontal velocity and no horizontal forces (ignoring air resistance), while the vertical motion is governed by gravity and the initial vertical velocity.

These two independent motions combine to produce the resultant trajectory.

Independence of Motion - Example: Projectile Motion

Consider two baseballs:

  1. One is dropped from rest.
  2. Another is thrown horizontally from the same height at the same instant.

Despite the horizontal motion of the second ball, both balls have identical vertical positions and velocities at every instant in time.

Why?

  • Horizontal Motion: The horizontally thrown ball has a constant horizontal velocity (assuming no air resistance). There are no horizontal forces acting on it.
  • Vertical Motion: Both balls experience the same downward acceleration due to gravity. They start with the same initial vertical velocity (zero).

The combination of these independent motions results in the distinct trajectories.

Caption: Figure 4.8 A diagram of the motions of two identical balls: one falls from rest and the other has an initial horizontal velocity. The vertical motions are identical.

This example, often demonstrated with a “monkey gun” apparatus or simulations, visually confirms the independence of motion. The horizontally thrown ball travels a parabolic path. This parabola is the result of its constant horizontal velocity combined with the constant downward acceleration due to gravity. The fact that the vertical motion is unaffected by the horizontal motion is key to analyzing projectile motion. We can calculate the time it takes for the ball to hit the ground using only the vertical information (initial vertical velocity, height, acceleration due to gravity) and then use that time to determine how far it traveled horizontally. This principle is fundamental to fields like ballistics, aerospace engineering, and sports science.

Key Takeaways

  • Position Vector: \(\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\hat{i} + y(t)\hat{j} + z(t)\hat{k}\) describes an object’s location in 2D or 3D space.
  • Displacement Vector: \(\Delta\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}(t_2) - \mathbf{r}(t_1)\) is the straight-line change in position, calculated using vector subtraction.
  • Instantaneous Velocity: \(\mathbf{v}(t) = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\) is the rate of change of position, tangent to the path.
  • Average Velocity: \(\mathbf{v}_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta\mathbf{r}}{\Delta t}\) is displacement divided by time.
  • Independence of Motion: Perpendicular components of motion (e.g., x and y) can be analyzed separately, simplifying complex trajectories.

These are the core concepts from today’s lecture.

Remember that in physics, “vector” means magnitude AND direction.

Always use vector algebra for displacement and velocity in multiple dimensions.

The independence of motion is a powerful tool for problem-solving, especially in projectile motion.

We’ve covered how to define position, calculate changes in position (displacement), and describe rates of change of position (velocity).

The next step in physics is usually acceleration, which follows similar principles.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Position Vector A vector pointing from the origin of a coordinate system to the location of an object.
