Position, Displacement, and Average Velocity

Welcome to Engineering Physics I! Today, we’re diving into the fundamental concepts of kinematics – the description of motion. We’ll specifically focus on defining and understanding position, displacement, and average velocity, which are crucial for analyzing any moving system in engineering.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Define position, displacement, and distance traveled.
  • Calculate the total displacement given position as a function of time.
  • Determine the total distance traveled.
  • Calculate the average velocity given displacement and elapsed time.

These learning objectives will guide our discussion. By the end, you should have a solid grasp of these terms and be able to apply them to practical problems, as we’ll see with an engineering challenge later.

Introduction: Describing Motion

To analyze any moving system, we ask fundamental questions:

  • Where is the object?
  • Where is it going?
  • How fast is it moving?

These questions lead us to define:

  1. Position
  2. Displacement
  3. Average Velocity

Note

In Engineering Physics, precise definitions are paramount. We need clear, unambiguous terms to build our models and ensure accurate analysis.

Whether you’re designing a high-speed train, programming an autonomous robot, or predicting the trajectory of a projectile, understanding these basics is essential. Kinematics is the branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of objects without reference to the forces that cause the motion.

Position (\(x\)) and Frame of Reference

Position (\(x\) or \(y\)) tells us where an object is at any particular time.

  • Defined relative to a frame of reference.
  • A frame of reference is an arbitrary set of axes, fixed to a chosen origin.
  • It provides a consistent baseline for measurement.

For example:

  • A rocket’s position relative to Earth.
  • A cyclist’s position relative to buildings along a road.
  • A passenger’s position relative to an airplane’s interior.

Figure 3.2: Cyclists in Vietnam. Their position can be described relative to buildings or a canal.

The choice of a frame of reference is critical and often simplifies problem-solving. For one-dimensional motion, we typically use the x-axis for horizontal motion and the y-axis for vertical motion. It’s arbitrary, but once chosen, we must stick to it consistently.

Displacement (\(\Delta x\))

Displacement is the change in an object’s position.

  • It implies an object has moved, or has been “displaced”.
  • Displacement is a vector quantity: it has both magnitude and direction.
    • Can be positive or negative, depending on the chosen positive direction.
  • Calculated as the difference between final and initial positions.

\[ \Delta x = x_f - x_0 \]

Where:

  • \(\Delta x\): displacement
  • \(x_f\): final position
  • \(x_0\): initial position

Important

The uppercase Greek letter \(\Delta\) (delta) always means “change in” whatever quantity follows it.

Figure 3.3: A professor paces. Her displacement is \(+2.0\text{ m}\) to the right.

Displacement is not just how far something traveled, but also in what direction. If you walk 5 meters forward and 5 meters backward, your total displacement is zero, even though you walked a total of 10 meters. This distinction is crucial in physics and engineering for understanding net movement versus actual path taken.

Displacement - Multiple Movements

Often, motion involves a series of individual displacements.

  • Total Displacement is the sum of all individual displacements.

\[ \Delta x_{\text{Total}} = \sum \Delta x_i \]

Example: A professor moves \(2\text{ m}\) right, then \(4\text{ m}\) left.

  • First displacement: \(\Delta x_1 = +2\text{ m}\)
  • Second displacement: \(\Delta x_2 = -4\text{ m}\)
  • Total Displacement: \(\Delta x_{\text{Total}} = (+2\text{ m}) + (-4\text{ m}) = -2\text{ m}\)

Tip

The sign of displacement indicates its direction relative to the chosen positive axis. A negative \(\Delta x\) means the object is ultimately positioned in the negative direction from its starting point.

Even with multiple movements, total displacement only cares about the net change from start to finish. It doesn’t track the actual path taken between those points. This leads us to distinguish it from another important quantity: distance traveled.

Displacement vs. Distance Traveled

These two terms are often confused, but are fundamentally different.

Displacement (\(\Delta x\))

  • Vector quantity.
  • Change in position from the initial point to the final point.
  • Can be positive, negative, or zero.
  • Depends only on initial and final positions.
  • Formula: \(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\)

Distance Traveled (\(x_{\text{Total}}\))

  • Scalar quantity.
  • Total length of the entire path covered.
  • Always positive (or zero if no motion occurs).
  • Depends on the entire path taken.
  • Formula: \(x_{\text{Total}} = \sum |\Delta x_i|\)

Example (Professor):

  • Displacement: \(-2\text{ m}\) (The professor is \(2\text{ m}\) to the left of their starting point)
  • Distance Traveled: \(|+2\text{ m}| + |-4\text{ m}| = 2\text{ m} + 4\text{ m} = 6\text{ m}\) (The professor covered a total path length of \(6\text{ m}\))

Think about a lap on a race track. If you start and end at the same point, your displacement is zero. But you’ve certainly traveled a significant distance! This distinction is crucial for many engineering applications, such as calculating fuel efficiency (distance) versus precise navigation (displacement).

Introducing Time and Average Velocity

To fully describe motion, we must consider time (\(\Delta t\)).

  • Time allows us to describe how fast an object is moving.
  • The rate at which position changes with time is called velocity.

For basic analysis, we often use average velocity (\(\bar{v}\) or \(v_{\text{avg}}\)).

Note

Elapsed Time (\(\Delta t\)) is the time taken to travel between two points: \(\Delta t = t_f - t_0\).

We use ‘average’ velocity when the exact details of motion at every instant aren’t critical, or when the velocity might be changing. For example, a car doesn’t always move at a constant velocity. Later, we’ll delve into instantaneous velocity for more precise analysis.

Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\))

Average Velocity is the total displacement divided by the elapsed time.

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{\text{Displacement}}{\text{Elapsed Time}} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_f - x_0}{t_f - t_0} \]

  • Vector quantity: has magnitude and direction.
  • Can be positive, negative, or zero.
  • SI unit: meters per second (\(\text{m/s}\)).

Caution

Do not confuse average velocity with average speed. Average speed = \(\frac{\text{Total Distance Traveled}}{\text{Elapsed Time}}\).

The sign of average velocity directly corresponds to the sign of the total displacement. A positive average velocity means the object moved in the positive direction overall, regardless of any back-and-forth motion within that time interval. Average speed, being a scalar, will always be non-negative.

Conceptual Check

A train travels from Station A to Station B, a distance of 100 km east, in 1 hour.

It then travels from Station B to Station C, which is 50 km west of Station B, in 0.5 hours.

What is the train’s total displacement?

  1. 150 km east
  2. 50 km east
  3. 50 km west
  4. 150 km west

Tip

Remember, displacement is about the net change in position from start to end, and direction matters.

Take a moment to consider the directions and define your positive axis. Calculate each displacement vectorially, then sum them up. We’ll go over the solution on the next slide.

Conceptual Check - Solution

Let East be the positive direction (+x).

  • First displacement (Station A to B): \(\Delta x_1 = +100\text{ km}\)
  • Second displacement (Station B to C): \(\Delta x_2 = -50\text{ km}\)

Total Displacement:

\(\Delta x_{\text{Total}} = \Delta x_1 + \Delta x_2 = (+100\text{ km}) + (-50\text{ km}) = +50\text{ km}\)

The train’s total displacement is 50 km east.

Answer: B) 50 km east

Even though the train traveled a total distance of 150 km (100 km + 50 km), its net change in position from the starting point was only 50 km to the east. This is a clear demonstration of the difference between distance and displacement.

Challenging Problem: Automated Drone Delivery

An engineering team is testing an autonomous delivery drone.

The drone starts from the base (position \(x=0\)).

  1. It flies 0.5 km East to deliver Package 1, taking 9 minutes.
  2. It realizes it missed a package and flies back to the base (West), taking another 9 minutes.
  3. After loading, it flies 1.0 km East from the base to deliver Package 2, taking 15 minutes.
  4. Finally, it flies 1.75 km West from its current position to scout a new delivery zone, taking 25 minutes, then rests.

Conceptual timeline of drone movements.

Your Task:

  1. What is the drone’s total displacement when it stops to rest?
  2. What is the magnitude of the final displacement?
  3. What is the average velocity during its entire trip?
  4. What is the total distance traveled?
  5. Sketch a position versus time graph.

This problem integrates all the concepts we’ve discussed today in a practical engineering context. Take your time to break it down, carefully define your positive direction, and track each leg of the journey. We’ll solve it step-by-step on the following slides.

Problem-Solving Strategy

To tackle this multi-leg motion problem:

  1. Define a positive direction: For instance, let East be +x.
  2. Tabulate Data: Create a table tracking cumulative time and position for each segment.
  3. Calculate Individual Displacements (\(\Delta x_i\)): For each leg of the journey.
  4. Calculate Total Displacement (\(\Delta x_{\text{Total}}\)): Sum of all individual displacements.
  5. Calculate Magnitude of Total Displacement: \(| \Delta x_{\text{Total}} |\), which is always positive.
  6. Calculate Total Elapsed Time (\(\Delta t_{\text{Total}}\)): Sum of individual time intervals.
  7. Calculate Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)): Total Displacement divided by Total Elapsed Time.
  8. Calculate Total Distance Traveled (\(x_{\text{Total}}\)): Sum of the magnitudes of all individual displacements.
  9. Visualize: Plot a position-time graph to better understand the motion.

A systematic approach is crucial in engineering. Breaking down complex problems into smaller, manageable steps reduces the chance of errors and clarifies your understanding of the physical processes involved.

Solution Part 1: Displacement Calculations

Let East be the positive direction (+). The drone starts at initial position (\(x_0\)) = 0 km at initial time (\(t_0\)) = 0 min.

Leg Time \(t_i\) (min) Position \(x_i\) (km) Displacement \(\Delta x_i\) (km)
0 \(t_0=0\) \(x_0=0\) \(\Delta x_0=0\)
1 \(t_1=9\) \(x_1=0.5\) \(\Delta x_1 = x_1 - x_0 = 0.5\)
2 \(t_2=9+9=18\) \(x_2=0\) \(\Delta x_2 = x_2 - x_1 = -0.5\)
3 \(t_3=18+15=33\) \(x_3=1.0\) \(\Delta x_3 = x_3 - x_2 = 1.0\)
4 \(t_4=33+25=58\) \(x_4=-0.75\) \(\Delta x_4 = x_4 - x_3 = -1.75\)

1. Total Displacement (\(\Delta x_{\text{Total}}\)):

Sum of individual displacements:

\(\Delta x_{\text{Total}} = 0.5 - 0.5 + 1.0 - 1.75\text{ km}\)

\(\Delta x_{\text{Total}} = -0.75\text{ km}\)

2. Magnitude of Final Displacement:

This is the absolute value of the total displacement.

\(| \Delta x_{\text{Total}} | = |-0.75\text{ km}| = 0.75\text{ km}\)

Notice how the positions are cumulative. The position at any given time is the sum of all preceding displacements from the origin. The final position directly gives us the total displacement when the starting point is zero. The negative sign for total displacement tells us the drone ended up West of its starting point.

Solution Part 2: Average Velocity & Distance Traveled

3. Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)):

  • Total Displacement: \(\Delta x_{\text{Total}} = -0.75\text{ km}\) (from previous slide)
  • Total Elapsed Time: \(\Delta t_{\text{Total}} = 58\text{ min}\) (from the table)

\(\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x_{\text{Total}}}{\Delta t_{\text{Total}}} = \frac{-0.75\text{ km}}{58\text{ min}}\)

\(\bar{v} \approx -0.013\text{ km/min}\)

4. Total Distance Traveled (\(x_{\text{Total}}\)):

Sum of the magnitudes of individual displacements:

\(x_{\text{Total}} = |\Delta x_1| + |\Delta x_2| + |\Delta x_3| + |\Delta x_4|\)

\(x_{\text{Total}} = |0.5\text{ km}| + |-0.5\text{ km}| + |1.0\text{ km}| + |-1.75\text{ km}|\)

\(x_{\text{Total}} = 0.5 + 0.5 + 1.0 + 1.75\text{ km}\)

\(x_{\text{Total}} = 3.75\text{ km}\)

The negative average velocity indicates that, on average, the drone moved in the negative (West) direction relative to its starting point over the entire trip. Its total path length, however, was 3.75 km. This highlights how an inefficient path can significantly increase distance traveled while having a small net displacement.

Solution Part 3: Position-Time Graph

5. Position vs. Time Graph:

Position vs. time graph for Jill’s movements (now drone)

  • The slope of the position-time graph at any point represents the instantaneous velocity.
  • For each segment, a constant slope indicates constant velocity during that time interval.
  • A positive slope means positive velocity (East direction).
  • A negative slope means negative velocity (West direction).
  • The average velocity for the entire trip is the slope of the straight line connecting the initial point (0 min, 0 km) and the final point (58 min, -0.75 km).

This graph is a powerful visualization tool for engineers. It allows us to quickly assess the nature of motion, identify periods of constant velocity, changes in direction, and overall trends. The steeper the slope, the greater the speed of the drone.

Engineering Significance of Results

Understanding these kinematic values is critical for drone system design and mission planning:

  • Total Displacement (\(\mathbf{-0.75\text{ km}}\)):
    • Indicates the drone’s final position is \(0.75\text{ km}\) West of its starting base.
    • Essential for navigation systems to guide the drone back to the correct landing spot or to its precise final destination.
  • Average Velocity (\(\mathbf{-0.013\text{ km/min}}\)):
    • Represents the effective “straight-line” velocity from the start to the end point over the entire mission duration.
    • Useful for high-level mission planning and estimating total mission duration if direct flight were possible, but doesn’t reflect the actual path efficiency.
  • Total Distance Traveled (\(\mathbf{3.75\text{ km}}\)):
    • Crucial for battery consumption calculations and range estimation.
    • A drone flying \(3.75\text{ km}\) consumes significantly more energy than one flying a direct \(0.75\text{ km}\) displacement, impacting operational costs and flight endurance.

Warning

For autonomous systems, accurately distinguishing between displacement and distance traveled is paramount for precise navigation, optimal path planning, and efficient resource (e.g., energy) management.

Imagine if the drone’s navigation system only considered displacement. It might think it’s close to its target when it has actually done a lot of unnecessary flying, which would drain its battery much faster than anticipated. Distance traveled dictates the actual work done and energy expenditure.

Check Your Understanding: Robotic Rover

A robotic rover exploring Mars travels 3 km West from its landing site, then turns around and travels 2 km East.

(a) What is the rover’s total displacement? (b) What is the total distance traveled? (c) What is the magnitude of its displacement?

Conceptual timeline of rover movements.

This is a very common scenario in planetary exploration where a rover might make multiple movements to navigate around obstacles or to investigate different sites. Apply the principles we’ve just covered to analyze its motion. For consistency, let’s assume East is the positive direction for our calculations.

Solution to Check Your Understanding

Let East be positive (+x), so West is negative (-x).

  • First movement: \(\Delta x_1 = -3\text{ km}\) (3 km West)
  • Second movement: \(\Delta x_2 = +2\text{ km}\) (2 km East)

(a) Total Displacement (\(\Delta x_{\text{Total}}\)):

\(\Delta x_{\text{Total}} = \Delta x_1 + \Delta x_2 = (-3\text{ km}) + (+2\text{ km}) = -1\text{ km}\)

The rover’s total displacement is 1 km West from its landing site.

(b) Total Distance Traveled (\(x_{\text{Total}}\)):

\(x_{\text{Total}} = |\Delta x_1| + |\Delta x_2| = |-3\text{ km}| + |+2\text{ km}| = 3\text{ km} + 2\text{ km} = 5\text{ km}\)

The total distance traveled is 5 km.

(c) Magnitude of Displacement:

\(| \Delta x_{\text{Total}} | = |-1\text{ km}| = 1\text{ km}\)

The magnitude of its displacement is 1 km.

This problem clearly highlights the critical distinction between displacement and distance. While the rover is effectively 1 km west of its starting point, its internal odometers would show 5 km traveled. This ‘travel distance’ directly impacts maintenance schedules, energy consumption, and the overall longevity of the rover’s mission.

Key Takeaways

1. Position is relative: Always defined with respect to a chosen frame of reference.

2. Displacement (\(\Delta x\)): - A vector quantity representing the net change in position from start to finish. - Calculated as: \(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\) - Can be positive, negative, or zero.

3. Distance Traveled (\(x_{\text{Total}}\)): - A scalar quantity representing the total length of the actual path taken. - Calculated as: \(x_{\text{Total}} = \sum |\Delta x_i|\) - Always positive.

4. Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)): - A vector quantity representing the rate of displacement over time. - Calculated as: \(\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\) - Can be positive, negative, or zero. - Distinct from average speed, which uses total distance.

5. Engineering Relevance: These concepts are foundational for: - Navigation systems (e.g., GPS, autonomous vehicles) - Trajectory planning and control (e.g., rockets, robotics) - Performance analysis of vehicles and machines - Resource management (e.g., fuel/energy consumption, wear-and-tear)

Important

Always pay attention to both magnitude and direction when dealing with vector quantities like displacement and velocity! This distinction is crucial for accurate engineering analysis.

These five points are the core knowledge you should retain from this lecture. Mastering them will enable you to effectively analyze and design systems involving motion, which is a cornerstone of many engineering disciplines. Thank you!
