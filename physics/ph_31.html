Position, Displacement, and Average Velocity

An Engineering Physics Perspective

Welcome to this session on kinematics, focusing on position, displacement, and average velocity. These fundamental concepts are crucial for understanding the motion of objects in engineering applications.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Define position, displacement, and distance traveled.
  • Calculate the total displacement given the position as a function of time.
  • Determine the total distance traveled.
  • Calculate the average velocity given the displacement and elapsed time.

These objectives will guide our discussion today. We’ll break down each concept with clear definitions, examples, and problem-solving strategies.

Describing Motion: Position

To describe an object’s motion, we first define its position (x).

  • Position: Where an object is located at any particular time.
  • Frame of Reference: An arbitrary set of axes from which position and motion are described.
    • Often Earth-based for stationary objects.
    • Can be moving, e.g., an airplane for a passenger.
  • For one-dimensional motion, x (or y for vertical) is used.

Figure 3.2: Cyclists’ position can be described relative to buildings or a canal.

Imagine a rocket launch. We describe its position relative to Earth. But for a person inside an airplane, we describe their position relative to the plane itself. The choice of frame of reference is critical for clear analysis.

Introducing Displacement

When an object moves, its position changes. This change is called displacement.

  • Displacement: The change in position of an object.
  • It implies movement and indicates both magnitude and direction.
  • Displacement is a vector quantity.
    • Can be positive or negative depending on the chosen positive direction.

Figure 3.3: A professor’s displacement from an initial position (\(x_0\)) to a final position (\(x_f\)).

Think of displacement as the straight-line distance and direction from a starting point to an ending point, regardless of the path taken. If you walk 2 meters right, then 4 meters left, your displacement is 2 meters to the left, not 6 meters total.

Displacement: Calculation and Total

Displacement \(\Delta x\) is defined as the change in position:

\[ \Delta x = x_f - x_0 \]

  • Where:
    • \(\Delta x\) is displacement.
    • \(x_f\) is the final position.
    • \(x_0\) is the initial position.

Tip

The uppercase Greek letter delta (\(\Delta\)) always means “change in” the quantity that follows it.

  • SI Unit for Displacement: Meter (m).
  • Total Displacement (\(\Delta x_{Total}\)): The sum of individual displacements. \[ \Delta x_{Total} = \sum \Delta x_i \]

Always remember that displacement is a vector, meaning its sign is crucial. If we define “right” as positive, then moving left means a negative displacement. The SI unit for displacement is meters, and it’s good practice to convert to meters for consistency in calculations.

Displacement vs. Distance Traveled

These terms are often confused, but they are distinct.

Displacement

  • Definition: Change in position (\(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\)).
  • Nature: Vector quantity (has magnitude and direction).
  • Value: Can be positive, negative, or zero.
  • Example: If you walk 5m right and 5m left, your displacement is 0m.
  • Magnitude of Displacement: The absolute value of the displacement (\(|\Delta x|\)). Always positive.

Distance Traveled

  • Definition: The total length of the path traveled.
  • Nature: Scalar quantity (only has magnitude).
  • Value: Always positive or zero.
  • Example: If you walk 5m right and 5m left, your distance traveled is 10m.
  • Calculation: Sum of the magnitudes of individual displacements: \[ x_{Total} = \sum |\Delta x_i| \]

This distinction is fundamental. Displacement only cares about the start and end points, while distance traveled accounts for every step taken. In engineering, understanding both is important for tasks like path planning or energy consumption analysis.

Introducing Average Velocity

To fully describe motion, we must also consider time.

  • The time variable allows us to quantify how fast an object is moving.
  • Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)): The rate at which position changes over a period of time.
  • It is a vector quantity.
    • Can be positive or negative.
  • Elapsed Time (\(\Delta t\)): The time taken to travel between two points.

Note

Average velocity describes the overall rate of change, not necessarily the speed at any given instant.

If an object changes position, it took some time to do so. Average velocity combines these two concepts: displacement and elapsed time. Think of it as the average speed and direction you would have needed to maintain to cover the displacement in the given time.

Average Velocity: The Formula

The average velocity \(\bar{v}\) is defined as the total displacement between two points divided by the elapsed time.

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{\text{Displacement between two points}}{\text{Time needed to make the displacement}} \]

Or, more formally:

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_f - x_0}{t_f - t_0} \]

  • Where:
    • \(\bar{v}\) is the average velocity.
    • \(\Delta x\) is the total displacement.
    • \(\Delta t\) is the elapsed time (\(t_f - t_0\)).
  • SI Unit for Average Velocity: Meters per second (m/s).

The units are consistent: meters for displacement, seconds for time, giving meters per second for velocity. This formula is a cornerstone of kinematics, allowing us to quantify how quickly an object is moving in a particular direction over an interval.

Problem: Delivering Flyers

Let’s apply these concepts to a real-world scenario.

Jill’s Trip: Jill sets out from home (x=0).

  1. Leg 1: Travels 0.5 km East in 9 min.
  2. Leg 2: Realizes no more flyers, returns home (0 km) in 9 min.
  3. Leg 3: Gets more flyers, goes 1.0 km East (from home) in 15 min.
  4. Leg 4: Turns West, travels 1.75 km in 25 min, then stops to rest.

This problem describes a multi-segment journey. We’ll need to carefully track Jill’s position and time for each leg to answer the questions. Visualizing this on a number line can be very helpful.

Delivering Flyers: The Questions

Let’s analyze Jill’s journey.

  1. What is Jill’s total displacement to the point where she stops to rest?
  2. What is the magnitude of the final displacement?
  3. What is the average velocity during her entire trip?
  4. What is the total distance traveled?
  5. Make a graph of position versus time.

Figure 3.4: Timeline of Jill’s movements. We define East as the positive direction.

These questions will test our understanding of position, displacement, average velocity, and total distance. Notice that Jill changes direction, which is important for displacement calculations.

Solution Strategy: Jill’s Trip

To tackle this problem effectively, we’ll organize the information.

  • Assume East is the positive direction.
  • Jill’s home is the starting point (\(x_0 = 0\)).
  • Create a table to track time, position, and individual displacements for each leg of the journey.

Tip

Organizing given information into a table is a highly effective problem-solving strategy in physics and engineering.

This systematic approach helps prevent errors and ensures all data points are considered correctly. The table will allow us to easily sum displacements and total time.

Solution: Jill’s Trip - Data Table

Here’s the compiled data for Jill’s trip:

Time \(t_i\) (min) Position \(x_i\) (km) Displacement \(\Delta x_i\) (km)
\(t_0 = 0\) \(x_0 = 0\) \(\Delta x_0 = 0\)
\(t_1 = 9\) \(x_1 = 0.5\) \(\Delta x_1 = x_1 - x_0 = 0.5\)
\(t_2 = 18\) \(x_2 = 0\) \(\Delta x_2 = x_2 - x_1 = -0.5\)
\(t_3 = 33\) \(x_3 = 1.0\) \(\Delta x_3 = x_3 - x_2 = 1.0\)
\(t_4 = 58\) \(x_4 = -0.75\) \(\Delta x_4 = x_4 - x_3 = -1.75\)

Let’s trace these values. Jill starts at 0. At 9 min, she’s at 0.5 km. Then she returns to 0 at 18 min, so her displacement for that leg is -0.5 km. She then goes to 1.0 km from home at 33 min. Finally, she moves 1.75 km west from that 1.0 km mark, placing her at -0.75 km.

Problem-Solving: Jill’s Trip (Q1 & Q2)

1. What is Jill’s total displacement?

  • Sum of individual displacements: \[ \sum \Delta x_i = 0.5 \text{ km} - 0.5 \text{ km} + 1.0 \text{ km} - 1.75 \text{ km} = -0.75 \text{ km} \]
  • Answer: Jill’s total displacement is -0.75 km (0.75 km West of home).

2. What is the magnitude of the final displacement?

  • Magnitude is the absolute value of the total displacement: \[ |-0.75 \text{ km}| = 0.75 \text{ km} \]
  • Answer: The magnitude of her final displacement is 0.75 km.

The negative sign for total displacement indicates direction: West, since we defined East as positive. The magnitude simply tells us “how far” from the start point, irrespective of direction.

Problem-Solving: Jill’s Trip (Q3 & Q4)

3. What is the average velocity during her entire trip?

  • Total displacement = -0.75 km
  • Total elapsed time = 58 min
  • Convert time to hours or seconds for standard units if needed, but for km/min: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x_{Total}}{\Delta t_{Total}} = \frac{-0.75 \text{ km}}{58 \text{ min}} \approx -0.013 \text{ km/min} \]
  • Answer: The average velocity is -0.013 km/min (0.013 km/min West).

4. What is the total distance traveled?

  • Sum of the magnitudes of individual displacements: \[ x_{Total} = |0.5| + |-0.5| + |1.0| + |-1.75| \text{ km} = 0.5 + 0.5 + 1.0 + 1.75 \text{ km} = 3.75 \text{ km} \]
  • Answer: The total distance traveled is 3.75 km.

Notice the stark difference between displacement (-0.75 km) and total distance traveled (3.75 km). This highlights why both concepts are needed. The average velocity tells us the overall effective “speed” and “direction” if she had traveled in a straight line from start to finish.

Problem-Solving: Jill’s Trip (Q5 - Graph)

5. Make a graph of position versus time.

Figure 3.5: Jill’s position versus time. The average velocity is the slope of a line connecting the initial and final points.

This graph provides a visual representation of Jill’s motion. Each segment’s slope represents her instantaneous velocity during that time. The overall slope from the origin to the final point represents the average velocity. Engineers often use such graphs to analyze and visualize system dynamics.

Significance of Jill’s Trip Results

  • Total Displacement: -0.75 km.
    • Means Jill ended up 0.75 km West of her home.
  • Average Velocity: -0.013 km/min.
    • If someone walked steadily West at this rate, they’d reach the final point at the same time as Jill.
    • If Jill ended at home, her total displacement and average velocity would be zero.
  • Total Distance Traveled: 3.75 km.
    • This is the actual path length Jill covered.

Important

Displacement and average velocity are path-independent (only depend on start/end points), while total distance traveled is path-dependent.

This summary reinforces the core differences. In engineering, displacement is often critical for structural positioning or robotic navigation, while total distance traveled might be important for fuel consumption or component wear.

Check Your Understanding

Problem: A cyclist rides 3 km west and then turns around and rides 2 km east.

Figure: Cyclist’s movement. Let’s define East as positive.

  1. What is their displacement?
  2. What is the distance traveled?
  3. What is the magnitude of their displacement?

Take a moment to solve this yourself. Think about the definitions of displacement and distance, and remember to assign a positive and negative direction.

Check Your Understanding - Solution (Displacement)

Let’s define East as the positive direction (+) and West as the negative direction (-).

Given: - First leg: 3 km West (\(\Delta x_1 = -3 \text{ km}\)) - Second leg: 2 km East (\(\Delta x_2 = +2 \text{ km}\))

(a) What is their displacement?

  • Total displacement \(\Delta x_{Total} = \Delta x_1 + \Delta x_2\)
  • \(\Delta x_{Total} = -3 \text{ km} + 2 \text{ km} = -1 \text{ km}\)

Answer: The cyclist’s displacement is -1 km (or 1 km West).

(c) What is the magnitude of their displacement?

  • Magnitude of displacement \(= |\Delta x_{Total}|\)
  • Magnitude \(= |-1 \text{ km}| = 1 \text{ km}\)

Answer: The magnitude of their displacement is 1 km.

The negative sign in the displacement signifies the direction (West). The magnitude tells us simply “how far” the cyclist is from their starting point in a straight line.

Check Your Understanding - Solution (Distance)

Recall: - First leg: 3 km West - Second leg: 2 km East

(b) What is the distance traveled?

  • Distance traveled is the sum of the magnitudes of individual paths: \[ x_{Total} = |\Delta x_1| + |\Delta x_2| \]
  • \(x_{Total} = |{-3 \text{ km}}| + |{+2 \text{ km}}| = 3 \text{ km} + 2 \text{ km} = 5 \text{ km}\)

Answer: The total distance traveled is 5 km.

Notice again the difference: 1 km net displacement vs. 5 km actual distance covered. This problem is a classic way to ensure you understand the core difference between these two fundamental kinematic quantities.

Key Takeaways

  • Position is an object’s location relative to a chosen frame of reference.
  • Displacement (\(\Delta x\)) is the change in position (\(x_f - x_0\)). It is a vector (magnitude and direction).
  • Total Displacement is the vector sum of individual displacements.
  • Distance Traveled is the total length of the path taken. It is a scalar (magnitude only).
  • The magnitude of displacement is the absolute value of displacement.
  • Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)) is the rate of change of position, calculated as displacement over elapsed time (\(\Delta x / \Delta t\)). It is a vector.
  • Understanding the distinction between displacement and distance, and velocity and speed, is crucial for kinematics.

These are the most critical points to remember from today’s lecture. They form the foundation for more advanced topics in mechanics. Always consider whether a quantity is a vector or a scalar.

Key Equations

Here are the essential equations from this chapter:

Equation Description
\(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\) Defines displacement, change in position
\(\Delta x_{Total} = \sum \Delta x_i\) Calculates total displacement from individual ones
\(x_{Total} = \sum |\Delta x_i|\) Calculates total distance traveled
\(\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\) Defines average velocity
\(\bar{v} = \frac{x_f - x_0}{t_f - t_0}\) Expanded form for average velocity

These equations are your tools for solving kinematics problems. Practice using them until they become second nature. Pay close attention to the variables and their meanings.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Position (\(\boldsymbol{x}\)) The location of an object at a particular time relative to a chosen frame of reference.
Frame of Reference An arbitrary set of axes from which the position and motion of an object are described.
Displacement (\(\Delta x\)) The change in position of an object, indicating both magnitude and direction (a vector). \(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\).
Distance Traveled The total length of the path an object travels between two positions (a scalar).
Elapsed Time (\(\Delta t\)) The difference between the final time and the initial time.
Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)) The displacement divided by the elapsed time; a vector quantity representing the average rate of position change. \(\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\).

Mastering these definitions is just as important as knowing the equations. A solid understanding of terminology prevents confusion and ensures accurate problem interpretation.
