3.1 Position, Displacement, and Average Velocity

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Define position, displacement, and distance traveled.
  • Calculate the total displacement given the position as a function of time.
  • Determine the total distance traveled.
  • Calculate the average velocity given the displacement and elapsed time.

Welcome to our first topic in kinematics: Position, Displacement, and Average Velocity. Understanding these fundamental concepts is crucial for describing any kind of motion. We’ll define each term, learn how to calculate them, and see how they relate to each other.

Describing Motion: Position

To describe motion, we first need to define position (x).

  • Position tells us where an object is at a particular time.
  • It’s always specified relative to a convenient frame of reference.

Frame of Reference

  • An arbitrary set of axes from which position and motion are described.
  • Often Earth is used as a reference frame (e.g., buildings, landmarks).
  • Can also be a moving reference frame (e.g., inside an airplane).

Think of position as an address. You can’t just say “I’m here,” you need to say “I’m here relative to something.” For example, 5 meters to the right of the whiteboard. The choice of reference frame is important and can simplify the description of motion. For one-dimensional motion, we typically use the variable ‘x’ for horizontal motion and ‘y’ for vertical motion.

Displacement

Displacement (\(\Delta x\)) is the change in an object’s position.

  • It’s a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.
  • Can be positive or negative, depending on the chosen positive direction.

\[ \Delta x = x_f - x_0 \]

  • \(\Delta x\): displacement
  • \(x_f\): final position
  • \(x_0\): initial position

Note

The SI unit for displacement is meters (m).

Displacement is not just how far an object has moved, but also in what direction. If you walk 5 meters forward and then 3 meters backward, your displacement is 2 meters forward, not 8 meters. The Greek letter delta, \(\Delta\), always means “change in” a quantity, calculated as the final value minus the initial value.

Total Displacement

An object can have a series of displacements.

  • Total displacement (\(\Delta x_{Total}\)) is the sum of individual displacements.

\[ \Delta x_{Total} = \sum \Delta x_i \]

  • \(\Delta x_i\): individual displacements.

Example:

  1. A professor moves 2 m to the right (\(\Delta x_1 = +2\) m).
  2. Then moves 4 m to the left (\(\Delta x_2 = -4\) m).

Total displacement: \(\Delta x_{Total} = \Delta x_1 + \Delta x_2 = 2 \, \text{m} + (-4 \, \text{m}) = -2 \, \text{m}\)

Figure 3.3: Displacement of a professor.

The total displacement tells us the net change in position from the very beginning to the very end of the motion, regardless of the path taken in between. It only depends on the initial and final positions. The magnitude of displacement is always positive, representing the size of the displacement.

Distance Traveled

Distance traveled (\(x_{Total}\)) is different from displacement.

  • It is the total length of the path traveled between two positions.
  • It is a scalar quantity, always positive.

\[ x_{Total} = \sum |\Delta x_i| \]

  • \(|\Delta x_i|\): magnitude of individual displacements.

Example (continued):

  1. Professor moves 2 m to the right (\(\Delta x_1 = +2\) m).
  2. Then moves 4 m to the left (\(\Delta x_2 = -4\) m).

Total distance traveled: \(x_{Total} = |\Delta x_1| + |\Delta x_2| = |+2 \, \text{m}| + |-4 \, \text{m}| = 2 \, \text{m} + 4 \, \text{m} = 6 \, \text{m}\)

Important

Displacement focuses on the net change, while distance traveled accounts for every step of the journey.

So, for the professor, the total displacement was -2 meters, meaning she ended up 2 meters to the left of her starting point. But the total distance she walked was 6 meters. This distinction is crucial in physics.

Average Velocity

Average velocity (\(\bar{v}\)) is the rate at which position changes over time.

  • It’s a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.
  • Can be positive or negative, depending on the displacement.

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{\text{Displacement between two points}}{\text{Time needed to make the displacement}} \] \[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1} \]

  • \(\Delta t\): elapsed time (\(t_2 - t_1\)).

Note

The SI unit for average velocity is meters per second (m/s).

Average velocity tells us the overall speed and direction of motion over a period of time. It doesn’t tell us what happened at every instant, just the average rate of change. For example, if you travel 100 km in 2 hours, your average velocity is 50 km/h, even if you stopped for a coffee break or sped up at times.

Example: Delivering Flyers - Part 1

Jill delivers flyers. Let’s analyze her trip.

Figure 3.4: Timeline of Jill’s movements.

Trip Details:

  • Leg 1: 0.5 km East (9 min).
  • Leg 2: 0.5 km West (9 min) - Back to house.
  • Leg 3: 1.0 km East (15 min) - Continues where she left off.
  • Leg 4: 1.75 km West (25 min) - Stops to rest.

Strategy:

  • Define East as positive direction.
  • Create a table of positions and times.
  • Calculate individual displacements.

This example allows us to apply all the concepts we just discussed. It’s a multi-leg journey, which will help illustrate the difference between total displacement and total distance. Setting up a table is a good strategy to organize the data.

Example: Delivering Flyers - Part 2

Table of Jill’s Movements (East = positive):

Time \(t_i\) (min) Position \(x_i\) (km) Displacement \(\Delta x_i\) (km)
\(t_0 = 0\) \(x_0 = 0\) \(\Delta x_0 = 0\)
\(t_1 = 9\) \(x_1 = 0.5\) \(\Delta x_1 = x_1 - x_0 = 0.5\)
\(t_2 = 18\) \(x_2 = 0\) \(\Delta x_2 = x_2 - x_1 = -0.5\)
\(t_3 = 33\) \(x_3 = 1.0\) \(\Delta x_3 = x_3 - x_2 = 1.0\)
\(t_4 = 58\) \(x_4 = -0.75\) \(\Delta x_4 = x_4 - x_3 = -1.75\)

1. Total Displacement:

  • Sum of individual displacements: \(\sum \Delta x_i = 0.5 - 0.5 + 1.0 - 1.75 \, \text{km} = -0.75 \, \text{km}\) (Jill is 0.75 km West of her home)

2. Magnitude of Final Displacement:

  • \(|-0.75 \, \text{km}| = 0.75 \, \text{km}\)

The table helps us track Jill’s position at different times and calculate the displacement for each leg of her journey. Notice how the displacement for leg 2 is negative because she moved west, back towards her home. For total displacement, we simply add these signed values. The magnitude is just the positive value.

Example: Delivering Flyers - Part 3

3. Average Velocity:

  • Average velocity = \(\frac{\text{Total displacement}}{\text{Elapsed time}}\)
  • \(\bar{v} = \frac{-0.75 \, \text{km}}{58 \, \text{min}} \approx -0.013 \, \text{km/min}\) (This means an average of 0.013 km/min West).

4. Total Distance Traveled:

  • Sum of magnitudes of individual displacements: \(x_{Total} = \sum |\Delta x_i| = |0.5| + |-0.5| + |1.0| + |-1.75| \, \text{km} = 0.5 + 0.5 + 1.0 + 1.75 \, \text{km} = 3.75 \, \text{km}\)

The average velocity is directly linked to the total displacement. If Jill had ended up back at her house, her total displacement would be zero, and thus her average velocity would also be zero, regardless of how much distance she covered. In contrast, the total distance traveled sums up all the paths she took, regardless of direction.

Check Your Understanding

A cyclist rides 3 km west and then turns around and rides 2 km east.

Let’s assume East is the positive direction.

  1. What is their displacement?
  2. What is the distance traveled?
  3. What is the magnitude of their displacement?

Take a moment to work through this problem using the definitions and equations we’ve covered. Remember to consider direction for displacement, and the absolute value for distance.

Check Your Understanding - Solution

Let’s assume East is the positive direction.

  • Leg 1: 3 km west \(\implies \Delta x_1 = -3 \, \text{km}\)
  • Leg 2: 2 km east \(\implies \Delta x_2 = +2 \, \text{km}\)

(a) What is their displacement?

\(\Delta x_{Total} = \Delta x_1 + \Delta x_2 = -3 \, \text{km} + 2 \, \text{km} = -1 \, \text{km}\) (The cyclist is 1 km West of their starting point).

(b) What is the distance traveled?

\(x_{Total} = |\Delta x_1| + |\Delta x_2| = |-3 \, \text{km}| + |+2 \, \text{km}| = 3 \, \text{km} + 2 \, \text{km} = 5 \, \text{km}\)

(c) What is the magnitude of their displacement?

\(|\Delta x_{Total}| = |-1 \, \text{km}| = 1 \, \text{km}\)

Notice how displacement is negative because the final position is to the west of the start, while distance traveled is a larger, positive value because it sums the total path length. The magnitude of displacement is simply the positive value of the total displacement.

Key Takeaways

  • Position (x): Where an object is relative to a reference frame.
  • Displacement (\(\Delta x\)): The change in position, a vector quantity (\(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\)).
  • Total Displacement (\(\Delta x_{Total}\)): The sum of individual displacements (\(\sum \Delta x_i\)).
  • Distance Traveled (\(x_{Total}\)): The total length of the path covered, a scalar quantity (\(\sum |\Delta x_i|\)).
  • Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)): The total displacement divided by the elapsed time (\(\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\)), a vector quantity.

These five points are the core concepts from this section. Ensure you can distinguish between displacement and distance, and understand why velocity is a vector while distance is a scalar.

Key Equations

Equation Description
\(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\) Displacement
\(\Delta x_{Total} = \sum \Delta x_i\) Total displacement
\(x_{Total} = \sum |\Delta x_i|\) Total distance traveled
\(\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{t_2 - t_1}\) Average velocity

These are the essential formulas you’ll need for calculations involving position, displacement, and average velocity. Pay close attention to the definitions of each variable.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Position (x) The location of an object at a specific time relative to a reference frame.
Frame of Reference An arbitrary set of axes from which the position and motion of an object are described.
Displacement (\(\Delta x\)) The change in position of an object, a vector quantity. (\(x_f - x_0\))
Total Displacement (\(\Delta x_{Total}\)) The sum of individual displacements, representing the net change in position.
Distance Traveled (\(x_{Total}\)) The total length of the path covered by an object during its motion, a scalar quantity.
Average Velocity (\(\bar{v}\)) The total displacement divided by the elapsed time, a vector quantity.
Elapsed Time (\(\Delta t\)) The time taken to travel between two points.

Familiarize yourself with these terms and their precise definitions. A clear understanding of this vocabulary is fundamental for success in kinematics.
