3.3 Average and Instantaneous Acceleration

The Rate of Change of Velocity

Today, we’re building on our understanding of position and velocity by introducing acceleration. This concept is vital for predicting how motion changes, from everyday scenarios to complex engineering problems.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Calculate the average acceleration between two points in time.
  • Calculate the instantaneous acceleration given the functional form of velocity.
  • Explain the vector nature of instantaneous acceleration and velocity.
  • Explain the difference between average acceleration and instantaneous acceleration.
  • Find instantaneous acceleration at a specified time on a graph of velocity versus time.

These objectives will guide our discussion. We’ll start with average acceleration, then move to the more precise instantaneous acceleration using calculus.

Understanding Acceleration

  • In everyday terms, accelerate means to speed up; applying brakes means to slow down.
  • In physics, acceleration is more inclusive: it’s about the change in velocity over time.
  • A greater acceleration implies a greater change in velocity over a given time.
  • Examples:
    • Car acceleration/braking.
    • Subatomic particles in accelerators.
    • Cosmic rays accelerated by supernovas.

Acceleration is not just about speeding up. It’s about any change in velocity, which, as we’ll see, includes changes in direction even if speed remains constant. This broad definition is crucial in physics and engineering.

Average Acceleration

Average acceleration is defined as the rate at which velocity changes.

\[ \bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_0}{t_f - t_0} \]

  • Where:
    • \(\bar{a}\) is average acceleration.
    • \(v_f\) is final velocity.
    • \(v_0\) is initial velocity.
    • \(\Delta t\) is elapsed time.
  • SI Units for Acceleration: meters per second squared (m/s\(^2\)).
    • This means “how many meters per second the velocity changes, each second.”

Recall that velocity is a vector. Therefore, a change in velocity can be a change in magnitude (speed), a change in direction, or both. Acceleration accounts for all these changes.

Acceleration as a Vector

  • Acceleration is a vector in the same direction as the change in velocity (\(\Delta v\)).
  • It can occur due to:
    1. Change in speed (increase or decrease).
    2. Change in direction.
    3. Both.

Caution

The term “deceleration” is often used but can be misleading in physics. We prefer to use “negative acceleration” or specify that “acceleration is opposite to the direction of motion.”

Figure 3.10: A subway train braking (accelerating opposite to motion).

If a train is moving East and slowing down, its acceleration is West. We would say it has negative acceleration if East is defined as positive. This is conceptually clearer than “deceleration.”

Acceleration and Direction of Motion

  • Acceleration is not always in the direction of motion.
  • When an object slows down, its acceleration is opposite to the direction of its motion.

Figure 3.11: An object with positive velocity and negative acceleration will eventually stop and reverse direction.

This figure illustrates a common scenario. If an object moving East (positive velocity) experiences a constant acceleration West (negative acceleration), it will slow down, momentarily stop, and then reverse direction, moving West with increasing speed. This phenomenon is critical in understanding projectile motion or braking systems.

Problem: Calculating Average Acceleration

A racehorse coming out of the gate accelerates from rest to a velocity of \(15.0 \text{ m/s}\) due west in \(1.80 \text{ s}\).

What is its average acceleration?

Figure 3.12: Racehorses accelerating out of the gate.

This is a straightforward application of the average acceleration formula. Remember to assign a coordinate system and be consistent with signs.

Strategy: Racehorse Acceleration

  1. Draw a sketch and assign a coordinate system.
    • Let’s define East as positive (+) and West as negative (-).
    • This means a velocity of 15.0 m/s due West is \(v_f = -15.0 \text{ m/s}\).

Figure 3.13: Coordinate system for the racehorse problem.

  1. Identify knowns:
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0\)): From rest, so \(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\).
    • Final velocity (\(v_f\)): \(15.0 \text{ m/s}\) due West, so \(v_f = -15.0 \text{ m/s}\).
    • Time interval (\(\Delta t\)): \(1.80 \text{ s}\).
  2. Use the average acceleration formula: \(\bar{a} = \frac{v_f - v_0}{\Delta t}\).

Setting up the problem correctly with a clear coordinate system is half the battle. The negative sign for velocity due to “West” is a critical detail.

Solution: Racehorse Acceleration

Calculations:

  • Change in velocity (\(\Delta v\)): \[ \Delta v = v_f - v_0 = -15.0 \text{ m/s} - 0 \text{ m/s} = -15.0 \text{ m/s} \]
  • Average acceleration (\(\bar{a}\)): \[ \bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{-15.0 \text{ m/s}}{1.80 \text{ s}} \approx -8.33 \text{ m/s}^2 \]

Answer: The average acceleration of the racehorse is \(-8.33 \text{ m/s}^2\).

Significance: - The negative sign indicates that the acceleration is due West (in the direction of the final velocity, as the horse is speeding up in that direction). - An acceleration of \(8.33 \text{ m/s}^2\) due West means the horse’s velocity changes by \(8.33 \text{ m/s}\) due West every second.

This is a significant acceleration! For context, free-fall acceleration near Earth’s surface is about 9.8 m/s^2. A jockey would experience substantial forces.

Problem: Protons in a linear accelerator are accelerated from rest to \(2.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s}\) in \(10^{-4} \text{ s}\).

What is the average acceleration of the protons?

Apply the same principles as the previous example. Identify the initial velocity, final velocity, and time interval.

Check Your Understanding 3.3 - Solution

Given: - Initial velocity (\(v_0\)): From rest, so \(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\). - Final velocity (\(v_f\)): \(2.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s}\). - Time interval (\(\Delta t\)): \(10^{-4} \text{ s}\).

Calculation of average acceleration (\(\bar{a}\)): \[ \bar{a} = \frac{v_f - v_0}{\Delta t} \] \[ \bar{a} = \frac{(2.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s}) - 0 \text{ m/s}}{10^{-4} \text{ s}} \] \[ \bar{a} = \frac{2.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s}}{10^{-4} \text{ s}} = 2.0 \times 10^{11} \text{ m/s}^2 \]

Answer: The average acceleration of the protons is \(2.0 \times 10^{11} \text{ m/s}^2\).

This is an incredibly large acceleration, typical for subatomic particles in particle accelerators, showcasing the extreme scales encountered in physics. The direction of acceleration would be in the direction of the final velocity, as they started from rest and sped up.

Instantaneous Acceleration

Instantaneous acceleration (\(a\)) is the acceleration at a specific instant in time.

  • It is obtained by taking the limit of average acceleration as the time interval (\(\Delta t\)) approaches zero.
  • Mathematically, instantaneous acceleration is the derivative of the velocity function (\(v(t)\)) with respect to time: \[ a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt} \]
  • Since \(v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt}\), it also means: \[ a(t) = \frac{d^2x(t)}{dt^2} \] (The second derivative of position with respect to time).

This directly extends our calculus discussion from velocity. Just as velocity is the slope of the position-time graph, acceleration is the slope of the velocity-time graph.

Instantaneous Acceleration: Graphical Interpretation

  • On a velocity-versus-time (v(t) vs t) graph, the instantaneous acceleration at any time \(t_0\) is the slope of the tangent line at that point.

Figure 3.14: Instantaneous acceleration is the slope of the tangent line on a v-t graph.

  • When the velocity function has a maximum or minimum, the slope (and thus instantaneous acceleration) is zero.

Figure 3.14 (a) shows acceleration is zero at the peak velocity. Figure 3.14 (b) shows acceleration is zero at the minimum velocity. These points are significant because they indicate moments when the rate of change of velocity momentarily stops.

Problem: Calculating Instantaneous Acceleration (Example 3.6)

A particle’s velocity is given by: \[ v(t) = (20 \text{ m/s}^2)t - (5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^2 \]

  1. Find the functional form of the acceleration.
  2. Find the instantaneous velocity at \(t = 1, 2, 3, \text{ and } 5 \text{ s}\).
  3. Find the instantaneous acceleration at \(t = 1, 2, 3, \text{ and } 5 \text{ s}\).
  4. Interpret the results of (c) in terms of the directions of the acceleration and velocity vectors.

This problem requires applying differentiation twice: once for velocity from position (implicitly, if needed), and once for acceleration from velocity. Then, interpreting the signs of velocity and acceleration together.

Solution: Calculating Instantaneous Acceleration (Part 1 & 2)

1. Functional form of acceleration: - Differentiate \(v(t)\) with respect to \(t\): \[ a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} \left[ (20 \text{ m/s}^2)t - (5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^2 \right] \] \[ a(t) = (20 \text{ m/s}^2) \cdot 1 \cdot t^{1-1} - (5 \text{ m/s}^3) \cdot 2 \cdot t^{2-1} \] \[ a(t) = (20 \text{ m/s}^2) - (10 \text{ m/s}^3)t \]

Answer (1): \(a(t) = (20 \text{ m/s}^2) - (10 \text{ m/s}^3)t\).

2. Instantaneous velocity at \(t = 1, 2, 3, \text{ and } 5 \text{ s}\): - Using \(v(t) = (20 \text{ m/s}^2)t - (5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^2\): - \(v(1 \text{ s}) = 20(1) - 5(1)^2 = 15 \text{ m/s}\) - \(v(2 \text{ s}) = 20(2) - 5(2)^2 = 40 - 20 = 20 \text{ m/s}\) - \(v(3 \text{ s}) = 20(3) - 5(3)^2 = 60 - 45 = 15 \text{ m/s}\) - \(v(5 \text{ s}) = 20(5) - 5(5)^2 = 100 - 125 = -25 \text{ m/s}\)

Answer (2): \(v(1 \text{ s}) = 15 \text{ m/s}\), \(v(2 \text{ s}) = 20 \text{ m/s}\), \(v(3 \text{ s}) = 15 \text{ m/s}\), \(v(5 \text{ s}) = -25 \text{ m/s}\).

Pay attention to the units throughout the calculation. For velocity, at each time point, the calculation leads to units of m/s. The coefficients in the v(t) function are not dimensionless.

Solution: Calculating Instantaneous Acceleration (Part 3 & 4)

3. Instantaneous acceleration at \(t = 1, 2, 3, \text{ and } 5 \text{ s}\): - Using \(a(t) = (20 \text{ m/s}^2) - (10 \text{ m/s}^3)t\): - \(a(1 \text{ s}) = 20 - 10(1) = 10 \text{ m/s}^2\) - \(a(2 \text{ s}) = 20 - 10(2) = 0 \text{ m/s}^2\) - \(a(3 \text{ s}) = 20 - 10(3) = -10 \text{ m/s}^2\) - \(a(5 \text{ s}) = 20 - 10(5) = -30 \text{ m/s}^2\)

Answer (3): \(a(1 \text{ s}) = 10 \text{ m/s}^2\), \(a(2 \text{ s}) = 0 \text{ m/s}^2\), \(a(3 \text{ s}) = -10 \text{ m/s}^2\), \(a(5 \text{ s}) = -30 \text{ m/s}^2\).

4. Interpretation of results:

  • At \(t = 1 \text{ s}\): \(v = 15 \text{ m/s}\) (positive), \(a = 10 \text{ m/s}^2\) (positive). Velocity and acceleration are in the same direction. Particle is speeding up.
  • At \(t = 2 \text{ s}\): \(v = 20 \text{ m/s}\) (positive), \(a = 0 \text{ m/s}^2\). Velocity is momentarily at its maximum; acceleration is zero.
  • At \(t = 3 \text{ s}\): \(v = 15 \text{ m/s}\) (positive), \(a = -10 \text{ m/s}^2\) (negative). Velocity and acceleration are in opposite directions. Particle is slowing down.
  • At \(t = 5 \text{ s}\): \(v = -25 \text{ m/s}\) (negative), \(a = -30 \text{ m/s}^2\) (negative). Velocity and acceleration are in the same direction (both negative). Particle has reversed direction and is speeding up in the negative direction.

The interpretation of the signs of velocity and acceleration is paramount. If they have the same sign, the object is speeding up. If they have opposite signs, the object is slowing down. When acceleration is zero, velocity is at an extremum (max or min).

Check Your Understanding 3.4

Problem: An airplane lands on a runway traveling east. Describe its acceleration.

Think about what happens to the airplane’s velocity as it lands. Which direction is its initial velocity, and how does that velocity change?

Check Your Understanding 3.4 - Solution

Answer:

  1. Direction of Motion: The airplane is traveling East. Let’s designate East as the positive (+) direction. Therefore, its initial velocity is positive.
  2. Change in Velocity: When an airplane lands, it is slowing down. Its speed decreases, eventually reaching zero.
  3. Direction of Acceleration: For the airplane to slow down while moving East (positive velocity), its acceleration must be in the opposite direction of its motion.

Therefore, the airplane’s acceleration is West (negative direction).

Even though the plane is “decelerating,” we explicitly state its acceleration is in the negative direction (West) because that is the direction of the change in velocity.

Getting a Feel for Acceleration

  • Accelerations vary widely, from continental drift (\(10^{-7} \text{ m/s}^2\)) to proton acceleration in a collider (\(1.9 \times 10^9 \text{ m/s}^2\)).
  • Acceleration magnitude is independent of object size or mass (in kinematics).
  • Average acceleration can be very different from instantaneous acceleration, especially when motion is not smooth.

Key Takeaways

  • Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. It is a vector quantity.
  • Average Acceleration (\(\bar{a} = \Delta v / \Delta t\)) describes the overall change in velocity over a time interval.
  • Instantaneous Acceleration (\(a(t) = dv(t)/dt = d^2x(t)/dt^2\)) describes the acceleration at a specific moment.
  • On a velocity-time graph, instantaneous acceleration is the slope of the tangent line.
  • Acceleration occurs when velocity changes magnitude, direction, or both.
  • If velocity and acceleration have the same sign, the object is speeding up.
  • If velocity and acceleration have opposite signs, the object is slowing down.
  • The term “deceleration” is avoided in favor of precise vector description.

These key takeaways summarize the core concepts of acceleration. Remember the vector nature of acceleration and its relationship to both position and velocity through derivatives.

Key Equations

Here are the essential equations for acceleration:

Equation Description
\(\bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_0}{t_f - t_0}\) Defines average acceleration
\(a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt}\) Defines instantaneous acceleration as derivative of velocity
\(a(t) = \frac{d^2x(t)}{dt^2}\) Instantaneous acceleration as second derivative of position
\(\frac{d(At^n)}{dt} = An \cdot t^{n-1}\) Power rule for differentiation (used repeatedly)

These equations are your tools for quantitative analysis of acceleration. Be comfortable applying differentiation to velocity functions.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Acceleration The rate of change of velocity; a vector quantity with units of m/s\(^2\).
Average Acceleration The change in velocity divided by the elapsed time; \(\bar{a} = \Delta v / \Delta t\).
Instantaneous Acceleration The acceleration of an object at a specific point in time; the derivative of the velocity function with respect to time (\(a(t) = dv(t)/dt\)).
Second Derivative The derivative of a derivative; related to instantaneous acceleration (\(a(t) = d^2x(t)/dt^2\)).

Understanding these terms is fundamental to describing and predicting the motion of objects in engineering physics.
