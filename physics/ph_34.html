Motion with Constant Acceleration

Good morning/afternoon everyone. Today, we’re building on our understanding of velocity and acceleration to analyze a very common and important type of motion: motion with constant acceleration. This is a foundational topic for many engineering applications. We will explore the set of equations that govern this type of motion and learn how to apply them to solve various problems, including those involving multiple objects.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Identify which equations of motion are to be used to solve for unknowns.
  • Use appropriate kinematic equations to solve problems involving single-body motion.
  • Apply kinematic equations to solve two-body pursuit problems.

Our main goals today are to understand and correctly apply the kinematic equations for constant acceleration. This involves recognizing the type of problem, identifying the knowns and unknowns, and then selecting the most efficient equations. We’ll start with single objects and then move to more complex scenarios where two objects interact.

Simplified Notation & Constant Acceleration

To simplify our analysis, we make two key assumptions:

  1. Initial Time is Zero (\(t_0 = 0\))
    • Elapsed time: \(\Delta t = t_f - t_0 \rightarrow t\)
    • Initial position: \(x_0\)
    • Initial velocity: \(v_0\)
    • Final position: \(x\)
    • Final velocity: \(v\)
  2. Acceleration is Constant (\(a = \text{constant}\))
    • This is a crucial assumption for all equations we’ll derive.
    • Average acceleration (\(\bar{a}\)) is equal to instantaneous acceleration (\(a\)).

Important

Why constant acceleration? Many real-world situations have constant acceleration (e.g., free fall, controlled braking). Complex motions can often be broken into segments of constant acceleration.

The first simplification is purely for convenience, allowing us to drop subscripts for final values. The second, however, is fundamental to the kinematic equations we’re about to introduce. If acceleration is constant, it means the rate of change of velocity is steady. This allows us to use simple algebraic equations rather than calculus for solving problems. Many engineering scenarios, like a car braking or an object falling, can be modeled accurately with constant acceleration, at least over certain intervals.

Displacement and Velocity Relationship

Starting from the definition of average velocity:

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \]

Using simplified notation:

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{x - x_0}{t} \]

Solving for final position \(x\):

\[ x = x_0 + \bar{v}t \]

Note

For Constant Acceleration: The average velocity is simply the average of initial and final velocities: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \]

Figure 3.18 (a) illustrates that for constant acceleration, \(\bar{v}\) is the average of \(v_0\) and \(v\).

This slide lays the groundwork for our first set of kinematic equations. The definition of average velocity is always true. However, the ability to simply average the initial and final velocities (v0+v)/2 is only valid when acceleration is constant. The graph helps visualize this: with constant acceleration, the velocity-time graph is a straight line, and the average height of that line is just the midpoint between the initial and final heights.

Kinematic Equation 1: Velocity-Time Relation

Deriving from the definition of acceleration:

\[ a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]

Using simplified notation (and constant \(a\)):

\[ a = \frac{v - v_0}{t} \]

Solving for final velocity \(v\):

\[ \mathbf{v = v_0 + at} \quad (\text{constant } a) \]

Tip

Key Insights:

  • Final velocity depends on initial velocity, acceleration, and time duration.
  • If \(a = 0\), then \(v = v_0\) (constant velocity).
  • If \(a\) is opposite to \(v_0\), the object slows down.

This is perhaps the most intuitive of the kinematic equations. It tells us directly how much our velocity changes if we accelerate at a constant rate for a certain amount of time. If you apply a constant force to an object (and therefore a constant acceleration), its velocity will change linearly with time. This equation is often used as a starting point for other derivations.

Example 3.7: Airplane Braking

An airplane lands with an initial velocity of \(70.0 \text{ m/s}\) and then accelerates opposite to the motion at \(1.50 \text{ m/s}^2\) for \(40.0 \text{ s}\).

What is its final velocity?

Strategy:

  1. Identify Knowns: \(v_0 = 70.0 \text{ m/s}\) (initial velocity) \(a = -1.50 \text{ m/s}^2\) (acceleration, negative because opposite to motion) \(t = 40.0 \text{ s}\) (time)
  2. Identify Unknown: \(v\) (final velocity)
  3. Choose Equation: The equation \(v = v_0 + at\) directly relates these quantities.

Figure 3.19: Acceleration is negative because its direction is opposite to velocity.

This problem is a direct application of the first kinematic equation. The crucial step here is correctly assigning the sign to acceleration. Since the airplane is slowing down while moving forward (which we’ll assume is the positive direction), its acceleration must be in the negative direction. This is a common point of error for students, so emphasize it.

Solution: Airplane Braking

Calculation:

Given:

\(v_0 = 70.0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(a = -1.50 \text{ m/s}^2\)

\(t = 40.0 \text{ s}\)

Using \(v = v_0 + at\):

\[ v = 70.0 \text{ m/s} + (-1.50 \text{ m/s}^2)(40.0 \text{ s}) \] \[ v = 70.0 \text{ m/s} - 60.0 \text{ m/s} \] \[ \mathbf{v = 10.0 \text{ m/s}} \]

Significance:

  • The final velocity is positive, meaning the airplane is still moving in the original (positive) direction.
  • It has significantly slowed down, as expected from the negative acceleration.
  • This calculation is vital for runway length requirements and braking system design.

The final velocity of 10 m/s is positive, indicating the plane is still moving forward, but much slower. This result makes sense and can be validated by our intuition. Engineers use these types of calculations to determine safe landing distances, design braking systems, and assess passenger comfort during deceleration.

Kinematic Equation 2: Position-Time Relation

We can combine:

  1. Average velocity \(\bar{v} = v_0 + \frac{1}{2}at\) (derived from \(v = v_0 + at\) and \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0+v}{2}\))
  2. Displacement \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\)

Substitute (1) into (2):

\[ x = x_0 + \left( v_0 + \frac{1}{2}at \right)t \]

\[ \mathbf{x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2} \quad (\text{constant } a) \]

Tip

Key Insights: - Displacement depends on the square of time when acceleration is present. - If \(a = 0\), then \(x = x_0 + v_0t\) (linear displacement for constant velocity).

This equation is very powerful as it directly relates position to time, initial velocity, and constant acceleration. The term 1/2at^2 shows that acceleration causes displacement to grow quadratically with time, meaning the distance covered increases rapidly as time passes when an object is accelerating. This is why a car needs significantly more distance to stop if its speed is doubled.

Example 3.8: Dragster Displacement

A dragster accelerates from rest at \(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\) for \(5.56 \text{ s}\).

How far does it travel in this time?

Strategy:

  1. Identify Knowns:

    \(x_0 = 0 \text{ m}\) (starting line)

    \(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\) (from rest)

    \(a = 26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\)

    \(t = 5.56 \text{ s}\)

  2. Identify Unknown:

    \(x\) (final position/displacement)

  3. Choose Equation:

    The equation \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\) is ideal.

Figure 3.20: A dragster begins a race.

This example demonstrates how an object starting from rest covers a considerable distance when undergoing high, constant acceleration. Notice how we set x0 to zero; this is a common practice when we’re interested in the displacement from the starting point.

Solution: Dragster Displacement

Calculation:

Given:

\(x_0 = 0 \text{ m}\)

\(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(a = 26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\)

\(t = 5.56 \text{ s}\)

Using \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\):

Since \(x_0 = 0\) and \(v_0 = 0\), the equation simplifies to:

\(x = \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

Substitute values: \[ x = \frac{1}{2}(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(5.56 \text{ s})^2 \] \[ x = \frac{1}{2}(26.0)(30.9136) \text{ m} \] \[ \mathbf{x = 401.87 \text{ m}} \quad (\approx 402 \text{ m}) \]

Significance:

  • A significant distance covered in a short time due to high acceleration.
  • This distance is approximately a quarter-mile, the standard for drag racing, confirming the result’s reasonableness.

The calculation shows that a dragster covers about 400 meters, or a quarter-mile, in under 6 seconds, which is remarkably fast. This highlights the practical application of this kinematic equation in performance vehicle analysis and track design.

Kinematic Equation 3: Velocity-Position Relation

This equation is especially useful when time is not known or not required.

Derivation:

  1. Solve \(v = v_0 + at\) for \(t\): \(t = \frac{v - v_0}{a}\)
  2. Substitute \(t\) and \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\) into \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\)

\[ x - x_0 = \left( \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \right) \left( \frac{v - v_0}{a} \right) \]

\[ 2a(x - x_0) = (v_0 + v)(v - v_0) \]

\[ 2a(x - x_0) = v^2 - v_0^2 \]

\[ \mathbf{v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)} \quad (\text{constant } a) \]

This equation is a powerful tool because it allows you to relate final velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and displacement without needing to know the time. This often simplifies problems significantly. Notice how the derivation involves combining the previous two equations and eliminating time.

Example 3.9: Dragster Final Velocity (Time-Independent)

Calculate the final velocity of the dragster from Example 3.8 (\(a = 26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\), starts from rest, travels \(402 \text{ m}\)) without using information about time.

Strategy:

  1. Identify Knowns:

    \(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\) (from rest)

    \(a = 26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\)

    \(x - x_0 = 402 \text{ m}\) (displacement from Ex 3.8)

  2. Identify Unknown:

    \(v\) (final velocity)

  3. Choose Equation:

    The equation \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\) is perfectly suited as it does not require time.

This example specifically asks us to use the time-independent equation, which is a great way to reinforce its utility. We’re using the displacement we calculated earlier to find the final velocity, essentially working backward without needing the time value.

Solution: Dragster Final Velocity (Time-Independent)

Calculation:

Given:

\(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(a = 26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\)

\(x - x_0 = 402 \text{ m}\)

Using \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\):

\[ v^2 = (0 \text{ m/s})^2 + 2(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(402 \text{ m}) \]

\[ v^2 = 20904 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 \]

\[ v = \sqrt{20904 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2} \]

\[ \mathbf{v = 144.58 \text{ m/s}} \quad (\approx 145 \text{ m/s}) \]

Significance:

  • A final velocity of \(145 \text{ m/s}\) is approximately \(522 \text{ km/h}\) or \(324 \text{ mi/h}\).
  • This equation highlights that final velocity depends on acceleration and the distance over which it acts.
  • For a fixed acceleration, stopping distance increases quadratically with initial velocity (e.g., doubling speed means quadrupling stopping distance). This is why speed limits near schools are so important.

The final velocity is extremely high, as expected for a dragster at the end of a quarter-mile. This result is also consistent with our previous calculations for the dragster. The significance emphasizes a critical safety aspect for engineers: stopping distances grow very rapidly with speed.

Summary of Kinematic Equations (Constant \(a\))

These five equations are your toolbox for solving problems with constant acceleration.

  1. Position from Average Velocity:

    \[ x = x_0 + \bar{v}t \]

    (Where \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\) for constant \(a\))

  2. Velocity-Time: \[ \mathbf{v = v_0 + at} \]

  3. Position-Time: \[ \mathbf{x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2} \]

  4. Velocity-Position (Time-Independent): \[ \mathbf{v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)} \]

Caution

Important Note: These equations are interdependent. You need as many equations as there are unknowns to solve a problem. Always confirm the acceleration is constant before using them!

This slide summarizes all the key equations. It’s like a toolkit. The flowchart visually represents how these equations link the initial and final states given constant acceleration. When solving problems, you’ll often have three knowns and want to find one of the two unknowns. By looking at the variables involved in each equation, you can quickly identify which one is most suitable for your problem.

Challenge Problem: Car Braking Distance

On dry concrete, a car can accelerate opposite to the motion at a rate of \(7.00 \text{ m/s}^2\). On wet concrete, this rate is only \(5.00 \text{ m/s}^2\). The car is moving at \(30.0 \text{ m/s}\) (about \(110 \text{ km/h}\)).

  1. Find the distance necessary to stop the car on dry concrete.
  2. Find the distance necessary to stop the car on wet concrete.
  3. Repeat both calculations, including a driver’s reaction time of \(0.500 \text{ s}\) to get their foot on the brake. (Assume velocity is constant during reaction time).

Tip

Problem-Solving Strategy: - Draw a sketch (like Figure 3.22 in the text). - List knowns and unknowns for each part of the problem. - Choose the most appropriate kinematic equation(s). - Pay attention to signs for acceleration (opposite to motion).

This problem is a comprehensive test of our understanding of constant acceleration. It breaks down into multiple parts, requires careful consideration of signs, and introduces the real-world factor of reaction time. Engineers regularly perform these calculations for road safety, traffic light timing, and vehicle design.

Solution: Car Braking Distance (Part 1/3)

1. Stopping Distance on Dry Concrete

Knowns:

\(v_0 = 30.0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(v = 0 \text{ m/s}\) (car stops)

\(a = -7.00 \text{ m/s}^2\) (opposite to motion)

\(x_0 = 0 \text{ m}\)

Unknown: \(x\) (displacement \(\Delta x = x - x_0\))

Equation: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)

\[ 0^2 = (30.0 \text{ m/s})^2 + 2(-7.00 \text{ m/s}^2)(x - 0) \]

\[ 0 = 900 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 - 14.0 \text{ m/s}^2 \cdot x \]

\[ 14.0 \text{ m/s}^2 \cdot x = 900 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 \]

\[ x = \frac{900}{14.0} \text{ m} = \mathbf{64.3 \text{ m}} \]

For the first part, we apply the time-independent equation because time isn’t given or asked for. The negative sign for acceleration is crucial because the car is slowing down.

Solution: Car Braking Distance (Part 2/3)

2. Stopping Distance on Wet Concrete

Knowns:

\(v_0 = 30.0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(v = 0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(a = -5.00 \text{ m/s}^2\)

\(x_0 = 0 \text{ m}\)

Unknown: \(x\)

Equation: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)

\[ 0^2 = (30.0 \text{ m/s})^2 + 2(-5.00 \text{ m/s}^2)(x - 0) \]

\[ 0 = 900 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 - 10.0 \text{ m/s}^2 \cdot x \]

\[ 10.0 \text{ m/s}^2 \cdot x = 900 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 \]

\[ x = \frac{900}{10.0} \text{ m} = \mathbf{90.0 \text{ m}} \]

As expected, the stopping distance on wet concrete is longer due to the reduced acceleration (less effective braking). This highlights the importance of road conditions for vehicle safety.

Solution: Car Braking Distance (Part 3/3)

3. Total Displacement with Reaction Time (\(0.500 \text{ s}\))

The total distance is the sum of:

  • Displacement during reaction time (\(x_{\text{reaction}}\))
  • Braking distance (\(x_{\text{braking}}\))

a) Displacement during Reaction Time:

Assume constant velocity (\(a=0\)) during reaction time.

Knowns:

\(v = 30.0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(t = 0.500 \text{ s}\)

\(x_0 = 0 \text{ m}\)

Equation: \(x = x_0 + vt\)

\(x_{\text{reaction}} = 0 + (30.0 \text{ m/s})(0.500 \text{ s}) = \mathbf{15.0 \text{ m}}\)

b) Total Displacement

  • Dry Concrete: \(x_{\text{total, dry}} = x_{\text{braking, dry}} + x_{\text{reaction}}\) \(x_{\text{total, dry}} = 64.3 \text{ m} + 15.0 \text{ m} = \mathbf{79.3 \text{ m}}\)
  • Wet Concrete: \(x_{\text{total, wet}} = x_{\text{braking, wet}} + x_{\text{reaction}}\) \(x_{\text{total, wet}} = 90.0 \text{ m} + 15.0 \text{ m} = \mathbf{105.0 \text{ m}}\)

Warning

Reaction time adds significantly to the total stopping distance, demonstrating a critical factor in road safety and accident analysis.

This part adds a layer of realism. During reaction time, the car continues at its initial speed before braking begins. The 15 meters added by reaction time is substantial, making the total stopping distances much larger. This is a crucial consideration in traffic engineering and vehicle safety systems.

Two-Body Pursuit Problems

These problems involve the motion of two interrelated objects.

  • The motions are “coupled” – the unknown depends on both objects.
  • Strategy:
    1. Write separate kinematic equations for each object.
    2. Identify a common parameter (e.g., time, position) that links their motions.
    3. Solve the equations simultaneously for the unknown(s).

Figure 3.25: Car 1 (accelerating) catches Car 2 (constant velocity).

Two-body pursuit problems are a step up in complexity but are very common in engineering. Think about one car trying to overtake another, or a missile tracking a target. The key is to treat each object separately using the kinematic equations, and then find the link that connects their motions, usually a shared time or a shared position.

Challenge Problem: Cheetah Catching a Gazelle

A cheetah waits in hiding behind a bush. A gazelle runs past at a constant velocity of \(10 \text{ m/s}\). At the instant the gazelle passes the cheetah, the cheetah accelerates from rest at \(4 \text{ m/s}^2\) to catch the gazelle.

  1. How long does it take the cheetah to catch the gazelle?
  2. What is the displacement of the gazelle and cheetah when the catch occurs?

Tip

Common Parameter: At the moment the cheetah catches the gazelle, their positions (\(x\)) will be the same, and they will have been moving for the same amount of time (\(t\)) since the chase began.

This is a classic pursuit problem. We have two animals, one with constant velocity and one with constant acceleration. The key to solving it is recognizing that at the moment the cheetah catches the gazelle, they share the same position at the same time. We’ll set their position equations equal to each other.

Solution: Cheetah and Gazelle (Part 1/2)

1. Time to Catch (\(t\))

Assume \(x_0 = 0\) for both animals at \(t=0\).

Gazelle’s motion (constant velocity):

\(v_G = 10 \text{ m/s}\)

\(x_G = x_{0G} + v_G t = 0 + 10t\)

\[ x_G = 10t \]

Cheetah’s motion (constant acceleration from rest):

\(v_{0C} = 0 \text{ m/s}\)

\(a_C = 4 \text{ m/s}^2\)

\(x_C = x_{0C} + v_{0C}t + \frac{1}{2}a_C t^2 = 0 + 0 \cdot t + \frac{1}{2}(4)t^2\)

\[ x_C = 2t^2 \]

When the cheetah catches the gazelle, \(x_C = x_G\):

\[ 2t^2 = 10t \]

\[ 2t^2 - 10t = 0 \]

\[ 2t(t - 5) = 0 \]

This yields two solutions: \(t = 0 \text{ s}\) (initial moment) and \(t = \mathbf{5 \text{ s}}\).

The cheetah catches the gazelle after \(\mathbf{5 \text{ seconds}}\).

By setting up separate equations for each animal and then equating their positions, we can solve for the common time. The t=0 solution simply means they were at the same place at the start of the chase. The t=5s is the meaningful solution for when the cheetah catches the gazelle.

Solution: Cheetah and Gazelle (Part 2/2)

2. Displacement at Catching Moment

Using \(t = 5 \text{ s}\):

Displacement of Gazelle (\(x_G\)):

\[ x_G = 10t = 10 \text{ m/s} \times 5 \text{ s} = \mathbf{50 \text{ m}} \]

Displacement of Cheetah (\(x_C\)):

\[ x_C = 2t^2 = 2 \text{ m/s}^2 \times (5 \text{ s})^2 = 2 \times 25 \text{ m} = \mathbf{50 \text{ m}} \]

Conclusion:

Both animals travel a distance of \(\mathbf{50 \text{ m}}\) when the cheetah catches the gazelle.

As expected, both displacements are equal, confirming the cheetah indeed caught the gazelle at this distance. This type of problem-solving approach is invaluable in many engineering fields, from robotics to traffic control, where you need to analyze the relative motion of multiple systems.

Key Takeaways

  • Constant Acceleration: The kinematic equations are valid only when acceleration is constant.
  • Toolkit of Equations:
    • \(v = v_0 + at\)
    • \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
    • \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)
    • \(x = x_0 + \frac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)t\)
  • Problem-Solving Strategy:
    • Establish a coordinate system.
    • Identify knowns and unknowns.
    • Select the appropriate equation(s).
    • Pay close attention to vector signs (direction).
  • Two-Body Problems: Write separate equations for each object and link them by a common variable (usually time or position) to solve simultaneously.

To wrap up, remember these critical points. The constant acceleration assumption is non-negotiable for these equations. Practice selecting the right equation for the given information. Always be mindful of the vector nature of velocity and acceleration through their signs. And finally, don’t be intimidated by two-body problems; just break them down into individual motions and find the common link. Mastering these concepts is fundamental for tackling more complex physics and engineering challenges. Thank you!
