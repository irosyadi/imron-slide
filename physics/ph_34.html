3.4 Motion with Constant Acceleration

Predicting Kinematic Behavior

Welcome to this session on motion with constant acceleration. This is a foundational topic in kinematics that allows us to predict and analyze the movement of objects under consistent forces, which is very common in engineering.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Identify which equations of motion are to be used to solve for unknowns.
  • Use appropriate equations of motion to solve for various kinematic variables.
  • Solve two-body pursuit problems using constant acceleration kinematics.

These objectives will guide our discussion. We’ll develop a set of powerful equations and learn how to apply them to single-body and multi-body motion scenarios.

The Importance of Constant Acceleration

  • We intuitively know that greater acceleration leads to greater displacement.
  • This section develops specific equations relating displacement, velocity, and acceleration under the assumption of constant acceleration.
  • This assumption is:
    • True for many real-world situations (e.g., free fall, many controlled systems).
    • A good approximation for average behavior in complex motions.
    • Allows complex motions to be broken into segments with constant acceleration.

While real-world acceleration might vary, constant acceleration provides a robust framework. Think of a car accelerating uniformly, or a ball falling near Earth’s surface. Even complex motions can often be modeled as a series of constant acceleration phases.

Simplifying Notation

To make our equations cleaner, we’ll adopt a simplified notation:

  • Initial time (\(t_0\)): Set to zero (like starting a stopwatch).
  • Elapsed time (\(\Delta t\)): Becomes simply t (final time).
  • Initial values: Denoted with subscript 0 (e.g., \(x_0\), \(v_0\)).
  • Final values: Have no subscript (e.g., \(x\), \(v\)).

Standard Notation

  • \(\Delta t = t_f - t_0\)
  • \(\Delta x = x_f - x_0\)
  • \(\Delta v = v_f - v_0\)

Simplified Notation (for \(t_0 = 0\))

  • \(\Delta t = t\)
  • \(\Delta x = x - x_0\)
  • \(\Delta v = v - v_0\)

Note

A key assumption: acceleration (\(\boldsymbol{a}\)) is constant. Therefore, average acceleration (\(\bar{a}\)) equals instantaneous acceleration (\(a\)).

This simplification is standard in kinematics for constant acceleration problems. It streamlines our equations and reduces clutter. The constant acceleration assumption is critical; it means we don’t need calculus to find instantaneous acceleration.

Kinematic Equation 1: Displacement from Average Velocity

We start with the definition of average velocity: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \]

Using simplified notation (\(t_0=0\), \(\Delta t=t\), \(\Delta x = x - x_0\)): \[ \bar{v} = \frac{x - x_0}{t} \]

Solving for final position \(x\): \[ x = x_0 + \bar{v}t \]

When acceleration is constant, the average velocity is the simple average of the initial and final velocities: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \]

Tip

This formula for \(\bar{v}\) is only valid when acceleration is constant.

This first equation relates position, initial position, average velocity, and time. The special case of constant acceleration allows us to express average velocity in a very convenient form, which we will use for further derivations.

Graphical Insight: Average Velocity with Constant Acceleration

Figure 3.18 (a): Constant acceleration. The average velocity is exactly halfway between \(v_0\) and \(v\).

  • When acceleration is constant, velocity changes linearly.
  • The average velocity over an interval is simply the arithmetic mean of the initial and final velocities. \[ \bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \] (For example, \((40 + 80) / 2 = 60 \text{ km/h}\))

This graph visually confirms why the simple average formula for velocity works. With a straight line (constant acceleration) on a velocity-time graph, the area under the curve (displacement) can be found using the average height of the trapezoid, which is exactly the average velocity.

Kinematic Equation 2: Final Velocity

We start with the definition of (constant) acceleration: \[ a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]

Using simplified notation (\(t_0=0\), \(\Delta t=t\), \(\Delta v = v - v_0\)): \[ a = \frac{v - v_0}{t} \]

Solving for final velocity \(v\): \[ v = v_0 + at \quad \text{(constant } a \text{)} \]

Important

This equation relates final velocity, initial velocity, constant acceleration, and time.

This is a very powerful equation. It tells us that the final velocity depends on the initial velocity and how much the acceleration “acts” over time.

Problem: Calculating Final Velocity (Airplane)

An airplane lands with an initial velocity of \(70.0 \text{ m/s}\) and then accelerates opposite to the motion at \(1.50 \text{ m/s}^2\) for \(40.0 \text{ s}\).

What is its final velocity?

This problem asks for final velocity and provides initial velocity, acceleration, and time. Choose the appropriate kinematic equation. Remember to define a positive direction!

Solution: Calculating Final Velocity (Airplane)

Strategy:

  1. Define coordinate system: Let the initial direction of motion (East) be positive (+).
  2. Identify knowns:
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0 = +70.0 \text{ m/s}\))
    • Acceleration (\(a = -1.50 \text{ m/s}^2\), since it’s opposite to motion)
    • Time (\(t = 40.0 \text{ s}\))
  3. Identify unknown: Final velocity (\(v\)).
  4. Choose equation: \(v = v_0 + at\).

Figure 3.19: Airplane slowing down.

Solution: \[ v = 70.0 \text{ m/s} + (-1.50 \text{ m/s}^2)(40.0 \text{ s}) \] \[ v = 70.0 \text{ m/s} - 60.0 \text{ m/s} = 10.0 \text{ m/s} \]

Answer: The airplane’s final velocity is \(10.0 \text{ m/s}\).

The final velocity is positive, meaning the plane is still moving in the original direction (East), but much slower. This calculation is vital for runway length requirements or braking system design.

Kinematic Equation 3: Final Position

We combine \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\) and \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\). Substituting \(\bar{v}\): \[ x = x_0 + \left( \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \right)t \]

Then substitute \(v = v_0 + at\): \[ x = x_0 + \left( \frac{v_0 + (v_0 + at)}{2} \right)t \] \[ x = x_0 + \left( \frac{2v_0 + at}{2} \right)t \] \[ x = x_0 + \left( v_0 + \frac{1}{2}at \right)t \] \[ x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \quad \text{(constant } a \text{)} \]

Important

This equation relates final position, initial position, initial velocity, constant acceleration, and time.

This equation is particularly useful when you don’t know the final velocity but know the acceleration and time. It shows how displacement grows quadratically with time when acceleration is present.

Problem: Calculating Displacement (Dragster)

A dragster accelerates from rest at \(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\) for \(5.56 \text{ s}\).

How far does it travel in this time?

Figure 3.20: Dragster at start.

Here, we need to find displacement. We’re given initial velocity (from rest), acceleration, and time. Which equation fits best?

Solution: Calculating Displacement (Dragster)

Strategy:

  1. Define coordinate system: Let the starting line be \(x_0 = 0\).
  2. Identify knowns:
    • Initial position (\(x_0 = 0 \text{ m}\))
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\), from rest)
    • Acceleration (\(a = 26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\))
    • Time (\(t = 5.56 \text{ s}\))
  3. Identify unknown: Final position (\(x\)).
  4. Choose equation: \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\).

Figure 3.21: Sketch of an accelerating dragster.

Solution: Since \(x_0 = 0\) and \(v_0 = 0\): \[ x = 0 + (0)t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \] \[ x = \frac{1}{2}(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(5.56 \text{ s})^2 \] \[ x = \frac{1}{2}(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(30.9136 \text{ s}^2) \approx 401.87 \text{ m} \]

Answer: The dragster travels approximately \(402 \text{ m}\).

This distance is very close to a quarter-mile, the standard for drag racing. This equation shows how quickly distance can accumulate even from rest with high acceleration.

Kinematic Equation 4: Final Velocity from Displacement

We can derive a fourth useful equation by combining \(v = v_0 + at\) and \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\) (where \(\bar{v} = (v_0 + v)/2\)).

  • From \(v = v_0 + at\), solve for \(t\): \[ t = \frac{v - v_0}{a} \]
  • Substitute this expression for \(t\) and \(\bar{v} = (v_0 + v)/2\) into \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\): \[ x = x_0 + \left( \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \right) \left( \frac{v - v_0}{a} \right) \] \[ x - x_0 = \frac{v^2 - v_0^2}{2a} \]

Solving for \(v^2\): \[ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0) \quad \text{(constant } a \text{)} \]

Important

This equation relates final velocity, initial velocity, constant acceleration, and displacement. Time is not directly included.

This equation is extremely useful when time is not known or not relevant to the problem. It highlights the direct relationship between velocity change and displacement under constant acceleration.

Problem: Calculating Final Velocity (without time)

Calculate the final velocity of the dragster from the previous problem (accelerates from rest at \(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\), travels \(402 \text{ m}\)) without using information about time.

We have initial velocity, acceleration, and displacement. We need final velocity. This is a perfect scenario for the fourth kinematic equation.

Solution: Calculating Final Velocity (without time)

Strategy:

  1. Identify knowns:
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\))
    • Acceleration (\(a = 26.0 \text{ m/s}^2\))
    • Displacement (\(x - x_0 = 402 \text{ m}\))
  2. Identify unknown: Final velocity (\(v\)).
  3. Choose equation: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\).

Solution: \[ v^2 = (0 \text{ m/s})^2 + 2(26.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(402 \text{ m}) \] \[ v^2 = 0 + 20904 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 \] \[ v = \sqrt{20904 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2} \approx 144.58 \text{ m/s} \]

Answer: The dragster’s final velocity is approximately \(145 \text{ m/s}\).

This is about 324 miles per hour! Note that when taking the square root, you would generally get two answers (positive and negative). Here, since the dragster is speeding up in the positive direction, we take the positive root. This equation also illustrates why high-speed stops require much longer distances, as stopping distance is proportional to \(v_0^2\).

Summary of Kinematic Equations (Constant \(a\))

These five equations are your toolbox for solving constant acceleration problems:

  1. \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\)
  2. \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\)
  3. \(v = v_0 + at\)
  4. \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
  5. \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)

Tip

You need as many independent equations as there are unknowns to solve a given situation. Always list your knowns and unknowns first to select the best equation(s).

These five equations are inter-related, but each is optimized for solving for a particular unknown when other specific values are known. Memorize them, but more importantly, understand when and how to apply each one.

Problem: How Far Does a Car Go? (Example 3.10)

On dry concrete, a car can accelerate opposite to the motion at \(7.00 \text{ m/s}^2\). On wet concrete, it’s only \(5.00 \text{ m/s}^2\). The car is initially moving at \(30.0 \text{ m/s}\).

(a) Find the stopping distance on dry concrete. (b) Find the stopping distance on wet concrete. (c) Repeat (a) and (b), but account for a reaction time of \(0.500 \text{ s}\) before braking.

This is a multi-part problem that requires careful application of the kinematic equations. For part (c), remember that during reaction time, the car moves at a constant velocity, and only then does acceleration (braking) begin.

Solution: How Far Does a Car Go? (Part a & b)

Strategy:

  • Define forward direction as positive (+).
  • Knowns: \(v_0 = 30.0 \text{ m/s}\), \(v = 0 \text{ m/s}\) (stops).
  • Unknown: Displacement (\(x - x_0\)).
  • Equation: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\) (since time is unknown).
  • Solve for \((x - x_0)\): \((x - x_0) = \frac{v^2 - v_0^2}{2a}\).

Figure 3.22: Car stopping distance sketch.

(a) Dry Concrete: \(a = -7.00 \text{ m/s}^2\) (negative because opposite to \(v_0\)).

\[ (x - x_0)_{\text{dry}} = \frac{(0 \text{ m/s})^2 - (30.0 \text{ m/s})^2}{2(-7.00 \text{ m/s}^2)} = \frac{-900 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2}{-14.00 \text{ m/s}^2} = 64.3 \text{ m} \]

Answer (a): Stopping distance on dry concrete is \(64.3 \text{ m}\).

(b) Wet Concrete: \(a = -5.00 \text{ m/s}^2\).

\[ (x - x_0)_{\text{wet}} = \frac{(0 \text{ m/s})^2 - (30.0 \text{ m/s})^2}{2(-5.00 \text{ m/s}^2)} = \frac{-900 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2}{-10.00 \text{ m/s}^2} = 90.0 \text{ m} \]

Answer (b): Stopping distance on wet concrete is \(90.0 \text{ m}\).

Notice how the reduced braking acceleration on wet concrete significantly increases the stopping distance. This is why driving slower in rain is critical.

Solution: How Far Does a Car Go? (Part c)

(c) With \(0.500 \text{ s}\) reaction time: During reaction time, velocity is constant (\(a = 0\)).

  • \(v_0 = 30.0 \text{ m/s}\)
  • \(t_{\text{reaction}} = 0.500 \text{ s}\)
  • Equation: \(x = x_0 + v_0t\) (since \(a=0\)).

\[ x_{\text{reaction}} = (30.0 \text{ m/s})(0.500 \text{ s}) = 15.0 \text{ m} \]

Now add this reaction distance to the braking distances:

  • Total distance (dry): \(64.3 \text{ m} + 15.0 \text{ m} = 79.3 \text{ m}\)
  • Total distance (wet): \(90.0 \text{ m} + 15.0 \text{ m} = 105.0 \text{ m}\)

Figure 3.23: Total stopping distance including reaction time.

Answer (c): Total stopping distance is \(79.3 \text{ m}\) (dry) and \(105.0 \text{ m}\) (wet).

Reaction time adds a significant amount to the stopping distance. This emphasizes why attentive driving and proper following distance are crucial for safety, especially at higher speeds and in adverse conditions.

Problem: Calculating Time (Freeway Ramp)

A car merges onto a freeway on a \(200 \text{ m}\) long ramp. Its initial velocity is \(10.0 \text{ m/s}\) and it accelerates at \(2.00 \text{ m/s}^2\).

How long does it take the car to travel the \(200 \text{ m}\) up the ramp?

We’re looking for time, given initial position, initial velocity, acceleration, and final position. Which kinematic equation fits this scenario?

Solution: Calculating Time (Freeway Ramp)

Strategy:

  1. Define coordinate system: Let start of ramp be \(x_0 = 0\).
  2. Identify knowns:
    • Initial position (\(x_0 = 0 \text{ m}\))
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0 = 10.0 \text{ m/s}\))
    • Acceleration (\(a = 2.00 \text{ m/s}^2\))
    • Final position (\(x = 200 \text{ m}\))
  3. Identify unknown: Time (\(t\)).
  4. Choose equation: \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\).

Figure 3.24: Car on a freeway ramp.

Solution: Substitute values into \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\): \[ 200 = 0 + (10.0)t + \frac{1}{2}(2.00)t^2 \] \[ 200 = 10t + t^2 \] Rearrange into a quadratic equation: \[ t^2 + 10t - 200 = 0 \] Use the quadratic formula \(t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\): \[ t = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{(10)^2 - 4(1)(-200)}}{2(1)} \] \[ t = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{100 + 800}}{2} = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{900}}{2} = \frac{-10 \pm 30}{2} \] Two solutions: \(t = \frac{-10 + 30}{2} = 10 \text{ s}\) and \(t = \frac{-10 - 30}{2} = -20 \text{ s}\). Time cannot be negative, so we discard \(t = -20 \text{ s}\).

Answer: It takes \(10.0 \text{ s}\) for the car to travel \(200 \text{ m}\).

When you encounter a quadratic equation, always consider both solutions and determine if both are physically meaningful. In this case, negative time is not valid for the physical scenario described.

Check Your Understanding 3.5

Problem: A rocket accelerates at a rate of \(20 \text{ m/s}^2\) during launch.

How long does it take the rocket to reach a velocity of \(400 \text{ m/s}\)?

Assume the rocket starts from rest. We have acceleration, final velocity, and initial velocity. We need to find time.

Check Your Understanding 3.5 - Solution

Strategy:

  • Assume initial velocity \(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\) (starts from rest).
  • Knowns: \(a = 20 \text{ m/s}^2\), \(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\), \(v = 400 \text{ m/s}\).
  • Unknown: Time (\(t\)).
  • Equation: \(v = v_0 + at\).

Solution:

\[ 400 \text{ m/s} = 0 \text{ m/s} + (20 \text{ m/s}^2)t \] \[ t = \frac{400 \text{ m/s}}{20 \text{ m/s}^2} = 20 \text{ s} \]

Answer: It takes \(20 \text{ s}\) for the rocket to reach \(400 \text{ m/s}\).

This is a direct application of the velocity-time equation. Engineers use this type of calculation to determine how quickly a vehicle can achieve a target speed.

Two-Body Pursuit Problems

  • Involve the motion of two objects where the unknown depends on both.
  • The motions of the objects are coupled.
  • To solve:
    1. Write separate equations of motion for each object.
    2. Identify a common parameter (e.g., time, position) that links their motions.
    3. Solve the equations simultaneously.

Figure 3.25: Car 1 pursuing Car 2.

These problems are more complex but very common in engineering, for example, in collision avoidance, docking maneuvers, or race simulations. The key is to set up an equation for each object and find what they have in common at the desired moment.

Problem: Cheetah Catching a Gazelle (Example 3.13)

A cheetah hides behind a bush. A gazelle runs past at a constant speed of \(10 \text{ m/s}\). At that instant, the cheetah accelerates from rest at \(4 \text{ m/s}^2\) to catch the gazelle.

(a) How long does it take the cheetah to catch the gazelle? (b) What is the displacement of the gazelle and cheetah when caught?

Here, we have two objects with different types of motion (constant velocity vs. constant acceleration). The common parameter when the cheetah catches the gazelle is their position and time.

Strategy & Solution (Part a): Cheetah Catching Gazelle

Strategy:

  1. Define coordinate system: Let \(x_0 = 0\) for both animals at \(t = 0\).
  2. Equations of motion:
    • Gazelle (constant velocity): \(x_g = x_{0g} + v_g t \Rightarrow x_g = (10 \text{ m/s})t\)
    • Cheetah (constant acceleration from rest): \(x_c = x_{0c} + v_{0c}t + \frac{1}{2}a_c t^2 \Rightarrow x_c = \frac{1}{2}(4 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 = (2 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2\)
  3. Common parameter: When the cheetah catches the gazelle, their positions are equal (\(x_g = x_c\)).
  4. Solve simultaneously for time (t):

Solution (a): Set \(x_g = x_c\):

\[ (10 \text{ m/s})t = (2 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 \]

Rearrange and solve for \(t\):

\[ (2 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 - (10 \text{ m/s})t = 0 \]

\[ t((2 \text{ m/s}^2)t - (10 \text{ m/s})) = 0 \]

Two solutions: \(t = 0 \text{ s}\) (start) or \((2 \text{ m/s}^2)t = 10 \text{ m/s}\).

\[ t = \frac{10 \text{ m/s}}{2 \text{ m/s}^2} = 5 \text{ s} \]

Answer (a): It takes \(5 \text{ s}\) for the cheetah to catch the gazelle.

The \(t=0\) solution simply represents the moment they are at the same position at the start. The \(t=5s\) solution is when the cheetah catches the gazelle. This technique of setting positions equal is fundamental for pursuit problems.

Solution (Part b): Cheetah Catching Gazelle

(b) What is the displacement of the gazelle and cheetah?

  • Use either the gazelle’s or the cheetah’s equation of motion with \(t = 5 \text{ s}\). They should yield the same displacement.

Using Gazelle’s equation:

\[ x_g = (10 \text{ m/s})t = (10 \text{ m/s})(5 \text{ s}) = 50 \text{ m} \]

Using Cheetah’s equation:

\[ x_c = (2 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 = (2 \text{ m/s}^2)(5 \text{ s})^2 = (2 \text{ m/s}^2)(25 \text{ s}^2) = 50 \text{ m} \]

Answer (b): The displacement for both is \(50 \text{ m}\).

The consistency of the displacement calculation from both equations confirms the correctness of our time calculation. This type of problem-solving approach is invaluable in many engineering fields.

Check Your Understanding 3.6

Problem: A bicycle has a constant velocity of \(10 \text{ m/s}\). A person starts from rest and begins to run to catch up to the bicycle in \(30 \text{ s}\). At \(t=30 \text{ s}\), the bicycle is at the same position as the person.

What is the acceleration of the person?

This is another two-body pursuit problem, but this time we are solving for an unknown acceleration. Set up the equations for both the bicycle and the person, then use the common information (position and time) to solve for the person’s acceleration.

Check Your Understanding 3.6 - Solution

Strategy:

  1. Define coordinate system: Let \(x_0 = 0\) for both at \(t = 0\).
  2. Equations of motion:
    • Bicycle (constant velocity): \(x_b = x_{0b} + v_b t \Rightarrow x_b = (10 \text{ m/s})t\)
    • Person (constant acceleration from rest): \(x_p = x_{0p} + v_{0p}t + \frac{1}{2}a_p t^2 \Rightarrow x_p = \frac{1}{2}a_p t^2\)
  3. Common parameter: At \(t = 30 \text{ s}\), their positions are equal (\(x_b = x_p\)).
  4. Solve for acceleration (\(a_p\)):

Solution: First, find the position of the bicycle at \(t=30 \text{ s}\): \[ x_b = (10 \text{ m/s})(30 \text{ s}) = 300 \text{ m} \]

Since the person is at the same position at \(t=30 \text{ s}\), \(x_p = 300 \text{ m}\). Now use the person’s equation of motion: \[ x_p = \frac{1}{2}a_p t^2 \] \[ 300 \text{ m} = \frac{1}{2}a_p (30 \text{ s})^2 \] \[ 300 \text{ m} = \frac{1}{2}a_p (900 \text{ s}^2) \] \[ 300 \text{ m} = (450 \text{ s}^2)a_p \] \[ a_p = \frac{300 \text{ m}}{450 \text{ s}^2} = \frac{2}{3} \text{ m/s}^2 \approx 0.67 \text{ m/s}^2 \]

Answer: The acceleration of the person is approximately \(0.67 \text{ m/s}^2\).

This acceleration is achievable for an athletic person. It shows how even a relatively small, constant acceleration can cover significant distances over time.

Key Takeaways

  • Constant acceleration simplifies kinematic analysis, allowing algebraic solutions.
  • Simplified Notation (\(t_0=0\), \(x_0\), \(v_0\) for initial; \(t, x, v\) for final) improves clarity.
  • Five core kinematic equations are used to solve for unknowns in constant acceleration problems.
  • Average velocity is \((\boldsymbol{v_0+v})/2\) only when acceleration is constant.
  • Problem-solving strategy: Identify knowns/unknowns, choose the appropriate equation(s).
  • Two-body pursuit problems require setting up separate equations for each object and solving simultaneously based on a common parameter (usually time or position).

These key points summarize the most important aspects of motion with constant acceleration. Mastering these concepts and equations is fundamental for all future studies in mechanics and many areas of engineering.

Key Equations (Constant \(a\))

Equation Description
\(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\) Position from average velocity and time
\(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\) Average velocity with constant acceleration
\(v = v_0 + at\) Final velocity from initial, acceleration, time
\(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\) Final position from initial, velocity, acceleration, time
\(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\) Final velocity from initial, acceleration, displacement

These five equations are your go-to tools. Practice using them to become proficient in solving a wide range of kinematics problems.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Constant Acceleration A state of motion where the rate of change of velocity remains uniform over time.
Kinematic Equations A set of five mathematical equations that describe the motion of an object under constant acceleration.
Initial Position (\(x_0\)) The starting location of an object in a given coordinate system.
Initial Velocity (\(v_0\)) The velocity of an object at the beginning of a time interval.
Final Position (\(x\)) The location of an object at the end of a time interval.
Final Velocity (\(v\)) The velocity of an object at the end of a time interval.
Two-Body Pursuit Problem A problem involving two objects whose motions are interdependent, requiring simultaneous solution of their kinematic equations.

Familiarize yourself with these terms. A clear understanding of the vocabulary is just as important as mastering the equations.
