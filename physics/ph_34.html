3.4 Motion with Constant Acceleration

Predicting Kinematic Behavior

Introduction to Constant Acceleration

In this section, we develop powerful equations to describe motion when acceleration is constant.

  • Relates displacement, velocity, acceleration, and time.
  • Essential for analyzing many real-world scenarios.

We will cover:

  1. Simplified notation for kinematic variables.
  2. Derivation of key kinematic equations.
  3. Solving single-body motion problems.
  4. Solving two-body pursuit problems.

Note

The assumption of constant acceleration is crucial as it simplifies calculations and allows us to avoid complex calculus for instantaneous values.

Simplified Notation

To simplify our kinematic equations, we make some notational conventions:

  • Initial time (\(t_0\)) = 0: As if starting a stopwatch.
    • Elapsed time \(\Delta t = t_f - t_0\) becomes \(\Delta t = t\).
  • Subscript ‘0’ for initial values:
    • Initial position: \(x_0\)
    • Initial velocity: \(v_0\)
  • No subscript for final values:
    • Final time: \(t\)
    • Final position: \(x\)
    • Final velocity: \(v\)

This simplifies expressions:

  • Displacement: \(\Delta x = x - x_0\)
  • Change in velocity: \(\Delta v = v - v_0\)

The Constant Acceleration Assumption

Our most important assumption: acceleration is constant.

  • This means \(\bar{a} = a = \text{constant}\).
  • We can use the symbol ‘\(a\)’ for acceleration at all times.

Tip

Why is this assumption so useful?
It means instantaneous acceleration is always equal to average acceleration, greatly simplifying problem-solving.

Justification:

  • Many real-world situations have constant acceleration (e.g., free fall).
  • For variable acceleration, motion can be broken into segments, each with approximate constant acceleration.

Kinematic Equation 1: Displacement from Average Velocity

We start with the definition of average velocity:

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} \]

Using simplified notation (\(\Delta x = x - x_0\), \(\Delta t = t\)):

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{x - x_0}{t} \]

Solving for \(x\):

\[ x = x_0 + \bar{v}t \]

This equation is fundamental, directly linking position, initial position, average velocity, and time.

Average Velocity with Constant Acceleration

When acceleration is constant, the average velocity (\(\bar{v}\)) is simply the average of the initial and final velocities:

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2} \]

This relationship is visually intuitive on a velocity-time graph with constant acceleration.

Figure 3.18 (a) Constant acceleration: Average velocity is the midpoint. (b) Non-constant acceleration: Average velocity is not simply \(\frac{v_0+v}{2}\).

The area under the curve of a velocity-time graph represents displacement. For constant acceleration, this area is a trapezoid. The average height of the trapezoid is \((v_0+v)/2\). For non-constant acceleration, the shape is more complex, and a simple average does not apply.

Kinematic Equation 2: Final Velocity from Acceleration and Time

We start with the definition of acceleration (\(a = \text{constant}\)):

\[ a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \]

Using simplified notation (\(\Delta v = v - v_0\), \(\Delta t = t\)):

\[ a = \frac{v - v_0}{t} \]

Solving for \(v\):

\[ v = v_0 + at \]

Note

This equation is very useful as it directly links final velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and time. It shows how much velocity changes due to acceleration over a given time.

Example: Calculating Final Velocity

Problem: An airplane lands with an initial velocity of 70.0 m/s and then accelerates opposite to the motion at 1.50 m/s² for 40.0 s. What is its final velocity?

Strategy:

  1. Identify Knowns: \(v_0 = 70.0\) m/s, \(a = -1.50\) m/s² (negative because opposite to motion), \(t = 40.0\) s.
  2. Identify Unknown: Final velocity, \(v\).
  3. Choose Equation: Use \(v = v_0 + at\).

Solution: \[ v = 70.0 \, \text{m/s} + (-1.50 \, \text{m/s}^2)(40.0 \, \text{s}) \] \[ v = 70.0 \, \text{m/s} - 60.0 \, \text{m/s} = 10.0 \, \text{m/s} \]

Figure 3.19: Airplane slowing down during landing.

The positive final velocity indicates the airplane is still moving forward (in the initial direction), but much slower. This makes intuitive sense for a landing plane.

Insights from \(v = v_0 + at\)

This equation provides useful insights:

  • Final velocity depends on acceleration magnitude and duration.
  • If \(a=0\), then \(v=v_0\) (velocity is constant).
  • If \(a\) is negative (opposite to \(v_0\)), then \(v < v_0\) (slowing down).

These observations align with our physical intuition.

Kinematic Equation 3: Final Position with Constant Acceleration

Combine \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\) and \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\) and \(v = v_0 + at\).

Substitute \(\bar{v} = v_0 + \frac{1}{2}at\) into \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\):

\[ x = x_0 + (v_0 + \frac{1}{2}at)t \]

\[ x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

Important

This is one of the most frequently used kinematic equations. It relates final position to initial position, initial velocity, acceleration, and time.

Example: Calculating Displacement of an Accelerating Object

Problem: A dragster accelerates from rest at 26.0 m/s² for 5.56 s. How far does it travel?

Strategy:

  1. Identify Knowns: \(v_0 = 0\) m/s (from rest), \(a = 26.0\) m/s², \(t = 5.56\) s. Assume \(x_0 = 0\).
  2. Identify Unknown: Final position, \(x\).
  3. Choose Equation: Use \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\).

Figure 3.21: Sketch of an accelerating dragster.

The sketch helps visualize the motion and define the starting position. Since it starts from rest, the \(v_0t\) term simplifies to zero.

Example: Solution

Solution:

Since \(x_0 = 0\) and \(v_0 = 0\), the equation simplifies to: \[ x = \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

Substitute the known values: \[ x = \frac{1}{2}(26.0 \, \text{m/s}^2)(5.56 \, \text{s})^2 \] \[ x = \frac{1}{2}(26.0 \, \text{m/s}^2)(30.9136 \, \text{s}^2) \] \[ x = 401.8768 \, \text{m} \approx 402 \, \text{m} \]

Significance: 402 m is approximately a quarter-mile, a standard distance for drag racing, making the answer reasonable. This equation shows displacement depends on the square of time (\(t^2\)) when acceleration is non-zero.

The squared dependence on time means that the distance covered increases rapidly over time when accelerating. For example, in the first half of the time, the car only covers a quarter of the total distance.

Kinematic Equation 4: Final Velocity from Distance and Acceleration

We can derive a fourth useful equation by eliminating time (\(t\)) from the others.

From \(v = v_0 + at\), solve for \(t\): \[ t = \frac{v - v_0}{a} \]

Substitute this \(t\) and \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\) into \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\): \[ x = x_0 + \left(\frac{v_0 + v}{2}\right)\left(\frac{v - v_0}{a}\right) \]

Rearrange to get: \[ v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0) \]

Important

This equation is powerful because it allows you to find final velocity without knowing the time, or to find displacement without knowing the time.

Example: Calculating Final Velocity Without Time

Problem: Calculate the final velocity of the dragster from the previous example (\(a = 26.0\) m/s², \(\Delta x = 402\) m, \(v_0 = 0\)) without using information about time.

Strategy:

  1. Identify Knowns: \(v_0 = 0\) m/s, \(\Delta x = x - x_0 = 402\) m, \(a = 26.0\) m/s².
  2. Identify Unknown: Final velocity, \(v\).
  3. Choose Equation: Use \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\).

Solution: \[ v^2 = (0 \, \text{m/s})^2 + 2(26.0 \, \text{m/s}^2)(402 \, \text{m}) \] \[ v^2 = 0 + 20904 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2 \] \[ v = \sqrt{20904 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2} \approx 144.6 \, \text{m/s} \approx 145 \, \text{m/s} \]

We take the positive square root because the dragster is moving in the positive direction. A velocity of 145 m/s is very fast, equivalent to about 324 mi/h, which is expected for a dragster.

Insights from \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)

This equation offers additional insights:

  • Final velocity depends on acceleration and the distance over which it acts.
  • For a fixed acceleration, if initial velocity doubles, the stopping distance quadruples (\(x \propto v_0^2\)).
    • This is why high speeds are dangerous; it takes much longer to stop!

Summary of Kinematic Equations (Constant \(a\))

These five equations are fundamental for solving problems with constant acceleration:

  1. \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\)
  2. \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\)
  3. \(v = v_0 + at\)
  4. \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
  5. \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)

Tip

When solving problems, identify your knowns and unknowns.

Choose the equation(s) that allow you to solve for the unknown(s) with the given information.

You need as many independent equations as you have unknowns.

Example: Stopping Distance of a Car

Problem: A car travels at 30.0 m/s.

  1. What is the stopping distance on dry concrete (\(a = -7.00\) m/s²)?

  2. What is the stopping distance on wet concrete (\(a = -5.00\) m/s²)?

  3. Repeat, accounting for a 0.500 s reaction time.

Strategy:

  • Set \(v_0 = 30.0\) m/s, \(v = 0\) m/s (car stops).
  • For (a) and (b), use \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\).
  • For (c), first calculate distance traveled during reaction time (constant velocity), then add to braking distance.

Figure 3.22: Sketch for stopping distance.

The sketch helps visualize the initial and final states and the direction of acceleration.

It’s important to use the correct sign for acceleration (negative, as it’s opposite to motion).

Example: Solution (Parts a & b)

Solution (Parts a & b):

Given: \(v_0 = 30.0\) m/s, \(v = 0\) m/s, \(x_0 = 0\).

Equation: \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)

Rearrange for \(x\): \(x = \frac{v^2 - v_0^2}{2a}\)

(a) Dry Concrete (\(a = -7.00\) m/s²): \[ x_{\text{dry}} = \frac{(0 \, \text{m/s})^2 - (30.0 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2(-7.00 \, \text{m/s}^2)} \] \[ x_{\text{dry}} = \frac{-900 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2}{-14.00 \, \text{m/s}^2} = 64.3 \, \text{m} \]

(b) Wet Concrete (\(a = -5.00\) m/s²): \[ x_{\text{wet}} = \frac{(0 \, \text{m/s})^2 - (30.0 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2(-5.00 \, \text{m/s}^2)} \] \[ x_{\text{wet}} = \frac{-900 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2}{-10.00 \, \text{m/s}^2} = 90.0 \, \text{m} \]

Notice that the stopping distance is significantly longer on wet concrete due to the lower magnitude of acceleration (less effective braking).

Example: Solution (Part c)

Solution (Part c):

During Reaction Time (0.500 s):

  • Velocity is constant: \(v_0 = 30.0\) m/s, \(a = 0\).
  • Equation: \(x = x_0 + v_0t\). Assume \(x_0 = 0\). \[ x_{\text{reaction}} = (30.0 \, \text{m/s})(0.500 \, \text{s}) = 15.0 \, \text{m} \]

Total Stopping Distances:

  • Dry Concrete: \(x_{\text{total, dry}} = x_{\text{reaction}} + x_{\text{dry}} = 15.0 \, \text{m} + 64.3 \, \text{m} = 79.3 \, \text{m}\)
  • Wet Concrete: \(x_{\text{total, wet}} = x_{\text{reaction}} + x_{\text{wet}} = 15.0 \, \text{m} + 90.0 \, \text{m} = 105.0 \, \text{m}\)

Figure 3.23: Braking distances with and without reaction time.

Reaction time adds a significant distance to the total stopping distance, highlighting its importance in road safety.

This part combines two phases of motion: constant velocity and constant acceleration.

Example: Calculating Time to Travel a Distance

Problem: A car merges onto a 200-m ramp. Initial velocity is 10.0 m/s, and acceleration is 2.00 m/s². How long does it take to travel 200 m?

Strategy:

  1. Identify Knowns: \(x_0 = 0\), \(x = 200\) m, \(v_0 = 10.0\) m/s, \(a = 2.00\) m/s².
  2. Identify Unknown: Time, \(t\).
  3. Choose Equation: Use \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\).

Figure 3.24: Car accelerating on a freeway ramp.

The visual helps to set up the coordinate system and understand the values. The chosen equation includes \(t^2\), indicating a quadratic equation will arise.

Example: Solution

Solution:

Substitute known values into \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\): \[ 200 \, \text{m} = 0 + (10.0 \, \text{m/s})t + \frac{1}{2}(2.00 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2 \] Simplify (and temporarily drop units for the quadratic equation): \[ 200 = 10t + t^2 \] Rearrange into standard quadratic form \(at^2 + bt + c = 0\): \[ t^2 + 10t - 200 = 0 \] Use the quadratic formula \(t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\): \[ t = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{(10)^2 - 4(1)(-200)}}{2(1)} \] \[ t = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{100 + 800}}{2} = \frac{-10 \pm \sqrt{900}}{2} = \frac{-10 \pm 30}{2} \] Two solutions:

\(t_1 = \frac{-10 + 30}{2} = \frac{20}{2} = 10.0 \, \text{s}\)

\(t_2 = \frac{-10 - 30}{2} = \frac{-40}{2} = -20.0 \, \text{s}\)

Significance:

A negative time is physically unreasonable in this context, so \(t = 10.0\) s is the correct answer.

Always consider the physical meaning of your mathematical solutions. Discarding negative time values is a common practice in kinematics problems where motion starts at \(t=0\).

Check Your Understanding 3.5

Problem: A rocket accelerates at 20 m/s² during launch. How long does it take the rocket to reach a velocity of 400 m/s?

Given:

  • \(v_0 = 0\) m/s (starts from rest)
  • \(a = 20\) m/s²
  • \(v = 400\) m/s

Formula to use:

\[ v = v_0 + at \]

Encourage students to calculate this.

Solution: \(400 = 0 + 20t \Rightarrow t = 20\) s.

Two-Body Pursuit Problems

These involve two objects whose motions are interconnected.

  • The unknown often depends on the motion of both objects.
  • Strategy: Write separate equations of motion for each object. Then solve them simultaneously for the common unknown (e.g., time or position).

Figure 3.25: Car 1 (accelerating) pursues Car 2 (constant velocity).

The key to two-body problems is identifying a common variable between the two motions, such as time or position, at the point of interest (e.g., when one catches the other).

Example: Cheetah Catching a Gazelle

Problem: A cheetah (\(v_0 = 0\), \(a = 4\) m/s²) spots a gazelle (\(v = 10\) m/s constant) passing at the same instant (\(x_0 = 0\) for both).

  1. How long does it take the cheetah to catch the gazelle?
  2. What is their displacement when the cheetah catches the gazelle?

Strategy:

  • Define equations of motion for both cheetah and gazelle.
  • At the moment the cheetah catches the gazelle, their positions (\(x\)) and times (\(t\)) will be the same.
  • Set their position equations equal to each other to solve for \(t\).

This is a classic pursuit problem. Emphasize that the relative starting position is zero, and the relative final position is zero, meaning their final positions are identical.

Example: Solution (Part a)

Solution (Part a): Time to catch

  1. Equation for Gazelle (constant velocity):

    \(x_{\text{gazelle}} = x_0 + v_0t\)

    Since \(x_0 = 0\) and \(v_0 = \bar{v} = 10\) m/s:

    \[ x_{\text{gazelle}} = (10 \, \text{m/s})t \]

  2. Equation for Cheetah (constant acceleration from rest):

    \(x_{\text{cheetah}} = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

    Since \(x_0 = 0\) and \(v_0 = 0\), and \(a = 4\) m/s²:

    \[ x_{\text{cheetah}} = \frac{1}{2}(4 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2 = (2 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2 \]

  3. Set positions equal and solve for \(t\):

    When the cheetah catches the gazelle, \(x_{\text{cheetah}} = x_{\text{gazelle}}\).

    \[ (10 \, \text{m/s})t = (2 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2 \] \[ 10t = 2t^2 \] \[ 2t^2 - 10t = 0 \] \[ 2t(t - 5) = 0 \]

    This gives two solutions: \(t = 0\) s (the start) and \(t = 5\) s.

    The time it takes to catch the gazelle is \(t = 5\) s.

The \(t=0\) solution simply means they were at the same point at the beginning. The \(t=5\)s solution is when the cheetah actually catches the gazelle.

Example: Solution (Part b)

Solution (Part b): Displacement

To find the displacement, substitute \(t = 5\) s into either equation of motion:

  1. Using Gazelle’s equation: \[ x_{\text{gazelle}} = (10 \, \text{m/s})(5 \, \text{s}) = 50 \, \text{m} \]

  2. Using Cheetah’s equation: \[ x_{\text{cheetah}} = (2 \, \text{m/s}^2)(5 \, \text{s})^2 = (2 \, \text{m/s}^2)(25 \, \text{s}^2) = 50 \, \text{m} \]

Both methods yield the same displacement, as expected.

The cheetah catches the gazelle after both have traveled 50 m.

This confirms the consistency of the solution and the logic of setting the positions equal.

Check Your Understanding 3.6

Problem: A bicycle has a constant velocity of 10 m/s. A person starts from rest and runs to catch up to the bicycle in 30 s when they are at the same position. What is the acceleration of the person?

Given:

  • Bicycle: \(v_B = 10\) m/s (constant)
  • Person: \(v_{P0} = 0\) m/s (from rest), \(t = 30\) s
  • At \(t=30\) s, \(x_B = x_P\) (and assume \(x_{B0}=x_{P0}=0\))

Formulas to use:

  • Bicycle: \(x_B = v_B t\)
  • Person: \(x_P = \frac{1}{2}a_P t^2\)
  • Then set \(x_B = x_P\).

Encourage students to solve this as a two-body pursuit problem. Solution: \(10t = \frac{1}{2}a t^2 \Rightarrow 10(30) = \frac{1}{2}a(30)^2 \Rightarrow 300 = 450a \Rightarrow a = 300/450 = 2/3 \approx 0.67\) m/s².

Key Takeaways

  • Simplified Notation: \(t_0=0\), \(x_0\), \(v_0\) for initial values; \(t, x, v\) for final values.
  • Constant Acceleration: A key assumption, implying \(\bar{a} = a\).
  • Five Kinematic Equations: A set of relationships for motion under constant acceleration:
    1. \(x = x_0 + \bar{v}t\)
    2. \(\bar{v} = \frac{v_0 + v}{2}\)
    3. \(v = v_0 + at\)
    4. \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
    5. \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)\)
  • Problem-Solving Strategy:
    1. Identify knowns and unknowns.
    2. Choose the appropriate equation(s).
    3. Solve algebraically.
    4. Check for reasonableness and physical meaning.
  • Two-Body Pursuit Problems:
    • Write separate equations for each object.
    • Identify a common parameter (usually time or position) to link the equations.
    • Solve simultaneously.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Constant Acceleration A condition where the acceleration of an object remains uniform over time.
Kinematic Equations A set of five equations describing motion under constant acceleration.
Simplified Notation Using subscripts for initial values (\(x_0, v_0\)) and no subscripts for final values (\(x, v, t\)) with \(t_0=0\).
