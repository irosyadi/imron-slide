3.6 Finding Velocity and Displacement from Acceleration

Using Integral Calculus

Today, we’re taking a deeper dive into kinematics, moving beyond the constant acceleration formulas. We’ll use integral calculus to derive these formulas and, more importantly, to analyze motion where acceleration is not constant but a function of time. This is where physics truly merges with advanced mathematics.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Derive the kinematic equations for constant acceleration using integral calculus.
  • Use the integral formulation of the kinematic equations in analyzing motion.
  • Find the functional form of velocity versus time given the acceleration function.
  • Find the functional form of position versus time given the velocity function.

These objectives will extend your analytical toolkit for kinematics. Understanding the integral approach is crucial for more complex, real-world scenarios where acceleration isn’t always a simple constant.

Review: Kinematic Functions and Derivatives

Recall from previous sections:

  • Velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time: \[ v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} \]
  • Acceleration is the derivative of velocity with respect to time: \[ a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt} \]

This means we can also think of acceleration as the second derivative of position: \[ a(t) = \frac{d^2x(t)}{dt^2} \]

These are the fundamental relationships. Now, we’ll reverse this process using integration to go from acceleration back to velocity and then to position.

Kinematic Equations from Integral Calculus

To go “backward” from acceleration to velocity, and from velocity to position, we use integration.

1. From Acceleration to Velocity:

Given \(a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt}\), we integrate both sides with respect to time: \[ \int \frac{dv(t)}{dt} dt = \int a(t) dt \] \[ v(t) = \int a(t) dt + C_1 \] where \(C_1\) is the constant of integration. We can find \(C_1\) using an initial condition, typically \(v(0) = v_0\).

2. From Velocity to Position:

Given \(v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt}\), we integrate both sides with respect to time: \[ \int \frac{dx(t)}{dt} dt = \int v(t) dt \] \[ x(t) = \int v(t) dt + C_2 \] where \(C_2\) is another constant of integration, found using an initial condition, typically \(x(0) = x_0\).

The constants of integration represent the initial velocity and initial position, respectively. These are determined by the specific conditions of the problem.

Deriving Constant Acceleration Equations using Integrals

Let’s derive the familiar kinematic equations assuming constant acceleration, \(a(t) = a\):

Step 1: Find Velocity \(v(t)\) \[ v(t) = \int a dt + C_1 \] Since \(a\) is constant: \[ v(t) = at + C_1 \] To find \(C_1\), use the initial condition: at \(t=0\), \(v(0) = v_0\). \[ v_0 = a(0) + C_1 \implies C_1 = v_0 \] So, the velocity function is: \[ v(t) = v_0 + at \] (This is the familiar \(v = v_0 + at\) equation).

Step 2: Find Position \(x(t)\)

Now integrate the velocity function \(v(t) = v_0 + at\): \[ x(t) = \int (v_0 + at) dt + C_2 \] \[ x(t) = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 + C_2 \] To find \(C_2\), use the initial condition: at \(t=0\), \(x(0) = x_0\). \[ x_0 = v_0(0) + \frac{1}{2}a(0)^2 + C_2 \implies C_2 = x_0 \] So, the position function is: \[ x(t) = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \] (This is the familiar \(x = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\) equation).

This derivation shows how integral calculus provides a more fundamental way to obtain the kinematic equations. It also prepares us for situations where ‘a’ is not constant.

Problem: Motion of a Motorboat (Example 3.17)

A motorboat is traveling at a constant velocity of \(5.0 \text{ m/s}\). At \(t=0\), it starts to accelerate in the opposite direction. Its acceleration is given by: \[ a(t) = -\frac{1}{4}t \text{ m/s}^3 \]

(a) What is the velocity function of the motorboat?
(b) At what time does the velocity reach zero?
(c) What is the position function of the motorboat?
(d) What is the displacement from \(t=0\) to when the velocity is zero?
(e) Graph the velocity and position functions.

This problem has a time-dependent acceleration. We’ll use the integral method to find the velocity and position functions, and then analyze the boat’s motion. The units of acceleration are important here, m/s^3, to ensure consistency after integration.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part a)

(a) What is the velocity function of the motorboat?

Strategy:

  1. Integrate the acceleration function \(a(t)\) to find \(v(t)\).
  2. Use the initial condition \(v(0) = 5.0 \text{ m/s}\) to find the constant of integration.

Solution (a): \[ v(t) = \int a(t) dt + C_1 \] \[ v(t) = \int \left( -\frac{1}{4}t \right) dt + C_1 \] \[ v(t) = -\frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{t^2}{2} \right) + C_1 \] \[ v(t) = -\frac{1}{8}t^2 + C_1 \] Now, apply the initial condition \(v(0) = 5.0 \text{ m/s}\): \[ 5.0 = -\frac{1}{8}(0)^2 + C_1 \implies C_1 = 5.0 \text{ m/s} \] So, the velocity function is: \[ v(t) = 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2 \] (The units are implicitly \(\text{m/s}\) for the constant and \(\text{m/s}^3 \cdot \text{s}^2 = \text{m/s}\) for the second term, so \(v(t)\) is in \(\text{m/s}\)).

Answer (a): \(v(t) = 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2 \text{ (m/s)}\).

Remember to always include the constant of integration after performing indefinite integrals and use initial conditions to solve for it. The units are also crucial to check for consistency.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part b)

(b) At what time does the velocity reach zero?

Strategy: Set \(v(t) = 0\) and solve for \(t\).

Solution (b):

We have \(v(t) = 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2\).

Set \(v(t) = 0\): \[ 0 = 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2 \] \[ \frac{1}{8}t^2 = 5.0 \] \[ t^2 = 5.0 \times 8 = 40.0 \] \[ t = \sqrt{40.0} \approx 6.32 \text{ s} \]

(We take the positive root for time).

Answer (b): The velocity reaches zero at approximately \(6.3 \text{ s}\).

This is the moment when the boat momentarily stops before potentially reversing direction if the acceleration continues. This point is often a critical analysis point in physics problems.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part c)

(c) What is the position function of the motorboat?

Strategy:

  1. Integrate the velocity function \(v(t)\) to find \(x(t)\).
  2. Assume the initial position \(x(0) = 0\) (starting point) to find the constant of integration.

Solution (c): \[ x(t) = \int v(t) dt + C_2 \] \[ x(t) = \int \left( 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2 \right) dt + C_2 \] \[ x(t) = 5.0t - \frac{1}{8} \left( \frac{t^3}{3} \right) + C_2 \] \[ x(t) = 5.0t - \frac{1}{24}t^3 + C_2 \] Now, apply the initial condition \(x(0) = 0\): \[ 0 = 5.0(0) - \frac{1}{24}(0)^3 + C_2 \implies C_2 = 0 \] So, the position function is: \[ x(t) = 5.0t - \frac{1}{24}t^3 \]

(The units are implicitly meters).

Answer (c): \(x(t) = 5.0t - \frac{1}{24}t^3 \text{ (m)}\).

We assumed \(x(0)=0\) which is a common and convenient choice when analyzing displacement from the start of the motion.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part d)

(d) What is the displacement of the motorboat from \(t=0\) to when the velocity is zero?

Strategy: Evaluate the position function \(x(t)\) at the time when \(v(t)=0\) (which we found in part b).

Solution (d):

From part (b), velocity reaches zero at \(t \approx 6.32 \text{ s}\).

Substitute \(t = 6.32 \text{ s}\) into the position function \(x(t) = 5.0t - \frac{1}{24}t^3\): \[ x(6.32) = 5.0(6.32) - \frac{1}{24}(6.32)^3 \] \[ x(6.32) = 31.6 - \frac{1}{24}(252.88) \] \[ x(6.32) = 31.6 - 10.54 \approx 21.06 \text{ m} \]

Answer (d): The displacement is approximately \(21.1 \text{ m}\).

This displacement represents the maximum distance the boat travels in the positive direction before it begins to move backward if the acceleration pattern continues.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part e)

(e) Graph the velocity and position functions.

Significance:

Check Your Understanding 3.8

Problem: A particle starts from rest and has an acceleration function: \[ a(t) = \left( 5 - \frac{1}{10}t \right) \text{ m/s}^2 \]

(a) What is the velocity function?
(b) What is the position function?
(c) When is the velocity zero?

This is a similar problem to the motorboat, but with a different form of time-dependent acceleration. Apply the same integral method. Remember to use “starts from rest” for initial velocity and assume initial position is zero.

Check Your Understanding 3.8 - Solution

Given:

  • Starts from rest: \(v(0) = 0\).
  • Acceleration function: \(a(t) = \left( 5 - \frac{1}{10}t \right)\).
  • Assume \(x(0) = 0\).

(a) Velocity function: \[ v(t) = \int a(t) dt + C_1 = \int \left( 5 - \frac{1}{10}t \right) dt + C_1 \] \[ v(t) = 5t - \frac{1}{10}\frac{t^2}{2} + C_1 = 5t - \frac{1}{20}t^2 + C_1 \] Using \(v(0) = 0\): \[ 0 = 5(0) - \frac{1}{20}(0)^2 + C_1 \implies C_1 = 0 \]

Answer (a): \(v(t) = 5t - \frac{1}{20}t^2 \text{ (m/s)}\).

(b) Position function:

\[ x(t) = \int v(t) dt + C_2 = \int \left( 5t - \frac{1}{20}t^2 \right) dt + C_2 \] \[ x(t) = 5\frac{t^2}{2} - \frac{1}{20}\frac{t^3}{3} + C_2 = \frac{5}{2}t^2 - \frac{1}{60}t^3 + C_2 \] Using \(x(0) = 0\): \[ 0 = \frac{5}{2}(0)^2 - \frac{1}{60}(0)^3 + C_2 \implies C_2 = 0 \] Answer (b): \(x(t) = \frac{5}{2}t^2 - \frac{1}{60}t^3 \text{ (m)}\).

(c) When is the velocity zero?

Set \(v(t) = 0\): \[ 0 = 5t - \frac{1}{20}t^2 \] \[ t \left( 5 - \frac{1}{20}t \right) = 0 \] Two solutions: \(t=0\) (at rest initially) or \(5 - \frac{1}{20}t = 0\). \[ \frac{1}{20}t = 5 \implies t = 100 \text{ s} \]

Answer (c): The velocity is zero at \(t=0 \text{ s}\) and \(t=100 \text{ s}\).

The interpretation of the two times when velocity is zero is crucial: \(t=0\) is the start from rest, and \(t=100s\) is when the particle momentarily stops before potentially reversing direction due to the changing acceleration.

Key Takeaways

  • Integral Calculus is used to determine velocity from acceleration, and position from velocity.
  • Integration of Acceleration: \(v(t) = \int a(t) dt + C_1\). The constant \(C_1\) is the initial velocity (\(v_0\)).
  • Integration of Velocity: \(x(t) = \int v(t) dt + C_2\). The constant \(C_2\) is the initial position (\(x_0\)).
  • This method can derive the kinematic equations for constant acceleration by treating ‘a’ as a constant in the integration.
  • For time-dependent acceleration, this integral method is essential for finding the velocity and position functions.
  • Interpreting the constants of integration as initial conditions is fundamental to solving problems.

This section bridges your calculus knowledge with physics. Understanding how to use integration to move between kinematics functions is a powerful skill for solving more advanced problems in engineering and physics.

Key Equations (Integral Kinematics)

Equation Description
\(v(t) = \int a(t) dt + C_1\) Velocity as integral of acceleration
\(x(t) = \int v(t) dt + C_2\) Position as integral of velocity
\(v(0) = v_0\) Initial velocity (to find \(C_1\))
\(x(0) = x_0\) Initial position (to find \(C_2\))

These equations are the mathematical foundation for analyzing non-constant acceleration. Practice applying them correctly to various functional forms of acceleration.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Integral Calculus A branch of calculus concerned with the accumulation of quantities, used to find functions from their rates of change.
Indefinite Integral The antiderivative of a function, which includes a constant of integration.
Constant of Integration An arbitrary constant introduced when finding an indefinite integral, determined by initial conditions.
Functional Form The mathematical expression (equation) that describes how a variable (e.g., velocity, position) changes with time.

A clear understanding of these calculus terms is necessary for correctly applying the integral method to kinematic problems.
