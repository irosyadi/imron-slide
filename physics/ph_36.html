Velocity and Displacement from Acceleration: The Calculus Approach

Good morning/afternoon, everyone. In our previous sessions, we focused on motion with constant acceleration, which allowed us to use simple algebraic kinematic equations. Today, we’re taking a step further, addressing situations where acceleration itself might change over time. This requires a more powerful mathematical tool: integral calculus. For engineering, understanding this connection between calculus and motion is essential for analyzing more complex, real-world systems.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Derive the kinematic equations for constant acceleration using integral calculus.
  • Use the integral formulation of kinematic equations in analyzing motion where acceleration is not constant.
  • Find the functional form of velocity versus time given the acceleration function.
  • Find the functional form of position versus time given the velocity function.

Our learning objectives reflect this shift. We’ll revisit the derivations of constant acceleration equations using integration, but more importantly, we’ll extend this method to handle variable acceleration. This means learning how to integrate an acceleration function to get velocity, and then integrating the velocity function to get position.

Calculus in Kinematics: A Recap

Recall the fundamental relationships between position, velocity, and acceleration using derivatives:

  • Velocity is the time derivative of position: \[ v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} \]
  • Acceleration is the time derivative of velocity: \[ a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt} = \frac{d^2x(t)}{dt^2} \]

Note

Working Backwards with Integrals: To go from acceleration to velocity, or from velocity to position, we use the inverse operation: integration.

This flowchart summarizes the derivative relationships we’ve already covered. Today, we’re focusing on the reverse direction: using integration. If differentiation tells us the rate of change, integration tells us the accumulation or total effect of that rate of change over time.

Deriving Velocity from Acceleration

Given an acceleration function \(a(t)\), we can find the velocity function \(v(t)\) by integrating \(a(t)\) with respect to time:

\[ \frac{dv(t)}{dt} = a(t) \] \[ \int \frac{dv(t)}{dt} dt = \int a(t) dt \] \[ v(t) = \int a(t) dt + C_1 \]

Where \(C_1\) is the constant of integration, determined by the initial velocity.

\[ v(t) = \int a(t) dt + v_0 \]

Tip

Finding the Constant of Integration (\(C_1\)):

If \(v(0) = v_0\) (initial velocity at \(t=0\)), then \(v_0 = \int a(0) d(0) + C_1 \implies C_1 = v_0\).

This is the core idea: integration of acceleration yields velocity. The constant of integration, C1, is physically significant; it represents the velocity at time t=0, our initial velocity. So, if we know how acceleration changes over time, and we know the object’s starting velocity, we can determine its velocity at any future time.

Deriving Position from Velocity

Similarly, given a velocity function \(v(t)\), we can find the position function \(x(t)\) by integrating \(v(t)\) with respect to time:

\[ \frac{dx(t)}{dt} = v(t) \] \[ \int \frac{dx(t)}{dt} dt = \int v(t) dt \] \[ x(t) = \int v(t) dt + C_2 \]

Where \(C_2\) is the constant of integration, determined by the initial position.

\[ x(t) = \int v(t) dt + x_0 \]

Tip

Finding the Constant of Integration (\(C_2\)):

If \(x(0) = x_0\) (initial position at \(t=0\)), then \(x_0 = \int v(0) d(0) + C_2 \implies C_2 = x_0\).

And here’s the second step: integration of velocity yields position. Again, the constant of integration, C2, is physically significant, representing the initial position. So, if we have the velocity function and the starting position, we can track the object’s position over time.

Deriving Constant Acceleration Equations with Calculus

Let’s use these integral forms to re-derive our familiar constant acceleration equations.

Assume \(a(t) = a\) (a constant).

Step 1: Find \(v(t)\)

\[ v(t) = \int a \, dt + C_1 = at + C_1 \]

At \(t=0\), \(v(0) = v_0 \implies C_1 = v_0\).

Thus: \[ \mathbf{v(t) = v_0 + at} \] (Our first kinematic equation!)

Step 2: Find \(x(t)\)

\[ x(t) = \int v(t) \, dt + C_2 = \int (v_0 + at) \, dt + C_2 \] \[ x(t) = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 + C_2 \]

At \(t=0\), \(x(0) = x_0 \implies C_2 = x_0\).

Thus: \[ \mathbf{x(t) = x_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2} \]

(Our second kinematic equation!)

This slide elegantly shows how the kinematic equations we’ve been using are simply special cases of these more general integral relationships, specifically when acceleration is constant. It reinforces the power of calculus in unifying these concepts. For engineers, understanding this derivation means you’re not just memorizing formulas, but understanding their fundamental origins.

Example 3.17: Motion of a Motorboat

A motorboat is traveling at a constant velocity of \(5.0 \text{ m/s}\). It then starts to accelerate opposite to its motion to arrive at the dock. Its acceleration is given by: \[ a(t) = -\frac{1}{4}t \text{ m/s}^3 \]

Assume \(t=0\) when it starts accelerating.

  1. What is the velocity function of the motorboat?
  2. At what time does the velocity reach zero?
  3. What is the position function of the motorboat?
  4. What is the displacement of the motorboat from the time it begins to accelerate to when the velocity is zero?

Note

Initial conditions: \(v(0) = 5.0 \text{ m/s}\), assume \(x(0) = 0 \text{ m}\).

Here we have an acceleration that is not constant; it’s a function of time. This is where the integral approach really shines. This problem will walk us through finding the velocity and position functions and then using them to answer questions about the boat’s motion.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part 1/3)

1. Velocity Function \(v(t)\)

Given \(a(t) = -\frac{1}{4}t\). Integrate to find \(v(t)\): \[ v(t) = \int a(t) dt = \int \left(-\frac{1}{4}t\right) dt + C_1 \] \[ v(t) = -\frac{1}{8}t^2 + C_1 \]

Use initial condition \(v(0) = 5.0 \text{ m/s}\): \[ 5.0 = -\frac{1}{8}(0)^2 + C_1 \implies C_1 = 5.0 \text{ m/s} \] Thus: \[ \mathbf{v(t) = 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2 \text{ m/s}} \] (Units are simplified for clarity, assuming \(t\) in seconds).

2. Time When Velocity Reaches Zero

Set \(v(t) = 0\): \[ 0 = 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2 \] \[ \frac{1}{8}t^2 = 5.0 \] \[ t^2 = 40 \] \[ t = \sqrt{40} \approx \mathbf{6.32 \text{ s}} \]

First, we perform the indefinite integral of the acceleration function. Then, we use the given initial velocity at t=0 to solve for the integration constant. With the velocity function in hand, finding when the velocity is zero is a simple algebraic step.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part 2/3)

3. Position Function \(x(t)\)

Given \(v(t) = 5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2\). Integrate to find \(x(t)\): \[ x(t) = \int v(t) dt = \int \left(5.0 - \frac{1}{8}t^2\right) dt + C_2 \] \[ x(t) = 5.0t - \frac{1}{24}t^3 + C_2 \]

Use initial condition \(x(0) = 0 \text{ m}\) (boat starts accelerating from this reference point): \[ 0 = 5.0(0) - \frac{1}{24}(0)^3 + C_2 \implies C_2 = 0 \text{ m} \] Thus: \[ \mathbf{x(t) = 5.0t - \frac{1}{24}t^3 \text{ m}} \]

Following the same process, we integrate the velocity function we just found. Since we’re interested in displacement from the start of acceleration, we set the initial position to zero, making the integration constant also zero.

Solution: Motorboat Motion (Part 3/3)

4. Displacement when Velocity is Zero

The velocity reaches zero at \(t \approx 6.32 \text{ s}\). Substitute this time into the position function \(x(t)\):

\[ x(6.32) = 5.0(6.32) - \frac{1}{24}(6.32)^3 \] \[ x(6.32) \approx 31.6 - \frac{1}{24}(252.88) \] \[ x(6.32) \approx 31.6 - 10.54 \] \[ \mathbf{x(6.32) \approx 21.06 \text{ m}} \] (The displacement is approximately \(21.1 \text{ m}\)).

Significance:

  • The acceleration is linear, leading to a quadratic velocity and cubic position function.
  • If the acceleration continued, the boat would reverse direction and eventually pass its starting point.
  • This method is crucial for systems with non-constant forces, common in advanced engineering dynamics.

Finally, to find the displacement, we simply evaluate the position function at the time when the velocity becomes zero. This type of analysis is common in engineering, for example, in designing control systems where acceleration might be modulated, or in modeling the trajectory of objects under complex forces. The graphs provided in the original text show that if the acceleration continued, the velocity would turn negative, and the boat would start moving backward, passing its original position.

Check Your Understanding: Particle Motion with Variable Acceleration

A particle starts from rest (\(v(0) = 0\)) and has an acceleration function: \[ a(t) = (5 - \frac{1}{2}t) \text{ m/s}^2 \]

  1. What is the velocity function?
  2. What is the position function?
  3. When is the velocity zero (other than at \(t=0\))?

Assume \(x(0) = 0 \text{ m}\).

This problem is very similar to the motorboat example, but with a different acceleration function and initial conditions. Apply the same integral approach to find the velocity and position functions, and then determine when the velocity becomes zero. Remember your constants of integration!

Solution: Particle Motion with Variable Acceleration

1. Velocity Function \(v(t)\)

Given \(a(t) = 5 - \frac{1}{2}t\). Integrate: \[ v(t) = \int \left(5 - \frac{1}{2}t\right) dt + C_1 = 5t - \frac{1}{4}t^2 + C_1 \] Given \(v(0) = 0\): \[ 0 = 5(0) - \frac{1}{4}(0)^2 + C_1 \implies C_1 = 0 \] Thus: \[ \mathbf{v(t) = 5t - \frac{1}{4}t^2 \text{ m/s}} \]

2. Position Function \(x(t)\)

Given \(v(t) = 5t - \frac{1}{4}t^2\). Integrate: \[ x(t) = \int \left(5t - \frac{1}{4}t^2\right) dt + C_2 = \frac{5}{2}t^2 - \frac{1}{12}t^3 + C_2 \] Given \(x(0) = 0\): \[ 0 = \frac{5}{2}(0)^2 - \frac{1}{12}(0)^3 + C_2 \implies C_2 = 0 \] Thus: \[ \mathbf{x(t) = \frac{5}{2}t^2 - \frac{1}{12}t^3 \text{ m}} \]

For the velocity and position functions, the integration steps are straightforward polynomial integrations. The initial conditions simplify the constants of integration to zero in this particular case.

Solution: Particle Motion with Variable Acceleration (Cont.)

3. When is the velocity zero (other than at \(t=0\))?

Set \(v(t) = 0\): \[ 0 = 5t - \frac{1}{4}t^2 \] Factor out \(t\): \[ 0 = t \left(5 - \frac{1}{4}t\right) \] This gives two solutions: - \(t = 0 \text{ s}\) (initial condition) - $5 - t = 0 t = 5 t = $

The velocity is zero at \(t = \mathbf{20 \text{ s}}\).

Tip

Interpretation: The particle speeds up from \(t=0\) until \(t=20 \text{ s}\), then starts to slow down and eventually reverses direction if the acceleration function remains valid.

Solving for when velocity is zero again involves an algebraic step. The two solutions indicate that the particle starts at rest and then comes to rest again at 20 seconds. If the acceleration continued in the same manner, the particle would then begin to move in the opposite direction. This problem showcases how calculus enables us to fully characterize the motion of objects even under more complex, time-varying accelerations, which is critical for realistic engineering models.

Key Takeaways

  • Calculus as a Tool: Integration allows us to find velocity from acceleration, and position from velocity, especially when acceleration is not constant.
  • Fundamental Relationships:
    • \(v(t) = \int a(t) dt + v_0\)
    • \(x(t) = \int v(t) dt + x_0\)
  • Initial Conditions: Constants of integration (\(v_0\) and \(x_0\)) are determined by the initial velocity and initial position, respectively.
  • Beyond Constant Acceleration: This integral method extends kinematics to scenarios with more complex, time-dependent accelerations, which are common in real-world engineering systems.

These key takeaways summarize the power and necessity of calculus in kinematics. While constant acceleration equations are useful, the integral approach provides the flexibility to analyze any acceleration profile. Understanding how to set up and solve these integrals, and how to use initial conditions to define the complete motion, is a fundamental skill for any engineer.
