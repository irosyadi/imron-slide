3.5 Free Fall

Motion Under Gravity

Today, we’re diving into a very common and important application of constant acceleration: free fall. This applies to any object moving under the sole influence of gravity, whether falling down or thrown upwards.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Use the kinematic equations with the variables y and g to analyze free-fall motion.
  • Describe how the values of the position, velocity, and acceleration change during a free fall.
  • Solve for the position, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time when an object is in a free fall.

These objectives will equip you to tackle a wide range of problems involving objects moving vertically under gravity. We’ll refine our use of kinematic equations specifically for this scenario.

What is Free Fall?

  • Free fall describes the motion of an object falling solely under the influence of gravity.
  • Key assumptions:
    • Air resistance is negligible.
    • Friction is negligible.
    • Motion is one-dimensional (straight up and down).
  • “Falling” doesn’t necessarily mean moving downwards. An object thrown upward is also in free fall once released.
  • The instant an object leaves contact with whatever held or threw it, it is in free fall.
  • Once it hits the ground or another object, it is no longer in free fall.

Think of it as pure gravitational motion. The moment you let go of something, or throw it, gravity is the only significant force influencing its vertical motion. We often simplify by neglecting air resistance for these early calculations, which is reasonable for dense objects over short distances.

The Universal Nature of Gravity

  • A remarkable fact: if air resistance and friction are negligible, all objects fall toward the center of Earth with the same constant acceleration, regardless of their mass.
  • This was famously demonstrated by Galileo Galilei, and later by astronaut David R. Scott on the Moon.

Figure 3.26: Hammer and feather falling in air vs. vacuum.

Note

In the real world, air resistance causes lighter, less dense objects to fall slower. Our free-fall model ignores this for simplicity.

This is a cornerstone concept in physics. It’s counter-intuitive because our everyday experience is dominated by air resistance. But in a vacuum, a feather and a bowling ball fall at the same rate.

Acceleration Due to Gravity (\(g\))

  • The constant acceleration of free-falling objects is called acceleration due to gravity.
  • Its magnitude is denoted by \(g\).
  • On Earth’s surface, the average value is: \[ g = 9.81 \text{ m/s}^2 \quad (\text{or } 32.2 \text{ ft/s}^2) \]
  • For simplicity, we will use \(g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\) unless specified otherwise.
  • The direction of acceleration due to gravity is always downward (towards the center of Earth). This direction defines “vertical.”

Important

The sign of \(g\) in kinematic equations depends on your chosen coordinate system:

  • If upward is positive, then \(a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\).
  • If downward is positive, then \(a = +g = +9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\). Choose a consistent coordinate system and stick to it!

The choice of positive direction is arbitrary but consistency is key. Most problems default to upward as positive, so ‘g’ often appears with a negative sign.

Kinematic Equations for Objects in Free Fall

We use the same kinematic equations for constant acceleration, but substitute \(y\) for \(x\) and \(a\) with either \(+g\) or \(-g\).

Assuming upward is positive, so \(a = -g\):

  1. \(v = v_0 - gt\)
  2. \(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)
  3. \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\)

Tip

These are simply the constant acceleration equations with vertical displacement (\(y\)) and acceleration (\(a=-g\)). Remember, if you define downward as positive, then \(a=+g\).

These equations are fundamental for analyzing vertical motion. It’s crucial to understand the implications of the signs and the direction of your chosen coordinate system.

Problem-Solving Strategy: Free Fall

  1. Define your coordinate system: Choose which direction is positive (usually upward) and state it clearly. This determines the sign of \(g\).
  2. Draw a sketch: Visualize the motion and label initial/final positions, velocities, and the direction of acceleration.
  3. Record knowns and unknowns: List all given values and what you need to find.
  4. Select appropriate equations: Choose from the kinematic equations those that contain your knowns and the single unknown you want to solve for. You may need to use multiple equations.

This structured approach helps minimize errors and ensures you apply the principles correctly. A clear diagram is often the most helpful first step.

Problem: Free Fall of a Ball (Example 3.14)

A ball is thrown downward from the top of a \(98 \text{ m}\) high building with an initial velocity of \(4.9 \text{ m/s}\) downward.

(a) How much time elapses before the ball reaches the ground? (b) What is the velocity when it arrives at the ground?

Figure 3.27: Ball thrown downward from a building.

Here, the ball is thrown downwards, so its initial velocity will be negative if we choose upward as positive. The final position will also be negative.

Solution: Free Fall of a Ball (Part a)

Strategy:

  1. Coordinate system: Let upward be positive (+y). Origin (\(y_0=0\)) at the top of the building.
  2. Knowns:
    • Initial position (\(y_0 = 0 \text{ m}\))
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0 = -4.9 \text{ m/s}\)) (downward is negative)
    • Acceleration (\(a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\))
    • Final position (\(y = -98 \text{ m}\)) (ground is 98 m below origin)
  3. Unknown: Time (\(t\)).
  4. Equation: \(y = y_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \Rightarrow y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\).

Solution (a): \[ -98 = 0 + (-4.9)t - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \] \[ -98 = -4.9t - 4.9t^2 \] Rearrange into a quadratic equation: \[ 4.9t^2 + 4.9t - 98 = 0 \] Divide by 4.9: \[ t^2 + t - 20 = 0 \] Using quadratic formula \(t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\): \[ t = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1^2 - 4(1)(-20)}}{2(1)} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 80}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{81}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm 9}{2} \] Solutions: \(t = \frac{-1 + 9}{2} = 4 \text{ s}\) or \(t = \frac{-1 - 9}{2} = -5 \text{ s}\). We choose the positive physically meaningful time.

Answer (a): The ball reaches the ground in \(4.0 \text{ s}\).

The negative time solution \((-5s)\) corresponds to when the ball would have been at \(y=0\) if it had been thrown upward from the ground and then passed the top of the building at \(4.9 m/s\) downwards. For our scenario, only the positive time is relevant.

Solution: Free Fall of a Ball (Part b)

Strategy (b):

  1. Knowns: From part (a), \(v_0 = -4.9 \text{ m/s}\), \(a = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\), \(t = 4.0 \text{ s}\).
  2. Unknown: Final velocity (\(v\)).
  3. Equation: \(v = v_0 + at \Rightarrow v = v_0 - gt\).

Solution (b): \[ v = -4.9 \text{ m/s} - (9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(4.0 \text{ s}) \] \[ v = -4.9 \text{ m/s} - 39.2 \text{ m/s} \] \[ v = -44.1 \text{ m/s} \]

Answer (b): The velocity when it arrives at the ground is \(-44.1 \text{ m/s}\).

The negative sign confirms that the ball is moving downward when it hits the ground. This problem emphasizes the importance of a consistent coordinate system and careful sign convention.

Problem: Vertical Motion of a Baseball (Example 3.15)

A batter hits a baseball straight upward. The ball is caught \(5.0 \text{ s}\) after it is struck, at the same location.

(a) What is the initial velocity of the ball?
(b) What is the maximum height the ball reaches?
(c) How long does it take to reach the maximum height?
(d) What is the acceleration at the top of its path?
(e) What is the velocity of the ball when it is caught?

Figure 3.28: Baseball hit straight up.

This is a classic free-fall problem involving symmetrical motion (up and down to the same height). We’ll analyze different points in its trajectory.

Solution: Vertical Motion of a Baseball (Part a, b, c)

Strategy:

  1. Coordinate system: Upward is positive (+y). Origin (\(y_0=0\)) at home plate.
  2. Knowns: \(y_0 = 0\), \(y = 0\) (caught at same height), \(t = 5.0 \text{ s}\), \(a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\).

(a) Initial velocity (\(v_0\)): Use \(y = y_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\): \[ 0 = 0 + v_0(5.0) - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(5.0)^2 \] \[ 0 = 5.0v_0 - 4.9(25.0) \] \[ 0 = 5.0v_0 - 122.5 \] \[ v_0 = \frac{122.5}{5.0} = 24.5 \text{ m/s} \] Answer (a): Initial velocity is \(24.5 \text{ m/s}\).

(b) Maximum height (\(y_{max}\)): At max height, \(v=0\).

Use \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\): \[ 0^2 = (24.5)^2 - 2(9.8)(y_{max} - 0) \] \[ 0 = 600.25 - 19.6 y_{max} \] \[ y_{max} = \frac{600.25}{19.6} = 30.6 \text{ m} \] Answer (b): Maximum height is \(30.6 \text{ m}\).

(c) Time to reach maximum height (\(t_{max}\)): At max height, \(v=0\).

Use \(v = v_0 - gt\): \[ 0 = 24.5 - 9.8 t_{max} \] \[ t_{max} = \frac{24.5}{9.8} = 2.5 \text{ s} \] Answer (c): Time to reach maximum height is \(2.5 \text{ s}\).

Notice how the time to reach maximum height (2.5s) is exactly half the total flight time (5.0s). This is a characteristic of symmetrical free-fall motion starting and ending at the same height.

Solution: Vertical Motion of a Baseball (Part d, e)

(d) Acceleration at the top of its path:

  • Throughout free fall, the acceleration due to gravity is constant.

Answer (d): The acceleration at the top of its path is \(-9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\).

(Even when \(v=0\), the velocity is still changing, meaning acceleration is still present.)

(e) Velocity of the ball when it is caught:

Use \(v = v_0 - gt\): \[ v = 24.5 \text{ m/s} - (9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(5.0 \text{ s}) \] \[ v = 24.5 \text{ m/s} - 49.0 \text{ m/s} = -24.5 \text{ m/s} \]

Answer (e): The velocity when caught is \(-24.5 \text{ m/s}\).

The ball returns with the same speed but opposite direction as its initial velocity. This is another key property of symmetrical free fall where air resistance is negligible. This is a very important concept for understanding projectile motion.

Check Your Understanding 3.7

Problem: A chunk of ice breaks off a glacier and falls \(30.0 \text{ m}\) before it hits the water. Assuming it falls freely (no air resistance):

(a) How long does it take to hit the water?
(b) Which quantity increases faster, the speed of the ice chunk or its distance traveled?

Here, we’re dropping an object, so initial velocity is zero. We need to find time and compare the rate of change of speed vs. distance.

Check Your Understanding 3.7 - Solution

Strategy:

  1. Coordinate system: Let downward be positive (+y). Origin (\(y_0=0\)) at the point where the ice breaks off.
  2. Knowns: \(y_0 = 0\), \(y = 30.0 \text{ m}\), \(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\), \(a = +g = +9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\).

(a) How long does it take to hit the water? Use \(y = y_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\): \[ 30.0 = 0 + (0)t + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \] \[ 30.0 = 4.9t^2 \] \[ t^2 = \frac{30.0}{4.9} \approx 6.12 \] \[ t = \sqrt{6.12} \approx 2.47 \text{ s} \]

Answer (a): It takes approximately \(2.47 \text{ s}\) to hit the water.

(b) Which quantity increases faster, the speed of the ice chunk or its distance traveled?

  • Speed: \(v = v_0 + gt = gt\) (linear increase with time).
  • Distance traveled: \(y = y_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2 = \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) (quadratic increase with time).

A quadratic function increases faster than a linear function for \(t > 0\).

Answer (b): The distance traveled increases faster.

This distinction is important. While speed increases steadily, the accumulated distance grows much more rapidly due to the accelerating motion.

Example 3.16: Rocket Booster

A small rocket with a booster blasts off and heads straight upward. When at a height of \(5.0 \text{ km}\) and velocity of \(200.0 \text{ m/s}\), it releases its booster.

(a) What is the maximum height the booster attains (relative to the release point)?
(b) What is the velocity of the booster at a height of \(6.0 \text{ km}\) (relative to Earth’s surface)?

Figure 3.29: Rocket releasing its booster.

This problem introduces an initial altitude. We need to be careful with our coordinate system and reference points. For part (a), the origin can be the release point. For part (b), we’ll need to consider the height from Earth’s surface.

Solution: Rocket Booster (Part a)

Strategy (a): Max height relative to release point

  1. Coordinate system: Let upward be positive (+y). Origin (\(y_0=0\)) at the booster release point (\(5.0 \text{ km}\) above Earth).
  2. Knowns:
    • Initial position (\(y_0 = 0 \text{ m}\))
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0 = 200.0 \text{ m/s}\))
    • Acceleration (\(a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\))
    • At max height, final velocity (\(v = 0 \text{ m/s}\)).
  3. Unknown: Maximum height (\(y_{max}\)).
  4. Equation: \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\).

Solution (a):

\[ 0^2 = (200.0)^2 - 2(9.8)(y_{max} - 0) \] \[ 0 = 40000 - 19.6 y_{max} \] \[ y_{max} = \frac{40000}{19.6} \approx 2040.8 \text{ m} \approx 2.04 \text{ km} \]

The maximum height the booster attains relative to the release point is \(2.04 \text{ km}\).

The maximum height from Earth’s surface is \(5.0 \text{ km} + 2.04 \text{ km} = 7.04 \text{ km}\).

Answer (a): The maximum height the booster attains is approximately \(2.04 \text{ km}\) above the release point (or \(7.04 \text{ km}\) above Earth’s surface).

It’s important to be clear about the reference frame. The question specified “attains”, so the maximum height above the ground would be the release height plus the calculated additional height.

Solution: Rocket Booster (Part b)

Strategy (b): Velocity at \(6.0 \text{ km}\) from Earth’s surface

  1. Coordinate system: Upward is positive (+y). Origin (\(y_0=0\)) at the booster release point.
  2. Knowns:
    • Initial position (\(y_0 = 0 \text{ m}\))
    • Initial velocity (\(v_0 = 200.0 \text{ m/s}\))
    • Acceleration (\(a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\))
    • Target height: \(6.0 \text{ km}\) from Earth. Since the release point is \(5.0 \text{ km}\) from Earth, the target height relative to the release point is \(y = 6.0 \text{ km} - 5.0 \text{ km} = 1.0 \text{ km} = 1000 \text{ m}\).
  3. Unknown: Velocity (\(v\)) at this height.
  4. Equation: \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\).

Solution (b):

\[ v^2 = (200.0 \text{ m/s})^2 - 2(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(1000 \text{ m} - 0) \] \[ v^2 = 40000 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 - 19600 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 \] \[ v^2 = 20400 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2 \] \[ v = \pm \sqrt{20400 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2} \approx \pm 142.8 \text{ m/s} \]

Answer (b): The velocity of the booster at \(6.0 \text{ km}\) is approximately \(\pm 142.8 \text{ m/s}\).

The \(\pm\) result is significant. The positive value (\(+142.8 \text{ m/s}\)) corresponds to the booster’s velocity as it moves upward through \(6.0 \text{ km}\). The negative value (\(-142.8 \text{ m/s}\)) corresponds to its velocity as it moves downward through \(6.0 \text{ km}\) after reaching its peak. This correctly accounts for the two times the booster will be at that altitude.

Key Takeaways

  • Free fall is motion solely under gravity, neglecting air resistance.
  • All objects in free fall accelerate at \(g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\) (downward) near Earth’s surface, regardless of mass.
  • The kinematic equations for constant acceleration apply, with y for displacement and a = ±g (depending on coordinate system).
  • A consistent coordinate system (e.g., upward positive) is critical for correctly handling signs of velocity, displacement, and acceleration.
  • At the peak of vertical trajectory, velocity is momentarily zero, but acceleration is still g (downward).
  • Objects returning to their starting height have the same speed but opposite velocity direction.

These are the core principles for understanding free fall. Engineering applications range from designing amusement park rides to analyzing projectile trajectories.

Key Equations (Free Fall)

Assuming upward is positive (\(a = -g\)):

Equation Description
\(v = v_0 - gt\) Final velocity based on initial, gravity, time
\(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) Final position based on initial, velocity, gravity, time
\(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\) Final velocity based on initial, gravity, displacement

These specific forms of the kinematic equations are your tools for free-fall problems. Be careful with the signs based on your chosen coordinate system.

Key Terms

Term Definition
Free Fall The motion of an object solely under the influence of gravity, neglecting air resistance and friction.
Acceleration due to Gravity (\(g\)) The constant acceleration experienced by objects in free fall near Earth’s surface, approximately \(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\).
Coordinate System A framework used to specify the position and orientation of objects in space, crucial for determining signs of vectors.
Peak Height The maximum vertical position reached by an object in free fall, where its instantaneous vertical velocity is zero.

A solid grasp of these terms is essential for accurate problem-solving and communication in physics and engineering.
