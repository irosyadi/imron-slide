3.5 Free Fall

Motion Under Gravity

Introduction to Free Fall

Free fall is the motion of an object solely under the influence of gravity.

  • Occurs near planetary surfaces where gravity is significant.
  • Assumes motion is one-dimensional (straight up or down).
  • Applies to objects falling downward and objects thrown upward.

Note

When an object is in “free fall,” it means that the only force acting on it is gravity. Air resistance and friction are assumed to be negligible.

Gravity: A Remarkable Fact

Galileo Galilei proved that, neglecting air resistance:

  • All objects fall with the same constant acceleration.
  • This acceleration is independent of their mass.

Figure 3.26: Hammer and feather falling in air vs. vacuum. The Moon demo shows they fall at the same rate without air.

In a vacuum, a feather and a bowling ball dropped from the same height will hit the ground simultaneously. This is because gravity accelerates all masses equally. Air resistance is what makes lighter objects fall slower in everyday situations.

Acceleration Due to Gravity (\(g\))

For objects in free fall near Earth’s surface, the acceleration is constant.

  • It’s called acceleration due to gravity.
  • Its magnitude is denoted by \(g\).
  • Average value: \(g = 9.81 \, \text{m/s}^2\) (or \(32.2 \, \text{ft/s}^2\)).
  • For most problems, we round to \(g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).

Direction of \(g\):

  • Always directed downward (towards the center of Earth).
  • The sign of \(g\) in equations depends on your chosen coordinate system.
    • If upward is positive, then \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).
    • If downward is positive, then \(a = +g = +9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).

One-Dimensional Motion Involving Gravity

We use the kinematic equations, replacing \(x\) with \(y\) for vertical motion, and \(a\) with \(-g\) (if upward is positive).

Assumptions for free fall:

  • Straight up-and-down motion.
  • No air resistance or friction.
  • Object is in free fall once released or thrown.

Key points:

  • If dropped, initial velocity \(v_0 = 0\).
  • If thrown, \(v_0\) is the speed at release.
  • Motion ends when object contacts ground or another object.

Kinematic Equations for Free Fall (Upward Positive)

When we define the upward direction as positive, then acceleration \(a = -g\). The kinematic equations become:

  1. \(v = v_0 - gt\)
  2. \(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)
  3. \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\)

These are simply the general kinematic equations with \(x\) replaced by \(y\) and \(a\) replaced by \(-g\). It’s crucial to be consistent with the chosen coordinate system throughout the problem.

Problem-Solving Strategy for Free Fall

  1. Define Coordinate System: Choose a positive direction (up or down).
    • This determines the sign of \(g\).
  2. Draw a Sketch: Visualize the motion.
  3. List Knowns & Unknowns: Identify all given values and what you need to find.
  4. Select Equations: Choose the kinematic equation(s) that link knowns and unknowns.

Example: Free Fall of a Ball (Thrown Downward)

Problem: A ball is thrown downward from a 98-m-high building with an initial velocity of 4.9 m/s downward.

  1. How much time until it hits the ground?
  2. What is its velocity when it hits the ground?

Strategy:

  • Coordinate System: Origin at the top of the building (\(y_0 = 0\)). Positive direction upward. Therefore, \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).
  • Knowns: \(y_0 = 0\), \(v_0 = -4.9 \, \text{m/s}\) (downward is negative), \(y = -98 \, \text{m}\) (ground is below origin).
  • Unknowns: (a) \(t\), (b) \(v_f\).

Figure 3.27: Ball thrown downward from a building.

The choice of coordinate system is critical. Here, choosing upward as positive makes the initial velocity and final position negative.

Example: Solution (Part a)

Solution (Part a): Time to reach ground (\(t\))

Use \(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\): \[ -98.0 \, \text{m} = 0 + (-4.9 \, \text{m/s})t - \frac{1}{2}(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2 \] \[ -98.0 = -4.9t - 4.9t^2 \] Rearrange into a quadratic equation (\(4.9t^2 + 4.9t - 98.0 = 0\)), then divide by 4.9: \[ t^2 + t - 20 = 0 \] Using the quadratic formula \(t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\): \[ t = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1^2 - 4(1)(-20)}}{2(1)} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 80}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{81}}{2} = \frac{-1 \pm 9}{2} \]

Two solutions: \(t = \frac{-1 + 9}{2} = 4.0 \, \text{s}\) and \(t = \frac{-1 - 9}{2} = -5.0 \, \text{s}\).

Since time cannot be negative from release, \(t = 4.0 \, \text{s}\).

The negative root corresponds to a hypothetical scenario where the ball started from the ground, was thrown upwards, passed the top of the building at 4.9 m/s downward, and continued its trajectory. In this problem’s context, only the positive time is relevant.

Example: Solution (Part b)

Solution (Part b): Velocity at ground (\(v\))

Use \(v = v_0 - gt\): \[ v = -4.9 \, \text{m/s} - (9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(4.0 \, \text{s}) \] \[ v = -4.9 \, \text{m/s} - 39.2 \, \text{m/s} \] \[ v = -44.1 \, \text{m/s} \]

Significance:

  • The negative velocity confirms the ball is moving downward.
  • The velocity is not zero immediately upon hitting the ground; it changes rapidly after impact.
  • This problem emphasizes consistent use of the coordinate system and signs.

The problem illustrates how important it is to be clear about the object’s state during free fall versus after it interacts with a surface. Free-fall equations apply only while gravity is the sole significant force.

Example: Vertical Motion of a Baseball (Thrown Upward)

Problem: A baseball is hit straight upward and caught 5.0 s later at the same location.

  1. Initial velocity (\(v_0\))?
  2. Maximum height (\(y_{\text{max}}\))?
  3. Time to reach maximum height (\(t_{\text{peak}}\))?
  4. Acceleration at the top of its path?
  5. Velocity when caught (\(v_f\))?

Strategy:

  • Coordinate System: Origin at home plate (\(y_0 = 0\)). Positive direction upward. Therefore, \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).
  • Knowns: \(y_0 = 0\), \(y_f = 0\) (caught at same spot), total \(t = 5.0\) s.
  • Key Insight: At maximum height, \(v = 0\).

Figure 3.28: Baseball hit straight up.

The symmetry of motion in free fall is a crucial concept here: time to go up equals time to come down, and speed upon return equals initial speed (but opposite direction).

Example: Solution (Parts a, b, c)

Solution:

(a) Initial velocity (\(v_0\)): Use \(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\): \[ 0 = 0 + v_0(5.0 \, \text{s}) - \frac{1}{2}(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(5.0 \, \text{s})^2 \] \[ 0 = 5.0v_0 - (4.9)(25) \] \[ 0 = 5.0v_0 - 122.5 \] \[ v_0 = \frac{122.5}{5.0} = 24.5 \, \text{m/s} \]

(b) Maximum height (\(y_{\text{max}}\)): At max height, \(v = 0\). Use \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\): \[ 0 = (24.5 \, \text{m/s})^2 - 2(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(y_{\text{max}} - 0) \] \[ 0 = 600.25 - 19.6y_{\text{max}} \] \[ y_{\text{max}} = \frac{600.25}{19.6} = 30.6 \, \text{m} \]

(c) Time to reach maximum height (\(t_{\text{peak}}\)): At max height, \(v = 0\). Use \(v = v_0 - gt\): \[ 0 = 24.5 \, \text{m/s} - (9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)t_{\text{peak}} \] \[ t_{\text{peak}} = \frac{24.5}{9.8} = 2.5 \, \text{s} \] Notice \(t_{\text{peak}}\) is half the total time (5.0 s), as expected due to symmetry.

Reiterate the concept of symmetry in vertical motion under gravity: time to rise equals time to fall. Also, the speed at any given height during ascent is equal to the speed at the same height during descent (but with opposite direction).

Example: Solution (Parts d & e)

Solution (continued):

(d) Acceleration at the top of its path:

  • The acceleration due to gravity is constant throughout the entire flight.
  • So, even at the peak where \(v = 0\), the acceleration is \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).

Warning

A common misconception is that acceleration is zero at the peak of the trajectory. This is incorrect; velocity is momentarily zero, but acceleration due to gravity is always acting downward.

(e) Velocity when caught (\(v_f\)):

Use \(v = v_0 - gt\) for the entire 5.0 s flight: \[ v_f = 24.5 \, \text{m/s} - (9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(5.0 \, \text{s}) \] \[ v_f = 24.5 \, \text{m/s} - 49.0 \, \text{m/s} \] \[ v_f = -24.5 \, \text{m/s} \]

Significance:

The final velocity is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction to the initial velocity, due to the symmetry of free fall when starting and ending at the same height.

Check Your Understanding 3.7

Problem: A chunk of ice breaks off a glacier and falls 30.0 m.

Assuming free fall, how long does it take to hit the water?

Which quantity increases faster, the speed of the ice chunk or its distance traveled?

Given:

  • \(y_0 = 30.0\) m (or \(y_0=0, y=-30.0\) m)
  • \(v_0 = 0\) m/s (falls from rest)
  • \(g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\)

Formulas to use:

  • For time: \(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) (if upward positive) or \(y = y_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) (if downward positive)
  • Speed increases linearly with time (\(v=gt\)).
  • Distance increases quadratically with time (\(y=\frac{1}{2}gt^2\)).

Guide students to choose a coordinate system (e.g., origin at initial height, positive down). \(y = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \Rightarrow 30.0 = \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \Rightarrow t = \sqrt{60/9.8} \approx 2.47\) s. Distance increases quadratically (\(t^2\)), while speed increases linearly (\(t\)), so distance increases faster.

Example: Rocket Booster

Problem: A rocket releases its booster at a height of 5.0 km with a velocity of 200.0 m/s (upward).

  1. What is the maximum height the booster attains (from release point)?

  2. What is the velocity of the booster at a height of 6.0 km (from ground)?

Strategy:

  • Coordinate System: Origin at the point of booster release (\(y_0 = 0\)). Positive direction upward. So, \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).
  • Initial Conditions (at release): \(y_0 = 0\), \(v_0 = 200.0\) m/s. (Note: The 5.0 km is the altitude of the origin above ground.)
  • Key Insight for (a): At maximum height, \(v = 0\).

Figure 3.29: Rocket releasing its booster.

The problem asks for maximum height attained by the booster (from the point of release), which clarifies the \(y_0=0\) choice for the booster’s motion. Part (b) will require careful interpretation of the 6.0 km height.

Example: Solution (Part a)

Solution (Part a): Maximum height attained by booster

Given: \(y_0 = 0\), \(v_0 = 200.0\) m/s, \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).

At max height, \(v = 0\).

Use \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\): \[ 0^2 = (200.0 \, \text{m/s})^2 - 2(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(y_{\text{max}} - 0) \] \[ 0 = 40000 - 19.6y_{\text{max}} \] \[ y_{\text{max}} = \frac{40000}{19.6} \approx 2040.8 \, \text{m} \]

This is the height above the release point.

Total maximum height from Earth’s surface: \(5.0 \, \text{km} + 2.04 \, \text{km} = 7.04 \, \text{km}\).

Ensure students distinguish between height relative to the origin and total height from the ground. The 2040.8 m is the additional height gained after release.

Example: Solution (Part b)

Solution (Part b): Velocity at height of 6.0 km

  • The 6.0 km height is from the ground.
  • Our origin is at 5.0 km from the ground.
  • So, the booster’s position \(y\) in our coordinate system is \(6.0 \, \text{km} - 5.0 \, \text{km} = 1.0 \, \text{km} = 1000 \, \text{m}\).

Given: \(y_0 = 0\), \(v_0 = 200.0\) m/s, \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\), \(y = 1000\) m.

Use \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\): \[ v^2 = (200.0 \, \text{m/s})^2 - 2(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)(1000 \, \text{m} - 0) \] \[ v^2 = 40000 - 19600 \] \[ v^2 = 20400 \] \[ v = \pm\sqrt{20400} \approx \pm 142.8 \, \text{m/s} \]

Significance:

  • The positive solution (\(+142.8\) m/s) is the velocity when the booster is still moving upward at 6.0 km altitude.
  • The negative solution (\(-142.8\) m/s) is the velocity when the booster is moving downward at 6.0 km altitude (after reaching its peak and falling).
  • Both are physically meaningful.

The appearance of both positive and negative roots is common in these types of problems, and both are physically significant, representing motion in different directions at the same position. This reinforces understanding of vector directions.

Key Takeaways

  • Free Fall Definition: Motion under gravity alone, neglecting air resistance.
  • Acceleration due to Gravity (\(g\)):
    • Constant: \(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\) (near Earth).
    • Direction: Always downward.
    • Independent of mass.
  • Coordinate System: Crucial for setting the sign of acceleration (\(a = \pm g\)).
    • If upward is positive, \(a = -g\).
    • If downward is positive, \(a = +g\).
  • Kinematic Equations: The standard constant acceleration equations apply, with \(y\) for displacement and \(\pm g\) for acceleration.
  • Symmetry in Vertical Motion:
    • Time to ascend equals time to descend (to the same height).
    • Speed at a given height during ascent equals speed at the same height during descent.
  • Acceleration at Peak: Velocity is zero, but acceleration is still \(g\) (downward).

Key Equations

Equation (Upward is Positive) Description
\(v = v_0 - gt\) Final velocity at time \(t\)
\(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) Final position at time \(t\)
\(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\) Final velocity squared, without time

Key Terms

Term Definition
Free Fall Motion of an object only under the influence of gravity, neglecting air resistance.
Acceleration Due to Gravity (\(g\)) The constant acceleration of an object in free fall near Earth’s surface, approximately \(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\).
Projectile Motion (Not explicitly covered, but relevant) The motion of an object thrown or projected into the air, subject only to gravity.
