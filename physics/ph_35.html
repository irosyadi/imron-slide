Free Fall: The Physics of Falling Objects

Welcome back! Today we’re taking our kinematic equations and applying them to a very specific, yet incredibly common and important, type of motion: free fall. This is the motion of objects solely under the influence of gravity, an essential concept for engineers in fields from aerospace to civil construction. We’ll discover how simple these motions become under certain assumptions.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Use the kinematic equations with vertical displacement (\(y\)) and gravitational acceleration (\(g\)) to analyze free-fall motion.
  • Describe how the position, velocity, and acceleration change during a free fall.
  • Solve for the position, velocity, and acceleration as functions of time for objects in free fall.

Our objectives today are focused on mastering free fall. This means we’ll adapt our familiar kinematic equations to vertical motion, understand the constant nature of gravitational acceleration, and then apply these tools to solve problems involving objects moving up and down under gravity.

What is Free Fall?

Free fall describes the motion of an object solely under the influence of gravity, neglecting air resistance and friction.

  • It applies to objects moving upward as well as downward.
  • Once an object leaves contact with whatever held or threw it, it is in free fall.

Note

Historical Insight: Galileo Galilei disproved the long-held belief that heavier objects fall faster. If air resistance is negligible, all objects fall with the same constant acceleration, regardless of their mass.

Figure 3.26: Hammer and feather fall at the same rate in a vacuum.

The concept of free fall is often misunderstood. It doesn’t just mean falling downwards; it refers to any motion where gravity is the only significant force. This includes an object thrown straight up at the peak of its trajectory, or even an object in orbit. The crucial part is neglecting air resistance, which in many engineering contexts is a reasonable first approximation or a baseline for more complex analyses. Galileo’s experiment, famously repeated on the moon, vividly demonstrates that mass does not affect the rate of fall in a vacuum.

Acceleration Due to Gravity (\(g\))

The constant acceleration experienced by objects in free fall is called acceleration due to gravity, denoted by \(g\).

  • It is constant at any given location on Earth.
  • Its average value on Earth’s surface is approximately: \[ g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2 \quad (\text{or } 32.2 \text{ ft/s}^2) \]
  • The direction of \(g\) is always downward (toward the center of Earth).

Important

Coordinate System Convention: - If upward is defined as positive (+y direction), then acceleration \(a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\). - If downward is defined as positive (+y direction), then acceleration \(a = +g = +9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\). Always define your positive direction consistently!

The value of ‘g’ is one of the most fundamental constants in mechanics. While it varies slightly with altitude and latitude, for most undergraduate engineering problems, 9.8 m/s² is a sufficient approximation. The choice of positive direction is arbitrary but critically important for the sign of ‘g’ in your equations. We will primarily use upward as positive, which means ‘g’ will typically be negative in our equations.

Kinematic Equations for Free Fall (Vertical Motion)

We adapt our constant acceleration equations for vertical motion, using \(y\) for displacement and \(a = -g\) (assuming upward is positive).

  1. Velocity-Time Relation: \[ \mathbf{v = v_0 - gt} \]

  2. Position-Time Relation: \[ \mathbf{y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2} \]

  3. Velocity-Position Relation (Time-Independent): \[ \mathbf{v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)} \]

Tip

General Problem-Solving Strategy for Free Fall:

  1. Choose a Coordinate System: Define origin (\(y_0=0\)) and positive direction (e.g., upward).
  2. Sketch the Problem: Visualize initial/final positions, velocities, and acceleration.
  3. List Knowns & Unknowns: Pay attention to initial conditions (e.g., “dropped from rest” means \(v_0=0\)).
  4. Select Equations: Choose the kinematic equation(s) that best fit your knowns and unknowns.

These are simply our familiar kinematic equations, but with x replaced by y and a replaced by -g (because we are setting upward as the positive direction). This standardization helps avoid confusion. The problem-solving strategy remains the same as for horizontal motion, with an added emphasis on establishing your vertical coordinate system clearly.

Example 3.14: Free Fall of a Ball

A ball is thrown downward from the top of a \(98 \text{ m}\) high building with an initial velocity of \(4.9 \text{ m/s}\) downward.

  1. How much time elapses before the ball reaches the ground?
  2. What is the velocity when it arrives at the ground?

Strategy:

  1. Coordinate System:
    • Origin at the top of the building (\(y_0 = 0\)).
    • Positive direction upward.
  2. Knowns:
    • \(y_0 = 0 \text{ m}\)
    • \(v_0 = -4.9 \text{ m/s}\) (downward is negative)
    • \(y = -98 \text{ m}\) (ground is 98m below origin)
    • \(g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2 \implies a = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\)
  3. Unknowns:
    • \(t\) (time to ground)
    • \(v\) (final velocity)

Figure 3.27: Positions and velocities at 1-s intervals.

This problem is a good test of coordinate system consistency. By choosing upward as positive, both the initial velocity and the final position (ground level) will be negative, and naturally, ‘g’ will be negative in the equations. The figure helps visualize the scenario.

Solution: Free Fall of a Ball (Part 1/2)

1. Time to reach the ground (\(t\))

Using \(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\):

\[ -98.0 = 0 + (-4.9)t - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \] \[ -98.0 = -4.9t - 4.9t^2 \] Rearrange into a quadratic equation: \[ 4.9t^2 + 4.9t - 98.0 = 0 \] Divide by 4.9: \[ t^2 + t - 20 = 0 \] Factor or use quadratic formula \((t = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a})\): \[ (t + 5)(t - 4) = 0 \] Solutions: \(t = -5 \text{ s}\) and \(t = 4 \text{ s}\).

Since time cannot be negative in this context, we choose: \[ t = \mathbf{4.0 \text{ s}} \]

We use the position-time equation because it contains ‘y’, ‘y0’, ‘v0’, ‘g’, and ‘t’. Solving the quadratic equation gives two mathematical solutions. We must apply physical reasoning to choose the correct one: time cannot be negative from the moment of release. The negative root simply represents a time before the ball was released, if its trajectory had been extended backward.

Solution: Free Fall of a Ball (Part 2/2)

2. Velocity when it arrives at the ground (\(v\))

Using \(v = v_0 - gt\) with \(t = 4.0 \text{ s}\):

\[ v = -4.9 \text{ m/s} - (9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(4.0 \text{ s}) \] \[ v = -4.9 \text{ m/s} - 39.2 \text{ m/s} \] \[ v = \mathbf{-44.1 \text{ m/s}} \]

Significance:

  • The negative sign for velocity confirms the ball is moving downward when it hits the ground.
  • It’s important to remember that motion stops being “free fall” as soon as the ball makes contact with the ground.
  • Consistency in the chosen coordinate system (upward positive) is crucial for correct sign conventions.

The final velocity is negative, which is consistent with our coordinate system (upward positive, downward negative) and indicates the ball is indeed traveling downward. This also gives us a sense of the speed the ball is moving at impact, which is important in engineering for structural design, impact testing, and safety considerations.

Example 3.15: Vertical Motion of a Baseball

A batter hits a baseball straight upward. It is caught \(5.0 \text{ s}\) later at the same location from which it was hit (\(y_0 = y = 0\)).

  1. What is the initial velocity of the ball?
  2. What is the maximum height the ball reaches?
  3. How long does it take to reach the maximum height?
  4. What is the acceleration at the top of its path?
  5. What is the velocity of the ball when it is caught?

Strategy:

  1. Coordinate System:
    • Origin at the hit/catch location (\(y_0 = 0\)).
    • Positive direction upward.
  2. Knowns:
    • \(y_0 = 0 \text{ m}\)
    • \(y = 0 \text{ m}\) (when caught)
    • \(t_{\text{total}} = 5.0 \text{ s}\)
    • \(a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\)
  3. Key Insight: At maximum height, \(v = 0\).

Figure 3.28: A baseball hit straight up is caught 5.0 s later.

This example addresses common misconceptions about free fall, particularly concerning the velocity and acceleration at the peak of the trajectory. Since the ball is caught at the same height it was hit, we know the total flight time. The key is to remember that at its maximum height, its instantaneous vertical velocity is zero.

Solution: Vertical Motion of a Baseball (Part 1/3)

1. Initial Velocity of the Ball (\(v_0\))

Using \(y = y_0 + v_0t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) with \(y_0=0\), \(y=0\), and \(t=5.0 \text{ s}\):

\[ 0 = 0 + v_0(5.0) - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)(5.0)^2 \]

\[ 0 = 5.0v_0 - 4.9(25.0) \]

\[ 0 = 5.0v_0 - 122.5 \]

\[ 5.0v_0 = 122.5 \]

\[ v_0 = \frac{122.5}{5.0} = \mathbf{24.5 \text{ m/s}} \]

2. Maximum Height the Ball Reaches (\(y_{\text{max}}\))

At maximum height, \(v = 0\). Using \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\) with \(y_0=0\):

\[ 0^2 = (24.5)^2 - 2(9.8)(y_{\text{max}} - 0) \]

\[ 0 = 600.25 - 19.6y_{\text{max}} \]

\[ 19.6y_{\text{max}} = 600.25 \]

\[ y_{\text{max}} = \frac{600.25}{19.6} = \mathbf{30.6 \text{ m}} \]

First, we find the initial velocity using the total flight time and the fact that the ball returns to its starting height. Then, knowing the initial velocity, we can find the maximum height by setting the final velocity to zero in the time-independent equation.

Solution: Vertical Motion of a Baseball (Part 2/3)

3. Time to Reach Maximum Height (\(t_{\text{max}}\))

At maximum height, \(v = 0\). Using \(v = v_0 - gt\):

\[ 0 = 24.5 - 9.8t_{\text{max}} \]

\[ 9.8t_{\text{max}} = 24.5 \]

\[ t_{\text{max}} = \frac{24.5}{9.8} = \mathbf{2.5 \text{ s}} \]

(Note: This is exactly half the total flight time, as expected for symmetric motion.)

4. Acceleration at the Top of Its Path (\(a_{\text{top}}\))

The acceleration due to gravity is constant throughout the entire flight.

So, at the top of its path, \(a = \mathbf{-9.8 \text{ m/s}^2}\).

(Even though \(v=0\), velocity is changing from positive to negative, so acceleration is non-zero).

The time to reach maximum height can be found from the first kinematic equation. It’s often useful to note that for symmetric free fall motion, the time to reach the peak is half the total flight time. The answer to question 4 is crucial: acceleration due to gravity never changes during free fall, even when the instantaneous velocity is zero at the peak. The object is still under the influence of gravity and its velocity is continuously changing.

Solution: Vertical Motion of a Baseball (Part 3/3)

5. Velocity of the Ball When It Is Caught (\(v_{\text{catch}}\))

Using \(v = v_0 - gt\) with \(t = 5.0 \text{ s}\):

\[ v_{\text{catch}} = 24.5 \text{ m/s} - (9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(5.0 \text{ s}) \]

\[ v_{\text{catch}} = 24.5 \text{ m/s} - 49.0 \text{ m/s} \]

\[ v_{\text{catch}} = \mathbf{-24.5 \text{ m/s}} \]

Significance:

  • The final velocity is the negative of the initial velocity, confirming the ball returns with the same speed but in the opposite direction.
  • This problem demonstrates that free fall equations apply to both upward and downward parts of the trajectory.

The final velocity is the negative of the initial velocity, which is a hallmark of symmetric free-fall trajectories. This example reinforces the understanding that “free fall” applies to the entire motion once launched, not just the downward part.

Check Your Understanding: Falling Ice Chunk

A chunk of ice breaks off a glacier and falls \(30.0 \text{ m}\) before it hits the water. Assuming it falls freely (no air resistance).

  1. How long does it take to hit the water?
  2. Which quantity increases faster, the speed of the ice chunk or its distance traveled?

Tip

Hint for Part 2: Think about the functional form of speed (\(v\) vs \(t\)) and distance (\(y\) vs \(t\)).

This is a straightforward free-fall problem. For part 2, remember the equations that describe speed and distance as functions of time. One of them grows linearly with time, and the other grows quadratically.

Solution: Falling Ice Chunk

1. Time to Hit the Water (\(t\))

Coordinate System: Let \(y_0 = 0\) at the glacier edge, and positive direction downward.

Then \(a = +g = +9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\).

Knowns:

\(y_0 = 0 \text{ m}\)

\(v_0 = 0 \text{ m/s}\) (starts from rest)

\(y = 30.0 \text{ m}\) (displacement downward)

\(a = +9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\)

Equation: \(y = y_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)

\[ 30.0 = 0 + 0 \cdot t + \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \]

\[ 30.0 = 4.9t^2 \]

\[ t^2 = \frac{30.0}{4.9} \approx 6.122 \]

\[ t = \sqrt{6.122} \approx \mathbf{2.47 \text{ s}} \]

2. Comparing Speed and Distance Traveled

  • Speed: \(v = v_0 + at \implies v = gt\) (since \(v_0=0\)). Speed increases linearly with time (\(v \propto t\)).
  • Distance Traveled: \(y = y_0 + v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \implies y = \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) (since \(y_0=0, v_0=0\)). Distance increases quadratically with time (\(y \propto t^2\)).

Therefore, the distance traveled increases faster than the speed of the ice chunk.

Using a downward positive coordinate system simplifies the sign of ‘g’. The time to fall 30 meters is just under 2.5 seconds. For part 2, it’s a conceptual question about the functional forms. Distance grows quadratically with time (t-squared), while speed grows linearly with time. So, as time progresses, the distance covered increases much more rapidly. This is a common characteristic of constantly accelerated motion.

Key Takeaways

  • Free fall is motion under gravity alone, neglecting air resistance.
  • Acceleration due to gravity (\(g\)) is constant: \(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\) (downward).
  • Choose a consistent coordinate system for vertical motion (\(y\)). If upward is positive, then \(a = -g\).
  • The kinematic equations for constant acceleration are directly applicable, with \(a\) replaced by \(g\) (with appropriate sign).
  • At the peak of a vertical trajectory, velocity is momentarily zero, but acceleration is still \(g\) downwards.
  • Symmetric free fall (launched and caught at same height):
    • Time to rise = Time to fall
    • Final speed = Initial speed (opposite direction)

These are the most important points to remember about free fall. Always check your coordinate system and the sign of ‘g’. Understand that acceleration is constant throughout the entire free fall motion, even at the very top. These principles are fundamental for understanding and designing anything that moves through the air or falls under gravity, from bridge structures to rocket trajectories.
