Instantaneous Velocity & Speed:

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Explain the difference between average velocity and instantaneous velocity.
  • Describe the difference between velocity and speed.
  • Calculate instantaneous velocity given a position function.
  • Calculate speed given instantaneous velocity.
  • Apply these concepts to analyze real-world engineering scenarios.

Today, we delve into the core concepts of instantaneous velocity and speed, crucial for analyzing dynamic systems in engineering. We’ll build on our understanding of average velocity and see how calculus allows us to precisely describe motion at any given moment.

Revisiting Average Velocity

Recall that average velocity describes motion over an interval.

\[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x(t_2) - x(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} \]

  • Definition: Displacement divided by the time interval.
  • Vector Quantity: Has both magnitude and direction.
  • Overall Motion: Provides a general sense of movement over a period.

Note

Think of a road trip: your average velocity might be 60 km/h, but you drove faster and slower at various points.

Important

Average velocity tells us where an object ends up relative to its start, and how long it took. It doesn’t describe the journey’s details.

Average velocity is a good starting point, but in many engineering applications, we need a much more granular understanding of motion. For example, if we’re designing a system to avoid collisions, knowing the average velocity isn’t enough; we need to know the velocity at the precise moment of potential impact.

Introduction to Instantaneous Velocity

Instantaneous velocity tells us how fast an object is moving at any single point in time.

It’s the limit of the average velocity as the time interval approaches zero.

\[ v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t} \]

This is the fundamental definition of a derivative.

\[ v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} \]

Tip

In physics, “velocity” typically refers to instantaneous velocity unless specified as “average velocity.”

This is where calculus becomes indispensable in physics and engineering. By taking the derivative of the position function with respect to time, we can pinpoint the exact velocity at any given instant. This transition from average to instantaneous is a powerful conceptual leap.

Instantaneous Velocity: Graphical Interpretation

On a position-versus-time graph, instantaneous velocity is the slope of the tangent line at a specific point.

Graph shows position plotted versus time

Note

At \(t_0\), the slope (and thus instantaneous velocity) is zero, indicating a momentary stop or reversal of direction.

This visual representation is crucial for understanding the concept. Imagine zooming in infinitely on a point on the x-t graph. The slope of that tiny segment is the instantaneous velocity. If the slope is positive, the object is moving in the positive direction. If negative, it’s moving in the negative direction. If zero, it’s momentarily at rest.

Velocity vs. Speed: A Crucial Distinction

In physics, velocity and speed are not interchangeable.

Velocity (\(v\))

  • Vector Quantity: Magnitude and Direction.
  • Describes rate of change of position.
  • Can be positive, negative, or zero.
  • Example: +5 m/s (moving east) or -5 m/s (moving west).

Speed (\(|v|\))

  • Scalar Quantity: Magnitude only.
  • Describes rate of change of distance traveled.
  • Always non-negative.
  • Calculated as the magnitude of instantaneous velocity.
  • Example: 5 m/s (regardless of direction).

Warning

Understanding this difference is critical for accurate problem-solving and system design in engineering. For instance, a robot might have zero average velocity if it returns to its starting point, but its average speed would reflect the total distance traveled.

This distinction is fundamental. While intuitively we might use them similarly, in physics and engineering, the direction component of velocity is often as important as its magnitude. Think about designing a vehicle: its speed limit is one thing, but its velocity vector determines its trajectory and collision potential.

Calculating Instantaneous Velocity & Speed

When position \(x(t)\) is a function of time, we use differentiation.

For terms of the form \(At^n\):

\[ \frac{d(At^n)}{dt} = An t^{n-1} \]

Steps to find \(v(t)\):

  1. Identify the position function, \(x(t)\).
  2. Differentiate \(x(t)\) with respect to \(t\) using the power rule.
  3. Substitute the specific time \(t\) into \(v(t)\) for instantaneous velocity.

Steps to find Speed:

  1. First, find the instantaneous velocity \(v(t)\).
  2. Calculate the magnitude of \(v(t)\): Speed \(= |v(t)|\).
    • If \(v(t)\) is 1D, this is simply the absolute value.

The power rule is our primary tool here. Remember that differentiation is a linear operation, meaning you can differentiate each term of a sum independently. Once you have the velocity, finding the speed is a straightforward step – just take the absolute value.

  • 1.0 s to 2.0 s: \(v = \frac{0.0 \text{ km} - 0.5 \text{ km}}{2.0 \text{ min} - 1.0 \text{ min}} = -0.5 \text{ km/min}\)

Resulting Velocity Graph:

Notice how the positive slope translates to a positive constant velocity, a zero slope to zero velocity, and a negative slope to a negative constant velocity. This direct relationship is fundamental.

Practical Application: Particle Motion

Consider a particle with position \(x(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})t + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^3\).

Part 1: Instantaneous Velocity

  1. Find the instantaneous velocity \(v(t)\) at \(t = 2.0\) s.

    Strategy: Differentiate \(x(t)\) to find \(v(t)\), then substitute \(t = 2.0\) s.

Part 2: Average Velocity

  1. Calculate the average velocity between \(t = 1.0\) s and \(t = 3.0\) s.

    Strategy: Find \(x(1.0 \text{ s})\) and \(x(3.0 \text{ s})\), then use the average velocity formula.

This problem highlights the difference between instantaneous and average velocity using a continuous function. Engineers frequently deal with such polynomial representations of motion, especially in trajectory analysis or control systems.

Practical Application: Particle Motion (Solution)

Part 1: Instantaneous Velocity at \(t = 2.0\) s

  1. Differentiate \(x(t)\): \(v(t) = \frac{d}{dt}[(3.0 \text{ m/s})t + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^3]\)

    \(v(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) + (1.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^2\)

  2. Substitute \(t = 2.0\) s: \(v(2.0 \text{ s}) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) + (1.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(2.0 \text{ s})^2\)

    \(v(2.0 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} + (1.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(4.0 \text{ s}^2)\)

    \(v(2.0 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} + 6.0 \text{ m/s} = 9.0 \text{ m/s}\)

Part 2: Average Velocity (1.0 s to 3.0 s)

  1. Calculate \(x(t)\) at \(t=1.0\) s and \(t=3.0\) s:

    \(x(1.0 \text{ s}) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})(1.0 \text{ s}) + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(1.0 \text{ s})^3 = 3.0 \text{ m} + 0.5 \text{ m} = 3.5 \text{ m}\)

    \(x(3.0 \text{ s}) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})(3.0 \text{ s}) + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(3.0 \text{ s})^3 = 9.0 \text{ m} + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(27.0 \text{ s}^3) = 9.0 \text{ m} + 13.5 \text{ m} = 22.5 \text{ m}\)

  2. Calculate average velocity:

    \(\bar{v} = \frac{x(3.0 \text{ s}) - x(1.0 \text{ s})}{3.0 \text{ s} - 1.0 \text{ s}} = \frac{22.5 \text{ m} - 3.5 \text{ m}}{2.0 \text{ s}}\)

    \(\bar{v} = \frac{19.0 \text{ m}}{2.0 \text{ s}} = 9.5 \text{ m/s}\)

Notice that the instantaneous velocity at 2.0 seconds (9.0 m/s) is close to, but not exactly equal to, the average velocity over the interval from 1.0 s to 3.0 s (9.5 m/s). This highlights that average velocity smooths out the details of motion.

Engineering Challenge: Drone Trajectory Analysis

A drone’s horizontal position (in meters) is described by \(x(t) = 0.5t^3 - 4.5t^2 + 12t\), where \(t\) is in seconds.

Assume motion along a straight line.

Problem Questions:

  1. What is the drone’s instantaneous velocity function, \(v(t)\)?
  2. At what time(s) does the drone momentarily stop (i.e., its velocity is zero)?
  3. What is the drone’s speed at \(t=1\) s and \(t=4\) s?
  4. Describe the drone’s motion (direction and speed) at \(t=0.5\) s and \(t=3.5\) s.

Note

Hint for (2): Set \(v(t)=0\) and solve for \(t\). You’ll likely need to factor a quadratic equation.

Important

This problem mimics analyzing sensor data or predicting movement in drone control systems. Understanding these turning points and speeds is critical for navigation and collision avoidance.

This problem is designed to apply all the concepts we’ve discussed: differentiation for velocity, finding roots for stopping points, and calculating speed. It’s a typical scenario in robotics or autonomous vehicle engineering. Take a few minutes to work through it.

Engineering Challenge: Drone Trajectory Analysis (Solution)

1. Instantaneous Velocity Function \(v(t)\):

\(x(t) = 0.5t^3 - 4.5t^2 + 12t\)

\(v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(0.5t^3 - 4.5t^2 + 12t)\)

\(v(t) = 1.5t^2 - 9t + 12\)

2. Time(s) when the drone momentarily stops (\(v(t)=0\)):

\(1.5t^2 - 9t + 12 = 0\)

Divide by 1.5:

\(t^2 - 6t + 8 = 0\)

Factor:

\((t-2)(t-4) = 0\)

So, \(t = 2\) s and \(t = 4\) s.

3. Speed at \(t=1\) s and \(t=4\) s:

  • At \(t=1\) s:

    \(v(1) = 1.5(1)^2 - 9(1) + 12 = 1.5 - 9 + 12 = 4.5\) m/s

    Speed \(= |v(1)| = |4.5|\) m/s \(= 4.5\) m/s

  • At \(t=4\) s:

    \(v(4) = 1.5(4)^2 - 9(4) + 12 = 1.5(16) - 36 + 12 = 24 - 36 + 12 = 0\) m/s

    Speed \(= |v(4)| = |0|\) m/s \(= 0\) m/s

    (Matches our finding from question 2!)

For question 2, solving the quadratic equation reveals two instances where the drone comes to a complete stop. These points are critical in trajectory planning for any moving system. For question 3, we see the speed calculation is straightforward once velocity is known.

Engineering Challenge: Drone Trajectory Analysis (Solution Cont.)

4. Describe drone’s motion at \(t=0.5\) s and \(t=3.5\) s:

Recall \(v(t) = 1.5t^2 - 9t + 12\).

  • At \(t=0.5\) s:

    \(v(0.5) = 1.5(0.5)^2 - 9(0.5) + 12\)

    \(v(0.5) = 1.5(0.25) - 4.5 + 12\)

    \(v(0.5) = 0.375 - 4.5 + 12 = 7.875\) m/s

    Motion: Drone is moving in the positive direction with a speed of 7.875 m/s.

  • At \(t=3.5\) s:

    \(v(3.5) = 1.5(3.5)^2 - 9(3.5) + 12\)

    \(v(3.5) = 1.5(12.25) - 31.5 + 12\)

    \(v(3.5) = 18.375 - 31.5 + 12 = -1.125\) m/s

    Motion: Drone is moving in the negative direction with a speed of 1.125 m/s.

Note

This analysis shows the drone starts moving in the positive direction, stops at \(t=2\)s, reverses direction to move negatively, stops again at \(t=4\)s, and then moves positively again. This type of detailed motion analysis is invaluable in engineering.

The final part of the problem demonstrates how the sign of velocity indicates direction. The drone changes direction at the points where its velocity is zero, as expected. This comprehensive understanding of motion is vital for designing robust and predictable engineering systems.

Key Takeaways

Instantaneous Velocity vs. Average Velocity

Instantaneous velocity, \(v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt}\), describes motion at a specific instant, as the slope of the tangent to the position-time graph. Average velocity describes motion over an interval.

Velocity vs. Speed

Velocity is a vector (magnitude and direction), calculated by differentiating position. Speed is a scalar (magnitude only), calculated as the absolute value of velocity, \(|v(t)|\).

Calculus is Key

Understanding derivatives is fundamental to analyzing dynamic systems and predicting motion in engineering. The power rule, \(\frac{d(At^n)}{dt} = Ant^{n-1}\), is a crucial tool.

These three points summarize the core concepts from today’s lecture. Remember that the precision offered by instantaneous velocity and speed, enabled by calculus, is a cornerstone of advanced physics and engineering analysis.
