3.2 Instantaneous Velocity and Speed

Precision in Describing Motion

Welcome to the second section of our kinematics module. Today, we’re taking a deeper dive into how we describe motion, moving beyond just average values to understand motion at any specific instant. This precision is essential in engineering.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Explain the difference between average velocity and instantaneous velocity.
  • Describe the difference between velocity and speed.
  • Calculate the instantaneous velocity given the mathematical equation for the position.
  • Calculate the speed given the instantaneous velocity.

These objectives will guide our exploration into more precise descriptions of motion. We’ll introduce calculus as a powerful tool to achieve this.

Beyond Average: The Need for Instantaneous Velocity

We’ve learned how to calculate average velocity between two points.

  • However, objects move continuously through space and time.
  • To fully understand motion, we need to know how fast an object is moving at any single point along its path.
  • This requires a more precise concept: instantaneous velocity.
  • To achieve this, we’ll leverage fundamental principles of calculus.

Average velocity gives us an overall picture, but it smooths out all the details. For instance, if you drive 100 miles in 2 hours, your average velocity is 50 mph. But you weren’t driving at exactly 50 mph the entire time; sometimes faster, sometimes slower, perhaps even stopped. Instantaneous velocity captures those precise moments.

Defining Instantaneous Velocity

The instantaneous velocity is the velocity of an object at a specific instant in time.

  • It is the average velocity between two points as the time interval (\(\Delta t\)) approaches zero.
  • Mathematically, if position x is a continuous function of time t, denoted by x(t): \[ \bar{v} = \frac{x(t_2) - x(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} \]
  • To find instantaneous velocity, we take the limit as \(\Delta t \to 0\): \[ v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} \]

Important

Instantaneous velocity is the derivative of the position function with respect to time.

This is the fundamental definition. It links the concepts of change in physics with the mathematical power of derivatives. When you see dx/dt, think “the rate of change of position with respect to time.”

Instantaneous Velocity: Graphical Interpretation

  • Like average velocity, instantaneous velocity v(t) is a vector quantity (has magnitude and direction).
  • On a position-versus-time (x(t) vs t) graph, the instantaneous velocity at any point t is the slope of the tangent line at that point.

Figure 3.6: Instantaneous velocity is the slope of the tangent line on an x-t graph.

Look at Figure 3.6. As the time interval Δt gets smaller, the secant line connecting two points on the curve approaches the tangent line at a single point. The slope of this tangent line at time t0 represents the instantaneous velocity at t0. Note that at t0, the slope is zero, meaning the object is momentarily at rest. This point usually corresponds to a maximum or minimum position.

Problem: Finding Velocity from a Position-Versus-Time Graph

Given the position-versus-time graph in Figure 3.7, find the corresponding velocity-versus-time graph.

Figure 3.7: Position versus time graph.

Tip

Strategy: The velocity during each time interval is the slope of the straight line segment on the position-versus-time graph.

This graph represents piecewise linear motion. For each segment, the slope is constant, meaning the velocity is constant. We simply need to calculate the slope for each interval.

Solution: Finding Velocity from a Position-Versus-Time Graph

Calculate the slope (velocity) for each interval:

  1. Time interval 0 s to 0.5 s: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.5 \text{ m} - 0.0 \text{ m}}{0.5 \text{ s} - 0.0 \text{ s}} = 1.0 \text{ m/s} \]
  2. Time interval 0.5 s to 1.0 s: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.5 \text{ m} - 0.5 \text{ m}}{1.0 \text{ s} - 0.5 \text{ s}} = 0.0 \text{ m/s} \]
  3. Time interval 1.0 s to 2.0 s: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.0 \text{ m} - 0.5 \text{ m}}{2.0 \text{ s} - 1.0 \text{ s}} = -0.5 \text{ m/s} \]

The corresponding velocity-versus-time graph is shown in Figure 3.8.

Figure 3.8: Velocity versus time graph.

The significance here is clear: a positive slope on the x-t graph means positive velocity, a zero slope means zero velocity (stopped), and a negative slope means negative velocity (moving in the opposite direction). This graphical analysis is a powerful tool for quickly understanding motion.

Speed: A Distinct Concept from Velocity

In physics, speed and velocity are not interchangeable terms.

  • Speed: Tells us how fast an object is moving.
    • It is a scalar quantity (magnitude only, no direction).
    • Always positive or zero.
  • Average Speed: The total distance traveled divided by the elapsed time. \[ \bar{s} = \frac{\text{Total distance}}{\text{Elapsed time}} \]

Note

Average speed is not necessarily the magnitude of the average velocity. Example: A round trip where you start and end at the same location has zero average velocity, but non-zero average speed.

This distinction is critical for engineers. When you’re calculating fuel consumption, you care about the total distance traveled (and thus average speed). When you’re designing a navigation system, you care about average velocity because direction matters.

Instantaneous Speed

While average speed differs from the magnitude of average velocity, instantaneous speed is directly related to instantaneous velocity.

  • Instantaneous Speed: The magnitude of the instantaneous velocity. \[ \text{Instantaneous speed} = |v(t)| \]

Example: - Particle A: +7.0 m/s (velocity) - Particle B: -7.0 m/s (velocity) - Both have an instantaneous speed of 7.0 m/s.

Instantaneous speed simply quantifies “how fast” without indicating “where to.” It’s the numerical value of the instantaneous velocity, always positive. This table gives you a sense of the vast range of speeds encountered in the physical world.

Calculating Instantaneous Velocity with Calculus

When the position function x(t) is given mathematically, we use differentiation to find v(t).

  • For polynomial position functions, x(t) often consists of terms in the form of At^n.
  • We use the power rule of differentiation: \[ \frac{d(At^n)}{dt} = An \cdot t^{n-1} \]
  • If x(t) has multiple terms added or subtracted, differentiate each term separately and sum the results.

The power rule is a cornerstone of calculus in physics. Make sure you’re comfortable with it. A is a constant coefficient, n is an integer power. Remember that the derivative of a constant term is zero, and the derivative of At (where n=1) is simply A.

Problem: Instantaneous vs. Average Velocity (Example 3.3)

The position of a particle is given by: \[ x(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})t + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^3 \]

  1. Find the instantaneous velocity at \(t = 2.0 \text{ s}\).
  2. Calculate the average velocity between \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\) and \(t = 3.0 \text{ s}\).

This problem directly tests your ability to apply the calculus rules for instantaneous velocity and the algebraic definition for average velocity. Pay attention to units!

Solution: Instantaneous vs. Average Velocity (Part 1)

1. Instantaneous velocity at \(t = 2.0 \text{ s}\):

  • First, find the general instantaneous velocity function v(t) by differentiating x(t): \[ x(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})t + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^3 \] \[ v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} \left[ (3.0 \text{ m/s})t \right] + \frac{d}{dt} \left[ (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^3 \right] \] Applying the power rule: \[ v(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) \cdot 1 \cdot t^{1-1} + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3) \cdot 3 \cdot t^{3-1} \] \[ v(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) + (1.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^2 \]

  • Now, substitute \(t = 2.0 \text{ s}\): \[ v(2.0 \text{ s}) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) + (1.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(2.0 \text{ s})^2 \] \[ v(2.0 \text{ s}) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) + (1.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(4.0 \text{ s}^2) \] \[ v(2.0 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} + 6.0 \text{ m/s} = 9.0 \text{ m/s} \]

Answer: The instantaneous velocity at \(t = 2.0 \text{ s}\) is \(9.0 \text{ m/s}\).

Make sure you understand each step of the differentiation. The units are important for verification; notice how s^2 in the denominator of the coefficient 1.5 m/s^3 cancels with s^2 from t^2 to yield m/s.

Solution: Instantaneous vs. Average Velocity (Part 2)

2. Average velocity between \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\) and \(t = 3.0 \text{ s}\):

  • First, find the position at \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\) and \(t = 3.0 \text{ s}\) using \(x(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})t + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)t^3\): \[ x(1.0 \text{ s}) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})(1.0 \text{ s}) + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(1.0 \text{ s})^3 = 3.0 \text{ m} + 0.5 \text{ m} = 3.5 \text{ m} \] \[ x(3.0 \text{ s}) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})(3.0 \text{ s}) + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(3.0 \text{ s})^3 = 9.0 \text{ m} + (0.5 \text{ m/s}^3)(27.0 \text{ s}^3) = 9.0 \text{ m} + 13.5 \text{ m} = 22.5 \text{ m} \]

  • Now, calculate the average velocity: \[ \bar{v} = \frac{x(3.0 \text{ s}) - x(1.0 \text{ s})}{3.0 \text{ s} - 1.0 \text{ s}} = \frac{22.5 \text{ m} - 3.5 \text{ m}}{2.0 \text{ s}} = \frac{19.0 \text{ m}}{2.0 \text{ s}} = 9.5 \text{ m/s} \]

Answer: The average velocity between \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\) and \(t = 3.0 \text{ s}\) is \(9.5 \text{ m/s}\).

Notice that the instantaneous velocity at 2.0 s (9.0 m/s) is close to the average velocity over the interval from 1.0 s to 3.0 s (9.5 m/s). As the time interval for average velocity shrinks and centers around 2.0 s, the average velocity would converge to the instantaneous velocity at 2.0 s.

Problem: Instantaneous Velocity Versus Speed (Example 3.4)

Consider the motion of a particle with position: \[ x(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s})t - (3.0 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 \]

  1. What is the instantaneous velocity at \(t = 0.25 \text{ s}\), \(t = 0.50 \text{ s}\), and \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\)?
  2. What is the speed of the particle at these times?

This problem highlights how velocity can change direction, and how speed is always positive. We’ll use differentiation to find velocity, then take its magnitude for speed.

Solution: Instantaneous Velocity Versus Speed

1. Instantaneous velocity:

  • First, find the general instantaneous velocity function v(t): \[ v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} \left[ (3.0 \text{ m/s})t - (3.0 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 \right] \] \[ v(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) - (3.0 \text{ m/s}^2) \cdot 2 \cdot t^{2-1} \] \[ v(t) = (3.0 \text{ m/s}) - (6.0 \text{ m/s}^2)t \]

  • Now, substitute the given times:

    • At \(t = 0.25 \text{ s}\): \(v(0.25 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} - (6.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(0.25 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} - 1.5 \text{ m/s} = 1.5 \text{ m/s}\)
    • At \(t = 0.50 \text{ s}\): \(v(0.50 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} - (6.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(0.50 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} - 3.0 \text{ m/s} = 0 \text{ m/s}\)
    • At \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\): \(v(1.0 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} - (6.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(1.0 \text{ s}) = 3.0 \text{ m/s} - 6.0 \text{ m/s} = -3.0 \text{ m/s}\)

2. Speed of the particle:

  • Speed is the magnitude of velocity, \(|v(t)|\):
    • At \(t = 0.25 \text{ s}\): Speed \(= |1.5 \text{ m/s}| = 1.5 \text{ m/s}\)
    • At \(t = 0.50 \text{ s}\): Speed \(= |0 \text{ m/s}| = 0 \text{ m/s}\)
    • At \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\): Speed \(= |-3.0 \text{ m/s}| = 3.0 \text{ m/s}\)

Observe the velocity at 0.50 s. It’s zero. This means the particle momentarily stops and reverses direction. At 1.0 s, the velocity is negative, indicating movement in the negative direction, but the speed is positive, just showing ‘how fast’.

Check Your Understanding 3.2

The position of an object as a function of time is given by: \[ x(t) = (-3 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 \]

  1. What is the velocity of the object as a function of time?

  2. Is the velocity ever positive?

  3. What are the velocity and speed at \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\)?

Pause here and try to solve this on your own. Remember the power rule for differentiation, and the definition of speed.

Check Your Understanding 3.2 - Solution

Given position function: \(x(t) = (-3 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2\)

(a) What is the velocity of the object as a function of time?

  • Differentiate \(x(t)\) with respect to \(t\):

    \[ v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} \left[ (-3 \text{ m/s}^2)t^2 \right] \]

    \[ v(t) = (-3 \text{ m/s}^2) \cdot 2 \cdot t^{2-1} \]

    \[ v(t) = (-6 \text{ m/s}^2)t \]

Answer (a): The velocity of the object as a function of time is \(v(t) = (-6 \text{ m/s}^2)t\).

(b) Is the velocity ever positive?

  • Since \(t\) (time) is always \(\ge 0\), and the coefficient \((-6 \text{ m/s}^2)\) is negative, the product \((-6 \text{ m/s}^2)t\) will always be \(\le 0\).

Answer (b): No, the velocity is never positive. It is either zero (at \(t=0\)) or negative.

(c) What are the velocity and speed at \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\)?

  • At \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\):

    \(v(1.0 \text{ s}) = (-6 \text{ m/s}^2)(1.0 \text{ s}) = -6.0 \text{ m/s}\)

  • Speed is the magnitude of velocity:

    Speed \(= |v(1.0 \text{ s})| = |-6.0 \text{ m/s}| = 6.0 \text{ m/s}\)

Answer (c): At \(t = 1.0 \text{ s}\), the velocity is \(-6.0 \text{ m/s}\) and the speed is \(6.0 \text{ m/s}\).

This problem reinforces the definitions. The object starts at rest at the origin and then continuously moves in the negative direction, with its speed increasing linearly.

Key Takeaways

  • Instantaneous velocity is the velocity at a specific moment, found by differentiating the position function with respect to time (\(v(t) = dx/dt\)).
  • It is the slope of the tangent line on an x-t graph.
  • Velocity is a vector quantity (magnitude and direction).
  • Speed is the magnitude of velocity; it is a scalar quantity (magnitude only) and is always positive or zero.
  • Average speed is total distance traveled divided by elapsed time and is not necessarily equal to the magnitude of average velocity.
  • Calculus (differentiation) is essential for analyzing motion at an instantaneous level.

Remember these core distinctions. The ability to use calculus to describe instantaneous motion is a powerful tool you’ll use throughout your engineering studies.
