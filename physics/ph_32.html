3.2 Instantaneous Velocity and Speed

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Explain the difference between average velocity and instantaneous velocity.
  • Describe the difference between velocity and speed.
  • Calculate the instantaneous velocity given the mathematical equation for the position.
  • Calculate the speed given the instantaneous velocity.

In the previous section, we learned about average velocity, which gives us an overall picture of motion over a time interval. Now, we’re going to dive into instantaneous velocity, which tells us how fast an object is moving at a single point in time. This requires an introduction to calculus, specifically derivatives. We’ll also clarify the important distinction between velocity and speed.

Instantaneous Velocity

Instantaneous velocity (often just called velocity) is how fast an object is moving at any single point along its path.

  • It is the limit of the average velocity as the elapsed time approaches zero.
  • Mathematically, it’s the derivative of position with respect to time.

\[ v(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{x(t + \Delta t) - x(t)}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} \]

  • Like average velocity, it’s a vector quantity (magnitude and direction).
  • The instantaneous velocity at time \(t_0\) is the slope of the tangent line to the position-time graph at \(t_0\).

Imagine a speedometer in a car. That’s instantaneous speed. Instantaneous velocity adds the direction. If your position function is \(x(t)\), then taking its derivative gives you the instantaneous velocity function \(v(t)\). This is a fundamental concept in calculus-based physics. The idea of the limit means we’re looking at smaller and smaller time intervals until it’s practically an instant.

Instantaneous Velocity on a Graph

Figure 3.6: In a graph of position versus time, the instantaneous velocity is the slope of the tangent line at a given point.

  • At \(t_0\), the slope is zero, so \(v(t_0) = 0\).
  • At other times like \(t_1, t_2\), the slope is positive or negative, indicating non-zero velocity.

Tip

The zeros of the velocity function (\(v(t)=0\)) correspond to the maximum or minimum points of the position function \(x(t)\). This is where the object momentarily stops and potentially reverses direction.

This graph clearly illustrates the concept. As the time interval \(\Delta t\) gets smaller and smaller, the average velocity (slope of the secant line) approaches the instantaneous velocity (slope of the tangent line). When the position graph reaches a peak or a valley, its slope is momentarily zero, meaning the object has zero instantaneous velocity at that exact moment.

Example: Velocity from a Position-Time Graph

Given the position-versus-time graph, find the velocity-versus-time graph.

Figure 3.7: Object moving away, stopping, and reversing direction.

Strategy:

  • The graph consists of three straight line segments.
  • For straight lines, the average velocity equals the instantaneous velocity.
  • Calculate the slope (\(\Delta x / \Delta t\)) for each segment.

When the position-time graph is made of straight lines, the velocity is constant during each segment. This simplifies finding the instantaneous velocity, as it’s simply the slope of that line segment.

Example: Velocity from a Position-Time Graph (Solution)

Calculations:

  • Interval 1 (0 s to 0.5 s): \(v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.5 \, \text{m} - 0.0 \, \text{m}}{0.5 \, \text{s} - 0.0 \, \text{s}} = 1.0 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • Interval 2 (0.5 s to 1.0 s): \(v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.5 \, \text{m} - 0.5 \, \text{m}}{1.0 \, \text{s} - 0.5 \, \text{s}} = 0.0 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • Interval 3 (1.0 s to 2.0 s): \(v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{0.0 \, \text{m} - 0.5 \, \text{m}}{2.0 \, \text{s} - 1.0 \, \text{s}} = -0.5 \, \text{m/s}\)

Velocity-Time Graph:

Figure 3.8: Velocity versus time.

The calculated velocities for each interval directly translate into the velocity-time graph. A positive slope on the position graph means positive velocity, a zero slope means zero velocity, and a negative slope means negative velocity. This demonstrates the direct relationship between the slope of a position-time graph and velocity.

Speed

In physics, speed and velocity are distinct concepts.

  • Speed is a scalar quantity: it only has magnitude.
  • Velocity is a vector quantity: it has both magnitude and direction.

Average Speed (\(\bar{s}\))

  • Total distance traveled divided by elapsed time. \[ \bar{s} = \frac{\text{Total distance}}{\text{Elapsed time}} \]
  • Not necessarily the magnitude of average velocity. (e.g., if you start and end at the same place, \(\bar{v}=0\) but \(\bar{s} \neq 0\)).

Instantaneous Speed

  • The magnitude of the instantaneous velocity. \[ \text{Instantaneous speed} = |v(t)| \]
  • Always positive or zero.

Think of it this way: your car’s speedometer shows speed. If you’re driving at 60 mph, that’s your speed. Your velocity might be 60 mph North, or 60 mph South. If you drive in a circle and end up where you started, your average velocity is zero (because displacement is zero), but your average speed is definitely not zero because you covered a distance.

Calculating Instantaneous Velocity (using Calculus)

To calculate instantaneous velocity from a position function \(x(t)\), we use differentiation.

  • If \(x(t)\) is a polynomial where each term has the form \(A t^n\):

\[ \frac{d(A t^n)}{dt} = A n t^{n-1} \]

  • If \(x(t)\) has multiple terms added together, differentiate each term and sum them up.

Note

This is the power rule of differentiation, a fundamental tool in calculus for finding rates of change.

This rule is extremely useful in physics. For example, if your position is given by \(x(t) = 5t^2 + 3t - 2\), then your velocity function would be \(v(t) = 10t + 3\). We’ll see how this works in an example.

Example: Instantaneous vs. Average Velocity

The position of a particle is given by \(x(t) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s})t + (0.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)t^3\).

1. Find the instantaneous velocity at \(t = 2.0\) s.

  • First, find the general velocity function by differentiating \(x(t)\):

    \(v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} [(3.0 \, \text{m/s})t + (0.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)t^3]\)

    \(v(t) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s}) + (0.5 \times 3 \, \text{m/s}^3)t^{3-1}\)

    \(v(t) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s}) + (1.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)t^2\)

  • Now, substitute \(t = 2.0\) s into \(v(t)\):

    \(v(2.0 \, \text{s}) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s}) + (1.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)(2.0 \, \text{s})^2\)

    \(v(2.0 \, \text{s}) = 3.0 \, \text{m/s} + (1.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)(4.0 \, \text{s}^2)\)

    \(v(2.0 \, \text{s}) = 3.0 \, \text{m/s} + 6.0 \, \text{m/s} = 9.0 \, \text{m/s}\)

Here, we apply the power rule. For the first term, \(t\) is \(t^1\), so \(A=3.0\), \(n=1\), giving \(3.0 \times 1 \times t^0 = 3.0\). For the second term, \(A=0.5\), \(n=3\), giving \(0.5 \times 3 \times t^2 = 1.5t^2\).

Example: Instantaneous vs. Average Velocity (continued)

The position of a particle is given by \(x(t) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s})t + (0.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)t^3\).

2. Calculate the average velocity between 1.0 s and 3.0 s.

  • First, find the positions at \(t = 1.0\) s and \(t = 3.0\) s:

    \(x(1.0 \, \text{s}) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s})(1.0 \, \text{s}) + (0.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)(1.0 \, \text{s})^3 = 3.0 \, \text{m} + 0.5 \, \text{m} = 3.5 \, \text{m}\)

    \(x(3.0 \, \text{s}) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s})(3.0 \, \text{s}) + (0.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)(3.0 \, \text{s})^3 = 9.0 \, \text{m} + (0.5 \, \text{m/s}^3)(27 \, \text{s}^3) = 9.0 \, \text{m} + 13.5 \, \text{m} = 22.5 \, \text{m}\)

  • Now, calculate average velocity:

    \(\bar{v} = \frac{x(3.0 \, \text{s}) - x(1.0 \, \text{s})}{3.0 \, \text{s} - 1.0 \, \text{s}} = \frac{22.5 \, \text{m} - 3.5 \, \text{m}}{2.0 \, \text{s}} = \frac{19.0 \, \text{m}}{2.0 \, \text{s}} = 9.5 \, \text{m/s}\)

Important

Notice that the instantaneous velocity at \(t=2.0\) s (\(9.0 \, \text{m/s}\)) is close to, but not exactly equal to, the average velocity over the interval from \(1.0\) s to \(3.0\) s (\(9.5 \, \text{m/s}\)). As the interval shrinks around \(t=2.0\) s, the average velocity would approach the instantaneous velocity.

This highlights the difference between instantaneous and average velocity. The average velocity calculation is much simpler, as it doesn’t require calculus, but it only provides a general idea of motion over an interval.

Position, Velocity, and Speed Graphs

Consider \(x(t) = (3.0 \, \text{m/s})t - (3 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2\).

  1. Instantaneous Velocity \(v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} = (3.0 \, \text{m/s}) - (6.0 \, \text{m/s}^2)t\)
    • \(v(0.25 \, \text{s}) = 3.0 - 6.0(0.25) = 1.50 \, \text{m/s}\)
    • \(v(0.50 \, \text{s}) = 3.0 - 6.0(0.50) = 0 \, \text{m/s}\)
    • \(v(1.0 \, \text{s}) = 3.0 - 6.0(1.0) = -3.0 \, \text{m/s}\)
  2. Speed = \(|v(t)|\)
    • Speed at \(0.25 \, \text{s} = |1.50 \, \text{m/s}| = 1.50 \, \text{m/s}\)
    • Speed at \(0.50 \, \text{s} = |0 \, \text{m/s}| = 0 \, \text{m/s}\)
    • Speed at \(1.0 \, \text{s} = |-3.0 \, \text{m/s}| = 3.0 \, \text{m/s}\)

Here, the position function is a parabola opening downwards. This means the object will move in one direction, momentarily stop at the peak of the parabola, and then reverse direction.

Check Your Understanding

The position of an object as a function of time is \(x(t) = (-3 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2\).

  1. What is the velocity of the object as a function of time?
  2. Is the velocity ever positive?
  3. What are the velocity and speed at \(t = 1.0\) s?

Apply the differentiation rule you’ve learned for part (a). For part (b), consider the sign of your velocity function. For part (c), plug in the time value and then take the absolute value for speed.

Check Your Understanding - Solution

The position of an object as a function of time is \(x(t) = (-3 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2\).

(a) What is the velocity of the object as a function of time?

\(v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt} [(-3 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2]\)

\(v(t) = (-3 \, \text{m/s}^2)(2)t^{2-1} = (-6 \, \text{m/s}^2)t\)

(b) Is the velocity ever positive?

No. Since \(t \ge 0\) (time cannot be negative) and the coefficient is negative, \(v(t) = -6t\) will always be zero or negative.

(c) What are the velocity and speed at \(t = 1.0\) s?

  • Velocity:

    \(v(1.0 \, \text{s}) = (-6 \, \text{m/s}^2)(1.0 \, \text{s}) = -6.0 \, \text{m/s}\)

  • Speed:

    \(\text{Speed} = |v(1.0 \, \text{s})| = |-6.0 \, \text{m/s}| = 6.0 \, \text{m/s}\)

This example shows an object that starts at the origin and constantly moves in the negative direction, with increasing speed. Its velocity is always negative (or zero at t=0), but its speed is always positive (or zero at t=0).

Key Takeaways

  • Instantaneous Velocity (\(v(t)\)): The velocity at a specific instant; it’s the derivative of the position function \(x(t)\) with respect to time (\(v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt}\)).
  • Slope of Position-Time Graph: The instantaneous velocity at any point is the slope of the tangent line to the \(x(t)\) graph at that point.
  • Speed vs. Velocity:
    • Velocity is a vector (magnitude and direction).
    • Speed is a scalar (magnitude only).
  • Instantaneous Speed: The magnitude of the instantaneous velocity (\(|v(t)|\)). It is always positive or zero.
  • Average Speed (\(\bar{s}\)): Total distance traveled divided by elapsed time. It is generally not equal to the magnitude of average velocity.

These are the most important distinctions and concepts from this section. Understanding the relationship between position, velocity, and speed, both mathematically and graphically, is key.

Key Equations

Equation Description
\(v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt}\) Instantaneous Velocity
\(\frac{d(At^n)}{dt} = Ant^{n-1}\) Power Rule for Differentiation
\(\bar{s} = \frac{\text{Total distance}}{\text{Elapsed time}}\) Average Speed
Instantaneous speed = \(|v(t)|\) Instantaneous Speed

These equations are your tools for calculating instantaneous velocity and speed. The power rule is particularly useful when position is given as a polynomial function of time.
